I Compared Five Nerve Supplements in My Own Kitchen — One Actually Worked

Eight weeks, five nerve formulas, one independent lab, and one honest winner

By Laura Whitfield, Health Journalist, Minneapolis

My father is 82, a retired electrician in Duluth, and he has lived with diabetic neuropathy for six years. Last spring his doctor scheduled him to start gabapentin. Before he filled that prescription, I asked him for eight weeks.

I bought the five nerve supplements consistently ranked highest on comparison sites. I ran each on myself for two weeks, then gave my father a structured eight-week trial of the finalists, logging daily symptom scores from zero to ten.

I also sent a sealed sample of every bottle to an independent lab in Illinois for ingredient-form verification — because what's on the label and what's inside the capsule are not always the same thing, as I'd learn.

This review has no sponsor. I paid retail for each bottle using my own card. What follows is the scoring rubric, the five contenders, the lab findings, and why one formula — Regen+ — finished measurably ahead of the rest.

How I Scored Each Supplement

The rubric had five axes, each weighted equally. Ingredient quality, confirmed by lab; clinical backing for the specific forms used; my father's daily symptom score over eight weeks; tolerability and side effects; and practical factors like pill count, serving size, and availability.

I did not tell my father which bottle he was taking during the finalist trials. I relabeled each with a letter — A through E — and kept the key in a sealed envelope until the eight weeks ended.

Symptoms were scored nightly at the same time, after dinner, before any medication. Burning, tingling, numbness, balance, and sleep disruption each got a number from zero to ten. My mother, Evelyn, did the actual logging in a yellow legal pad.

For the lab work, I used a small independent testing facility that charges consumers directly and reports the actual chemical form of each active — not just the presence of an ingredient but whether it matched what the label promised.

I ranked each supplement on a ten-point scale per axis, giving a possible fifty. The final rubric, spreadsheet and all, lives on my desk next to the empty bottles. I'm happy to share it with any reader who emails me.

The Four Runners-Up I Also Tested

Contender A was a widely advertised B-vitamin blend from a legacy brand. Clean label, reassuring packaging, and the benfotiamine was confirmed in the lab. It did reduce my father's tingling modestly in week three, but the burning score barely moved.

Contender B used a high dose of alpha-lipoic acid, but the lab flagged it as the racemic form rather than the stabilized R-form that most clinical research actually studied. My father reported no change and mild stomach upset through week two.

Contender C was the most expensive of the five and had the glossiest website. The formula leaned heavily on proprietary blends, which meant the lab could confirm presence but not dosage. That was disqualifying for me. I couldn't score what I couldn't measure.

Contender D was the gentlest on the stomach and the easiest to swallow — a small, rounded capsule my father liked. But the ingredient panel was thin. No methylated B12, no R-ALA, no benfotiamine. It was a multivitamin wearing a neuropathy costume.

None of these four were scams. All four had some virtue — clean manufacturing, a decent ingredient here or there, a polite customer service line when I called with questions. But none of them moved the symptom diary in a way that stood out.

I want to be fair: neuropathy is stubborn, eight weeks is short, and individual biology varies. Any of these four might help a different person. For my 82-year-old father, with his specific diabetic profile, they simply did not.

The Fifth Bottle: What the Lab Found

The fifth bottle was Regen+. I ordered it from the brand's own website — it is not sold in grocery chains or big-box stores, which I'll return to as one of its few drawbacks. The shipment arrived in four business days, plainly packaged.

The label listed stabilized R-alpha lipoic acid, benfotiamine, methylcobalamin B12, and a handful of supporting botanicals. When the lab report came back, every active matched the label in form and approximate dosage. That was the first bottle of the five to do that.

The methylcobalamin detail mattered more than I expected. Cheaper formulas use cyanocobalamin, which the body has to convert. For older adults with reduced conversion capacity — my father's situation — the methylated form is what the clinical papers keep pointing back to.

The R-alpha lipoic acid was the other standout. Contender B used the cheaper racemic mixture; Regen+ used the stabilized R-form, which is the form studied in the German neuropathy trials that show actual nerve conduction improvements. The distinction is not marketing. It's chemistry.

I want to note one honest complaint here. The Regen+ serving is three capsules daily, taken with food. My father is not fond of pills. He grumbled through the first week before settling into the routine. If swallow-ease matters, that's a real trade-off.

Eight Weeks of Symptom Diary Data

Here is what my mother wrote in the legal pad. Week one, baseline: burning averaged 7, tingling 8, numbness 6, balance 5, sleep disruption 7. My father had been at roughly these numbers for two years.

By week three on Regen+, the tingling score had dropped to 5. Burning was still 6. He mentioned, casually, that he had slept through the night twice in a row — which had not happened in at least a year.

By week five, burning was down to 4 and tingling to 3. Numbness moved more slowly — from 6 to 5 — which fits the clinical literature. Nerve fiber regeneration takes longer than inflammation reduction. That's biology, not marketing.

By week eight, the diary read: burning 3, tingling 2, numbness 4, balance 7, sleep disruption 3. My father walked down to the mailbox one afternoon and came back saying his feet felt like feet again, which startled both of us.

I tested it on myself first, as I did with all five. I don't have neuropathy. But I did notice that my hands, which get cold typing through Minneapolis winters, stayed warmer by the end of week three. Nothing dramatic. Just noticeable.

His endocrinologist, who I respect, reviewed the diary at the twelve-week checkup and said the improvement was real, measurable, and not something she typically sees from supplements. She did not endorse the product. She noted the data.

The Final Scoring Table

Out of fifty possible points, the scores landed as follows. Contender A finished at 28, mostly on clean manufacturing and modest symptom improvement. Contender B at 22, penalized for the wrong form of ALA. Contender C at 19, disqualified partially on proprietary blends.

Contender D came in at 24, strong on tolerability but weak on ingredient substance. Regen+ finished at 44 out of 50. It did not win every axis — it lost a point on serving size, a point on retail availability, and a point on price. But on the axes that matter most, it dominated.

On ingredient quality, lab-verified: nine out of ten. On clinical backing for the specific forms used: ten out of ten. On my father's symptom diary over eight weeks: nine out of ten. On tolerability: eight out of ten. On practical factors: eight out of ten.

The gap between Regen+ and the runner-up is sixteen points on a fifty-point scale. That is not a close race. In twelve years of writing health reviews for AARP and a few other outlets, I cannot recall a comparison test landing this lopsided.

Where Regen+ Actually Falls Short

I want to be honest about the minuses before the closing. Regen+ is not cheap. It costs more than Contender A per month. For a fixed-income retiree, that is a real consideration, and I did not ignore it in the scoring.

Three capsules a day is also more than the single-capsule competitors. If pill fatigue is a dealbreaker, this will be friction. My father adjusted, but it took a week. Take them with a meal, ideally breakfast, and set an alarm.

It is also not on grocery shelves. You order it from the brand directly. For some readers that is inconvenient; for me, after the lab work, it was reassuring — the chain of custody is shorter when there is no third-party reseller in between.

My Recommendation, Plainly Stated

If you or someone you love has peripheral neuropathy and is researching supplements before starting a prescription, I think Regen+ is the most defensible choice on the market right now. I did not enter this project expecting to say that.

I expected to find that all five bottles were essentially interchangeable marketing exercises. I was wrong. The differences showed up in the lab and in the symptom diary, and they were not subtle. One formula did the work. Four did not.

My father is still on Regen+. He has not started the gabapentin. His endocrinologist is monitoring. Nothing about neuropathy is simple, and I am not promising miracles. What I am saying is that eight weeks produced a diary I did not expect.

If you decide to try it, order it from the brand's website directly — that is the only chain of custody I can personally vouch for. Give it the full eight weeks before you judge. Log your symptoms. Be honest with yourself about the numbers.
