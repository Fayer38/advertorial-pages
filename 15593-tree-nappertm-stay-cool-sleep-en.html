The Real Problem

For decades, the conventional wisdom positioned melatonin as a benign sleep nudge, a soft alternative to prescription sedatives, and largely free of consequence.

That framing has unraveled quickly. Sleep specialists are now publishing on the precise issue Dana's physician identified: the body adapts, and adapts badly.

When exogenous melatonin floods the bloodstream nightly, the pineal gland receives the signal to slow its own output, sometimes for months after stopping.
