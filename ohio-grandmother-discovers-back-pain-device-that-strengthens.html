Nicky turned four last September. He had a dinosaur cake, a bounce house, and a backyard full of kids screaming with joy. When he saw me standing by the patio door, he dropped his juice box and sprinted toward me with both arms wide open.

I wanted to scoop him up more than anything. But my lower back seized the moment I bent forward. I froze. My daughter saw my face and quietly stepped in, lifting him before he reached me. He never noticed. But I did.

That was the moment I understood what three years of degenerative disc disease had actually taken from me. Not just comfort. Not just mobility. It had taken away my ability to be the grandmother Nicky deserved.

I am Linda Moretti. I am sixty-three years old. I taught fourth grade in Columbus, Ohio for thirty-one years. And for the last three years, chronic lower back pain has quietly dismantled my life piece by piece.

The Slow Unraveling Nobody Warns You About

It did not start dramatically. A stiffness getting out of bed. A twinge reaching for a mixing bowl. I told myself it was normal aging and kept moving. But the pain kept growing, and the things I could do kept shrinking.

My raised-bed garden went first. I had grown tomatoes, peppers, and herbs every summer for twenty years. One spring I knelt down to plant seedlings and could not stand back up without grabbing the fence. The garden went to weeds that year. It has stayed that way.

Then Sunday dinners disappeared. For decades I hosted my family every week. Lasagna, garlic bread, the whole production. But standing at the stove for an hour became unbearable. I tried sitting on a stool, but reaching and stirring still sent bolts of pain down my legs.

I stopped volunteering at my old school. Stopped walking with my neighbor Diane in the mornings. Stopped bending down to tie my own shoes without wincing. Every week the circle got a little smaller.

The worst part was the loneliness. When you cancel enough plans, people stop inviting you. Nobody means to leave you behind. They just get used to you not being there.

The Expensive Cycle of Temporary Fixes

I did everything a responsible person does. I went to my doctor. He referred me to a chiropractor. The chiropractor cracked and adjusted me twice a week for months. Each visit brought a day, maybe two, of relief before the tightness and aching crept right back.

Then came physical therapy. Three sessions a week, forty-five minutes each, plus the drive across town. The exercises helped while I was there, but the relief never lasted past the parking lot. My co-pays were adding up faster than my progress.

Over two years I spent thousands on appointments, adjustments, and therapy sessions. I bought two different TENS units after seeing them advertised online. They buzzed and tingled and did absolutely nothing lasting. Expensive distractions, both of them.

I tried every cream, patch, and heating pad on the pharmacy shelf. I slept with pillows between my knees, under my knees, behind my back. I watched YouTube stretching videos at midnight. Nothing held.

My orthopedist finally sat me down and said we should start discussing surgical options. The word surgery landed in my stomach like a stone. I was sixty-two years old, living alone. Who would take care of me during recovery? What if it did not work?

I left that appointment feeling like I had two choices: live with the pain forever, or gamble on a surgery that terrified me. Neither felt like an answer.
