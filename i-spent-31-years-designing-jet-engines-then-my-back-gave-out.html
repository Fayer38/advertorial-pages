Last October, I did something I'd done a thousand times before. I walked into my garage, grabbed my 23-pound gas blower, yanked the starter cord — and heard a crack that wasn't the engine.

It was my L4-L5 disc.

I was flat on my garage floor for what felt like an eternity. My wife found me twenty minutes later, unable to stand. Three days in bed. Two weeks of physical therapy. And one thought that wouldn't leave me alone:

There has to be a better way.

My name is Albert Renaud. I'm 64 years old. I spent 31 years at Boeing designing turbine compression systems for the 787 Dreamliner. And lying on that garage floor, staring at the ceiling, I decided to use everything I'd learned about moving air — to solve a problem that millions of homeowners silently suffer through every single weekend.

The Silent Epidemic Nobody Talks About

Here's something most people don't realize: yard work is one of the leading causes of back injury in adults over 50.

Every autumn, emergency rooms across America see a predictable spike in lower back injuries. The culprit isn't heavy lifting or car accidents. It's leaf blowers, rakes, and the repetitive bending and twisting that comes with keeping your property clean.

Warning: According to orthopedic specialists, repetitive use of heavy vibrating tools — especially gas-powered blowers weighing 15-25 lbs — dramatically increases the risk of herniated discs, chronic lower back strain, and shoulder injuries in adults over 50. The damage is cumulative and often irreversible.

I knew those statistics. I'd lived them. But what surprised me was talking to my neighbors, my friends, my physical therapist's other patients. Almost every single one of them had a version of my story.

Tom, three houses down, had stopped doing yard work entirely. His wife hired a service — $200 a month. Linda across the street switched to a rake and now takes Advil every Saturday night. My buddy Phil sold his house and moved to a condo because — his exact words — "I just can't keep up with the yard anymore."

These aren't lazy people. These are proud homeowners who love their properties. They just can't physically do it anymore without paying the price in pain.

I Tried Every "Solution" on the Market — None of Them Worked

During my recovery, I became obsessed with finding an alternative. I ordered five different cordless blowers. I tested lightweight rakes, ergonomic brooms, even one of those wheeled leaf vacuums that look like a baby stroller.

Every single one fell into the same trap.

The lightweight ones? Useless. They couldn't move wet leaves off concrete. You'd stand there waving the thing around like you were blow-drying your driveway with a hair dryer. Embarrassing and ineffective.

The powerful ones? Still heavy. The "lightweight" cordless models on the market weighed 8 to 12 pounds. That might not sound like much — until you're holding one at arm's length for 20 minutes. For anyone with a bad shoulder, arthritis, or a disc problem like mine, it's torture.

And the gas-powered monsters? They deliver power, sure. But they also deliver vibration, exhaust fumes, pull-cord strain, and 20+ pounds of dead weight hanging off your body.

One evening, lying on my couch with an ice pack on my back after testing yet another "ergonomic" blower, my wife said something that stuck with me:

"Albert, you literally designed systems that move air at 500 miles per hour through a jet engine. Why can't you make something that blows leaves without destroying your spine?"

She was right. And that question changed everything.

What 31 Years of Aerospace Engineering Taught Me About Moving Air

The next morning, I walked into my workshop — the same one where I'd spent three decades solving Boeing's most complex airflow problems — and asked myself a question no blower manufacturer had ever seriously considered:

"What if you could compress professional-grade airflow into something that weighs less than a bag of sugar?"

In aerospace, we do this every day. A jet engine compresses massive volumes of air through impossibly small spaces using advanced turbine geometry. The principle is elegant: it's not about brute force. It's about intelligent airflow design.

Traditional leaf blowers use brushed motors — technology from the 1970s. They're heavy, inefficient, and generate enormous vibration. They compensate for poor design with sheer size and weight.

I took a completely different approach.

I sourced a brushless motor — the same technology used in professional drones and Tesla's electric vehicles. Brushless motors have no friction parts. They spin faster, last longer, run cooler, and weigh a fraction of traditional motors.

Then I applied turbine compression principles from my Boeing work. Instead of pushing air harder (which requires a bigger, heavier motor), I designed a nozzle system that accelerates air more efficiently through optimized geometry.

Eight months. Forty-seven prototypes. Countless evenings in the workshop.

The result: a handheld blower that weighs 2.1 pounds and delivers 200 MPH airspeed with 1,200 CFM of volume. Those are numbers that rival — and in some cases exceed — gas blowers five times its weight.

I called it the Seese Pro.

How Does Something This Small Deliver This Much Power?

I get this question constantly. And honestly, I understand the skepticism. When I first showed the prototype to my neighbor Tom, he laughed. "That little thing? No way."

Then I pointed it at the pile of wet oak leaves that had been stuck to his patio for a week.

They were gone in seconds. Tom stopped laughing.

Here's the simple explanation: traditional blowers waste enormous energy on friction, heat, and vibration. The Seese Pro's brushless motor converts nearly all its energy into airflow. It spins at 30,000 RPM with almost zero wasted motion.

Combined with the aerospace-inspired nozzle design, you get concentrated, high-velocity air — not just volume. That's the difference between a garden hose and a pressure washer. Same water, completely different result.
