IT DIDN'T WIN ON RAW PSI — IT WON ON THE METRIC THAT ACTUALLY MATTERED

A subscriber emailed me last month asking which cordless pressure washer was actually worth buying. So I did what any reasonable person with too much garage space would do — I ordered six units on my own dime.

On the bench were DeWalt's DCPW550B, EGO's HPW3200, the Greenworks GPW1800HYB, a Mrliance unit, the corded Ryobi as my benchmark, and the Seese Pro Anywhere Washer my brother-in-law had been texting me about for weeks.

I timed setup, weighed each unit with battery, ran each through the same dirty driveway panel and the same muddy mountain bike, and tracked the all-in cost including the batteries DeWalt and EGO sell separately. The result wasn't what I expected.

How I Scored These Six Units

🖼️

"Stopwatch and notepad on a workbench beside several pressure washers with batter"

I gave each unit four scores from one to ten: setup time from cold to first spray, real cleaning power on a baseline 3x3 driveway panel I'd let stew for two weeks, portability with a fully loaded battery, and total cost of ownership including batteries.

The corded Ryobi was my control. It pushes around 1900 PSI through a fixed water line and exists in this test only to remind us what real driveway cleaning power feels like before we go cordless.

The DeWalt DCPW550B impressed me on raw output. It's a 550 PSI tool-only unit built like the rest of the FlexVolt lineup — solid, ergonomic, and obviously engineered by people who've held a pressure washer before. The catch is the battery you'll need to add separately.

THE ANYWHERE WASHER DIDN'T WIN ON PSI — IT WON ON SETUP

EGO's HPW3200 was the heaviest hitter cordless on paper at 3200 PSI from a 56V platform. It cleaned aggressively and felt closest to the corded Ryobi in pure stripping power. It's also the bulkiest and the most expensive once you factor in the second battery.

The Greenworks GPW1800HYB hybrid sat in the middle on output and price. The Mrliance felt like what you'd expect from an unfamiliar imported brand — capable but uninspiring. And the Seese Pro Anywhere Washer broke the entire test framework, which I'll explain shortly.

Where The Seese Pro Broke My Test

🖼️

"Cordless pressure washer with intake hose dropped into a five-gallon bucket of w"

Here's the thing nobody told me before this test: setup is the metric most reviewers ignore, and the one homeowners actually feel. The DeWalt and EGO took eight to twelve minutes from box to first spray once you account for hose, tap, and battery.

The Seese Pro Anywhere Washer was spraying in under two minutes. No hose. No tap. No infrastructure. I dropped the intake into a five-gallon bucket of water from my rain barrel, hit the trigger, and started cleaning my mountain bike on the patio.

That's the entire pitch in one paragraph. It works from any water source — bucket, pool, barrel, even a kitchen sink full of warm water if you want. For the eighty percent of weekend cleaning that doesn't need 3000 PSI, this changes the math entirely.

Where The Seese Pro Is Not The Right Tool

I want to be honest because that's the only reason anyone watches my channel. The Seese Pro is not going to strip paint off a deck. It is not going to replace a gas unit if you're cleaning a stained concrete patio after a decade.

On raw cleaning power, the EGO and DeWalt beat it cleanly. If your weekend project is a heavily oxidized siding job or a fence that's gone gray, you should be looking at one of those, or the corded Ryobi if you have a tap nearby.

What The All-In Cost Actually Looks Like

Here's where most reviews lie by omission. The DeWalt DCPW550B is sold tool-only. The EGO HPW3200 is sold tool-only. By the time you add a 5Ah battery and charger to either, the real out-the-door cost can climb significantly above what the headline sticker suggests.

The Greenworks ships ready to run, which is fair. The Mrliance is the cheapest in the test on paper, but durability is the question I'd want answered after eighteen months. The Seese Pro is sold direct-to-consumer with everything included in the box.

When I lined up final receipts, the Seese Pro came in below the EGO and DeWalt by a meaningful margin without needing a single accessory purchase. That changed how I'd answer the question my subscriber actually asked: which one would I tell my brother-in-law to buy.

Real-World Tests On The Same Five Jobs

I ran every unit through the same five tasks: a two-week-old driveway oil stain panel, a muddy mountain bike post-trail, a cedar deck section, the side of my Honda Pilot, and a stack of plastic patio chairs that hadn't been touched since last summer.

On the driveway oil stain, the EGO and Ryobi were the clear leaders. The Anywhere Washer cleaned it eventually but took longer. On the bike, the deck, the SUV, and the patio chairs, every cordless unit handled the job and you'd struggle to spot a difference.

What separated the field was friction. Setup, takedown, storage, where you could use it. The Anywhere Washer was the only unit I could grab off a shelf, fill a bucket, and use without unwinding fifty feet of hose or running an extension cord. That's not nothing.

What My Audience Actually Scored Highest

When I ran the poll on my channel asking what mattered most when buying a pressure washer, raw PSI was not number one. Setup time and portability were. That tells you something about how my audience uses these tools versus how they get reviewed elsewhere.

Most of us aren't pressure washing the side of a barn. We're cleaning bikes, cars, kayaks, decks, garage floors, garbage cans, and the kids' soccer cleats. We do it on a Saturday morning between coffee and lunch, and we want to be done.

  • DeWalt DCPW550B Strong build, real cleaning power, 550 PSI cordless. Tool-only purchase means a battery and charger add real cost before your first use.
  • EGO HPW3200 Highest cordless output of the test at 3200 PSI. Heavy, bulky, and the most expensive once you factor in the second 56V battery and charger.
  • Seese Pro Anywhere Lower output than the EGO and DeWalt but spraying in under two minutes from any bucket. Wins on setup, portability, and total ownership cost.

For that user — which is me, and probably you if you're still reading — the Seese Pro Anywhere Washer is the unit that fits the actual job. Not the spec sheet job. The Saturday morning between coffee and lunch job.

If you need to strip a deck or clean a thousand square feet of stained concrete, buy the EGO. If you need a tool you'll actually grab off the shelf to clean the things you own week to week, this is the one I'd recommend.

Questions I Keep Getting From Subscribers

Will the Seese Pro Anywhere Washer actually clean a moderately dirty driveway, or is that a job for the corded Ryobi?

Yes for the kind of dirt a normal driveway sees in a season. For deep oil stains, you want the EGO or a corded gas unit.

How long does the battery actually last on a single charge in real-world cleaning use?

I averaged about thirty to forty minutes of trigger time per charge across my tests, which is enough to clean two cars or a patio set.

Can I really run it from a bucket, or does that only work for two minutes before something fails?

I ran one full battery from a five-gallon bucket and refilled twice. The intake handles bucket use as the primary water source, not a workaround.
