If you've ever raised a pair of binoculars to a distant ridge and watched the image swim, fog, or simply fall apart, you already know the dirty secret of the optics aisle at every American sporting-goods store: the gap between drugstore plastic and real glass is enormous, and the markup is worse.

Birders, hunters, and porch-side wildlife watchers over fifty are caught in the middle. Cheap pairs go in the trash within a season. The premium brands sit behind locked glass cases at prices that read like a small mortgage payment, defended by retail chains that take a bite out of every sale.

Then there's Frank Holzmann. Sixty-five years old, forty-one years grinding lenses inside two of Bavaria's most respected optics houses, now living in a quiet street in Asheville, North Carolina. He retired furious. And he is shipping the binoculars he always wanted to build directly from his garage.

Forty-One Years Inside The Markup Machine

Frank started in a Bavarian workshop in 1984, polishing roof prisms by hand for a brand that still puts a four-figure sticker on its flagship pair. By the time he retired in 2024, he could quote you the exact bill of materials on any luxury binocular sold in a Cabela's display case.

Anyone who has stood in front of that display case knows the feeling: a spotting scope on one shelf priced like a used car, a plastic toy on the other priced like a tank of gas, and absolutely nothing in between that a serious birder can trust without flinching at the receipt.

The breaking point came at a trade show in Las Vegas. A US distributor walked Frank past a wall of his own employer's binoculars, marked up nearly four hundred percent over the factory cost he had personally signed off on the week before.

Same glass. Same prisms. Without the four-figure luxury tax.

He walked out of that hall, flew home to Munich, and filed his retirement papers the next Monday. He told his wife he was going to spend his pension on one last production run, his way, and ship it himself. She told him to stop sulking and get to work.

What he built next is the reason this article exists, and the reason a lot of the big sporting-goods chains are quietly furious about a quiet garage in North Carolina.

What The Optics Industry Won't Tell You

Here's what nobody on the retail side will say out loud. The cost difference between a great pair of binoculars and an average one is not the glass. It's the prism alignment, the lens coatings, and whether the housing is sealed against fog — three steps a factory either does properly or skips.

Premium pairs use BAK4 prisms hand-aligned to a fraction of a millimeter, fully multi-coated lenses that pull every available photon through the tube, and a nitrogen-purged sealed body that refuses to fog when you walk from a heated truck into a cold dawn.

The luxury brands charge a four-figure premium for that work. The bargain brands skip it entirely and hope you don't notice until the second hunting season. Almost no one in the middle does it right and prices it honestly — because the retail chain takes its cut either way.

One Final Production Run

Frank cashed in his pension, took a second mortgage on the Asheville house, and commissioned 1,800 units of the binocular he'd wanted to build for two decades — the Clariva 12x42, with the same HD optics, hand-aligned prisms, and sealed housing as the four-figure pairs he shipped out of Bavaria.

Inside the housing: fully multi-coated HD optics, hand-aligned BAK4 prisms, a magnesium-alloy chassis, nitrogen-purged sealing against rain and fog, twist-up eyecups that hold their position, and a focus wheel with the kind of damped resistance that only comes from properly machined parts.
