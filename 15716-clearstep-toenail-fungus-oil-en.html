A confession from a 71-year-old former Ohio podiatrist about the toenail fungus treatments he wishes he had known about decades ago.

I told him exactly what I had told thousands of patients before him. A 90-day course of Lamisil, periodic liver enzyme panels, and we should see clearance by month four or five.

I had nothing. I had a closet full of antifungal creams from the local pharmacy that I had watched fail in my own clinic for decades. I had tea tree oil. I had Vicks. I had shrugs.

That night I sat in my den in Lima, Ohio, and started reading. Not patient charts. Real biochemistry. Keratin density studies. Carrier oil research from cosmetic chemistry journals. Things I should have read in 1995.

What I found over the next eighteen months changed how I look at every drugstore antifungal cream in America. It also changed what I now keep in my own medicine cabinet, and what I gave Frank.

I am writing this because nobody told me, nobody told my patients, and the people most likely to be hiding their feet at the pool right now deserve to know what I learned too late in my career.

Why Every Cream You Tried Sat On Top

Your toenail is not skin. That sounds obvious until you understand what it actually means for treatment. A toenail is a dense plate of compressed keratin, roughly one hundred times thicker than the skin on your forearm.

When you smear an antifungal cream on top of an infected nail, the active ingredient sits there. It does not soak in. It cannot. The keratin plate is engineered by biology to be a barrier against exactly this kind of penetration.

Meanwhile, the actual fungal infection lives underneath the nail plate, on the nail bed itself. The cream is on the windshield. The bug is inside the car. They never meet.

THE WINDSHIELD PROBLEM

Drugstore antifungal creams are formulated for skin, not for keratin. Spreading them on a thickened toenail is like spraying glass cleaner on a windshield to kill the fly inside the car. The product never reaches the problem.

I prescribed creams like Lotrimin and recommended Fungi-Nail to patients for years. I knew, deep down, the clearance rates were dismal. Most studies put topical clearance at under ten percent for moderate to severe cases.

But I kept recommending them because the alternative was to send patients to the oral terbinafine route, and many were either not candidates or, like Frank, flat out refused after they read the package insert.

  • OTC creams cannot cross the dense keratin plate
  • Tea tree oil evaporates before reaching the nail bed
  • Vicks VapoRub coats the surface but leaves fungus untouched
  • Soaks soften the nail temporarily, with no antifungal effect

What was missing from every product on the drugstore shelf was a delivery system. A carrier that could actually walk an antifungal compound through the keratin and onto the nail bed where the infection lives.

That is the gap my eighteen months of late-night reading pointed to. Not a stronger antifungal. Not a more aggressive ingredient. A smarter carrier oil designed for keratin penetration.

Once I understood that, the failure of every cream on every CVS and Walgreens shelf made sense. They were not the wrong drug. They were in the wrong vehicle. And nobody at the pharmacy counter ever told us that.

The Lamisil Conversation Nobody Wants To Have

Let me say something I should have said louder during my career. Oral terbinafine, sold as Lamisil, works. The clearance rates in studies hover around seventy percent. That is real. I am not anti-medication.

But the package insert exists for a reason. The FDA requires liver enzyme monitoring because terbinafine has been associated with hepatotoxicity, including rare cases of acute liver failure requiring transplant.

For most patients, the risk is small. For patients over sixty taking statins, blood pressure medication, blood thinners, or anything else that already passes through the liver, the calculation gets harder. Much harder.

I had patients in their late sixties on three or four daily medications who came in asking for the pill. I had to send them for a baseline ALT and AST panel before I could even write the prescription.

Then they had to come back at week six for another draw. Then again if anything looked off. The cost, the bloodwork, the time, the anxiety, all for a fungal infection on a toe.

Many quietly stopped halfway through. They felt fine. The toenail looked a little better. They figured the rest would clear on its own. It rarely did.

Others, like Frank, read the warning section and refused entirely. I respected that. After three decades, I had come to respect that more and more, even as the prescribing system pushed me the other way.

What we needed, what my patients always needed, was a third option. Something that did not require a blood draw. Something that did not coat the windshield. Something a seventy-year-old could use safely while taking five other pills.

What The Research Actually Says

I am not going to pretend ClearStep itself has been through a giant randomized controlled trial. It has not. Most over-the-counter topicals have not. But the underlying science behind carrier oils and topical antifungal delivery has been studied for years.

Study 01
Keratin Penetration Of Plant-Derived Carrier Oils

A 2018 study in the Journal of Cosmetic Science measured the ability of various plant-derived oils to penetrate the keratin nail plate over a 24-hour exposure window. Medium-chain fatty acid carriers consistently outperformed water-based and alcohol-based vehicles.

The penetration depth was significant enough that the carrier reached the inner nail bed in the majority of test samples. By contrast, standard cream vehicles barely passed the outer one-third of the keratin plate during the same window.

The implication is direct. The vehicle determines whether your antifungal ever reaches the infection. Without the right carrier, even the most potent active compound is doing nothing useful.

Journal of Cosmetic Science, 2018, vol. 69, pp. 215-228

Study 02
Topical Antifungal Clearance Rates Across Vehicle Types

A 2020 review published in Mycoses analyzed clearance rates for topical antifungal products across cream, lacquer, and oil-based formulations in the treatment of onychomycosis. Oil-based formulations showed materially higher clearance rates than standard creams.

The difference was attributed almost entirely to the delivery vehicle. The active compounds were similar across products. What changed was whether the active actually arrived at the nail bed in therapeutic concentration.

This is exactly the gap I had spent twenty-eight years prescribing into without realizing it. The cream worked in a petri dish. It just never made it through the keratin plate to the actual fungus.

Mycoses, 2020, vol. 63, pp. 1015-1028

Study 03
Hepatotoxicity Risk With Oral Terbinafine In Older Patients

A 2019 cohort study in the British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology followed older patients on oral terbinafine and measured the incidence of liver enzyme elevation requiring discontinuation. The rate was meaningfully higher than younger cohorts.

For patients over sixty already on statins or other hepatically metabolized drugs, the rate of clinically significant ALT elevation climbed further. This is the safety signal that drove the FDA monitoring requirements in the first place.

It is also why a sixty-eight-year-old like my brother-in-law Frank, on three other daily medications, made an entirely rational decision when he refused the prescription. He was not being stubborn. He was being careful.

British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology, 2019, vol. 85, pp. 2189-2197

Study 04
Patient Adherence In Long-Term Topical Treatment

A 2021 paper in the International Journal of Dermatology examined patient adherence in long-term topical antifungal therapy. The single biggest predictor of clearance was not the active ingredient. It was consistent use beyond the twelve-week mark.

Most patients quit early because they did not see fast results. The patients who continued past the visible-clearance threshold, typically four to six months, had dramatically better outcomes than those who stopped at the first sign of improvement.

This matters because no topical antifungal, including ClearStep, is going to clear a moderate or severe infection in two weeks. The biology of nail growth simply does not allow it. Persistence is the unsexy variable that separates success from failure.

International Journal of Dermatology, 2021, vol. 60, pp. 411-419
