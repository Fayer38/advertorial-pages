The Real Root Cause

Most people assume nerve pain is simply nerves wearing out with age, the way a worn cable might fray. The literature suggests something more specific.

Peripheral nerves are wrapped in a fatty insulating sheath called myelin, and when that sheath thins or degrades, signals misfire as burning, tingling, or numbness.

Researchers I interviewed simply call it myelin erosion, the slow stripping of the protective coating that keeps nerve signals clean.

Myelin erosion: the quiet driver behind most nerve discomfort.

Once you understand it that way, a lot of small mysteries click into place: why symptoms flare at night, why they migrate, why creams barely scratch the surface.

And unfortunately, the news only gets worse the longer this condition is quietly left untreated.

Myelin erosion compounds quietly, year over year, accelerated by elevated blood sugar, certain medications, B-vitamin shortfalls, and the sheer mileage of a long life.

Left alone, what begins as evening tingling tends to become daytime numbness, then unsteady balance, then the very real fear of a fall on the basement stairs.
