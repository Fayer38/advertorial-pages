In October 2019, a delivery truck ran a red light on SE Division Street in Portland, Oregon — and drove straight through the driver-side door of my Honda Accord.

I don't remember the impact. I remember the paramedics cutting me out of the wreckage. I remember my daughter Lily's shoes sitting on the passenger seat, untouched, while the rest of the car looked like a crushed soda can.

Three surgeries. $287,000 in medical bills. And a body that would never move the same way again.

My name is Marcus Chen. I'm 47 now. I used to be a physical therapist with my own clinic — the guy who fixed other people's pain for a living. After that accident, I couldn't even walk my 6-year-old to school without stopping twice to catch my breath.

This isn't a sob story. It's the story of how the worst chapter of my life led me to a device that changed everything — not just for me, but for thousands of homeowners who were silently suffering through the same kind of pain every single weekend.

The $287,000 Lesson Nobody Warns You About

After the accident, I lost everything in the order you'd expect — and then some.

I sold my physical therapy clinic first. Then my house on Hawthorne. Then my late mother's jade bracelet — the one she wore every day of her life — because the hospital billing department doesn't accept sentiment as payment.

Lily and I moved into a one-bedroom apartment off 82nd Avenue. I was 41 years old, walking with a cane, and wondering if I'd ever feel like myself again.

But here's what nobody tells you about recovering from a catastrophic injury: the surgeries fix the big stuff. They don't fix your life.

I could walk again — technically. But every chore became a negotiation with pain. Taking out the trash. Carrying groceries. And the thing that broke me most? Yard work.

Warning: Over 47,000 Americans are treated for back injuries caused by yard equipment every year. Heavy gas blowers — averaging 15-23 lbs — are one of the most common culprits, especially for adults over 40 or anyone with a prior spinal injury.

Our apartment had a small patio and a shared driveway. Nothing fancy. But leaves pile up in Portland like it's their religion. And every time I dragged out the landlord's old gas blower — a 19-pound beast that shook like a jackhammer — I'd spend the next two days in bed, popping the painkillers I could barely afford.

Lily started raking the leaves herself. She was seven.

That image — my little girl doing my job because her dad was too broken — haunted me more than any medical bill ever could.

I Tried Every Shortcut. None of Them Worked.

As a physical therapist, I knew pain. I knew biomechanics. I knew that the answer wasn't "just push through it." So I tried to be smart about it.

First, I bought a corded electric blower. Lighter than gas — but the cord tangled on everything, and I still had to bend and twist in ways my spine couldn't handle.

Then I tried a cheap cordless blower from the hardware store. 30 bucks. It moved air about as effectively as blowing through a straw. Useless on wet leaves. Dead battery in 12 minutes.

I tried a leaf vacuum. A push broom. A neighbor's kid who lasted exactly one weekend before ghosting me.

Every solution was either too heavy, too weak, too expensive, or too unreliable.

And every failed attempt reminded me of the same ugly truth: I was a man who couldn't take care of his own home.

That's the part nobody talks about. It's not just the pain. It's the identity you lose. I spent my career helping people move better, live better. Now I couldn't clear leaves off a 400-square-foot patio without paying for it in pain for days.
