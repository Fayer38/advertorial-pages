Dear Friend,

73% of women over 30 experience chronic water retention — and most of them have no idea that the "solutions" they're using are actually making it worse.

I know, because I was one of them. And I spent the better part of six years swollen, frustrated, and quietly ashamed before I figured out what was really going on inside my body.

My name is Claire Elliston, and this letter is the one I wish someone had written to me back when I was standing in my bathroom at 6 a.m., pressing my thumbs into my puffy ankles and wondering what was wrong with me.

It Started With My Face

I'm a naturopathic formulator. I've spent fourteen years studying how the body processes fluids, minerals, and inflammation. I've helped hundreds of women with hormonal balance, gut health, and metabolic issues. So you'd think I'd have noticed what was happening to my own body sooner. But the truth is, water retention is sneaky. It doesn't announce itself like a rash or a fever. It just slowly, quietly takes over — a little puffiness under the eyes one morning, rings that feel tighter by afternoon, jeans that fit on Monday but not on Thursday. I kept telling myself it was normal. Stress. Hormones. Not enough sleep. I drank more water. I cut my sodium intake to almost nothing. I elevated my legs at night. I bought compression socks. I even tried prescription diuretics for three months — and yes, they worked. But "worked" is a generous word for what those pills did to me. I was running to the bathroom every twenty minutes. My potassium levels crashed. I got muscle cramps so bad they woke me up at 2 a.m. I felt hollow, dried out, like someone had wrung me out like a sponge. My doctor told me that was just "the tradeoff." I refused to accept that.

The morning everything changed, I was getting ready for my sister's wedding. I'd bought a beautiful fitted dress — champagne colored, simple, elegant. But when I looked in the mirror that morning, I barely recognized myself. My face was puffy. My fingers were so swollen I couldn't get my own wedding ring on. My ankles looked like they belonged to someone twenty years older. I stood there in that bathroom, holding my ring, and I cried. Not because of vanity. Because I was a trained professional who helped other people feel better in their bodies, and I couldn't help myself. That was October 17th, 2019. And it was the last day I accepted water retention as something I just had to "live with."

What I Discovered in My Own Lab

Here's what most people — and honestly, most doctors — get wrong about water retention. They treat it like a plumbing problem. Too much water? Flush it out. That's what diuretics do. But your body isn't a bathtub with a clogged drain. It's an incredibly complex system where fluid balance depends on three things working together: your lymphatic system, your electrolyte ratios, and your inflammatory response. When any one of those is off, your body holds water in the wrong places — your face, your hands, your belly, your ankles. And when you force-flush with harsh diuretics, you don't fix the underlying imbalance. You just strip your body of the minerals it needs to regulate fluid properly. Which means the bloating comes back worse than before. I spent eleven months in my lab, reviewing every published study I could find on gentle, evidence-based approaches to fluid balance. I consulted with Dr. Ramona Velez, a nephrologist I'd worked with for years, and Dr. Alan Tsai, an integrative medicine physician specializing in lymphatic health. Together, we identified four botanical compounds that, when combined at specific ratios, address all three root causes simultaneously.

The first is Dandelion Root Extract — not the weak powder you find in most supplements, but a standardized extract at 500mg. A 2009 study published in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine confirmed that dandelion extract significantly increased urinary frequency and volume within five hours of ingestion — but unlike pharmaceutical diuretics, it's naturally rich in potassium, so it replaces what it flushes. The second is Horse Chestnut Seed Extract (Aescin) at 300mg — clinically shown in a Lancet-reviewed meta-analysis to reduce leg swelling by strengthening capillary walls and improving lymphatic drainage. The third is Potassium Citrate at 200mg, which directly supports the electrolyte balance that harsh diuretics destroy. And the fourth is Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine) at 50mg — a 1987 study in the Journal of the Royal College of General Practitioners found that B6 supplementation significantly reduced water retention symptoms, particularly premenstrual bloating and facial puffiness. These four ingredients, at these specific dosages, became the formula I named PureLift.
