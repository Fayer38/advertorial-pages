What came out of that garage changed Ray's life. Then it changed 14,000 other lives. And now, because of something that happened to me on Highway 84 last November, it might change yours too — at a price I never planned to offer.

The Problem Nobody Talks About After 50

Here's what I learned when I started researching: Ray wasn't alone. Not even close.

Millions of homeowners over 50 are quietly hurting themselves every weekend doing basic yard work. The tools haven't changed in decades — gas blowers that weigh 15 to 23 pounds, rakes that torque your spine with every stroke, push brooms that turn a simple patio cleanup into a 25-minute ordeal.

Warning: According to the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission, yard tools send over 47,000 Americans to the emergency room each year with back, shoulder, and wrist injuries — and adults over 55 account for the fastest-growing segment of those injuries.

I saw it with Ray. I saw it with my neighbors. And honestly? I was starting to feel it myself.

That dull ache in the lower back after 20 minutes of sweeping the driveway. The stiff shoulders the morning after clearing the patio. The slow, creeping realization that your body can't do what it did ten years ago — but your yard still expects the same effort.

The worst part? Most people just push through the pain. They pop an ibuprofen, grab the rake, and tell themselves it's no big deal. Until one day it is a big deal. Until one day, like Ray, you can barely stand up straight on Sunday morning.

I Tried the "Solutions" on the Market. They Were All Compromises.

Before I started building anything, I tried to solve Ray's problem the easy way. I bought him every tool I could find.

First, a cordless leaf blower from a big-box store. Lighter than gas, sure — but it felt like blowing through a straw. Wet leaves on concrete? Forget it. It couldn't even move pine needles off a smooth driveway.

Then I tried a mid-range electric model. Better power, but still 9 pounds. Nine pounds doesn't sound like much — until you're holding it at arm's length for 15 minutes with a bad back. Ray used it twice and went back to the rake.

I even looked at professional-grade battery blowers. Some were genuinely powerful. But they cost $350 to $500, weighed 11+ pounds, and Ray would've needed a second mortgage to justify the price on his postal retirement pension.

Every option was the same frustrating trade-off: light but weak, or powerful but heavy. Nobody had figured out how to deliver real force in a package a 70-year-old man with sciatica could hold comfortably in one hand.

That's when the engineer in me woke up.
