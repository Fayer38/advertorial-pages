Frank Holzmann is sixty-five, lives in Asheville, North Carolina, and spends most mornings on his back porch watching the pileated woodpeckers work the oak line behind his property.

For two summers, he watched those birds through mid-priced binoculars he kept telling himself were good enough. They weren't. The image went soft at the edges, colors washed out at dawn. Every serious birder kept nudging him toward Austrian glass north of twelve hundred dollars. Frank kept putting it off. He'd spent his career inside that industry. He knew exactly what those binoculars actually cost to build.

So he did something most retired engineers never bother with. He went back into his shop and built the pair he had always wanted to own.

"The glass in a $1,400 pair costs the factory under a hundred. I know. I ground it."

Forty-One Years Inside the Premium Optics Business

🖼️
Emplacement média — problem_section.image
"Older male optics engineer in a clean lab coat examining a prism under bright sh"

Frank trained as an optical technician outside Munich at twenty-two and stayed inside the trade for forty-one years, grinding lenses and tuning prisms for two of the most respected binocular houses in Bavaria.

He knows the bill of materials cold. A flagship 12x42 with fully multi-coated HD glass, hand-aligned BAK4 prisms, and a nitrogen-purged magnesium chassis runs the factory under one hundred dollars in parts and labor. The same instrument leaves the showroom floor at fourteen hundred. "That gap isn't engineering," Frank likes to say. "That's a marketing department, a distributor cut, and a hangtag."

The Austrian and German luxury houses had built an entire pricing tier on serious birders simply having no other option. Frank had watched it for four decades and knew a different path was technically possible.

He retired in 2024 and spent eight months iterating in his Asheville garage — same coatings, same prisms, same waterproof spec, no luxury hangtag. The contradiction: nobody in the U.S. was actually selling that build at a sane price, because nobody outside the factories knew it could be done.

The Clariva is not the kind of pair you find blister-packed at a sporting-goods chain. Those are ground-up budget builds — single-coated glass, BK7 prisms, plastic chassis — the optical equivalent of looking through a dirty kitchen window. They cost the factory almost nothing and they show it the moment the light gets tricky.

What Frank built instead is a fully multi-coated HD optical stack mated to hand-aligned BAK4 roof prisms in a nitrogen-purged magnesium-alloy housing. The 12x magnification with 42mm objectives is the configuration serious birders have been buying at the four-figure tier for thirty years — same glass science, same waterproof spec, no luxury markup attached to it.

No tripod required. No phone app. No subscription. You pull them out of the case, twist the diopter once for your eyes, and that's the entire setup.
