But here is what the blanket industry does not tell you: the overheating problem was solved years ago by a completely different category of fiber.

The Real Problem

Your body naturally drops its core temperature by one to two degrees to initiate sleep. This thermoregulation process is essential for entering deep, restorative sleep cycles.

When a blanket traps heat against your skin, it blocks this natural cooling mechanism. Your body fights to regulate, you wake up, and the calming weight becomes useless.

Traditional weighted blankets use glass beads or plastic pellets sewn inside polyester shells. The weight works. The material does not. Polyester has almost zero moisture-wicking ability and minimal breathability.

Cotton is a step up from polyester, but cotton absorbs moisture and holds it. On a warm night, a cotton weighted blanket becomes damp, clingy, and uncomfortable within hours.

The uncomfortable truth: most weighted blankets were designed for weight, not for sleep

Manufacturers focused on making blankets heavy enough to trigger deep pressure stimulation. They chose the cheapest materials that could hold the weight — polyester and cotton blends.

Nobody asked whether those materials would let your body actually do what it needs to do at night: cool down, stay dry, and breathe.

What sleep researchers actually recommend you look for in a weighted blanket

The material that solves overheating is not a new synthetic or gel layer. It is eucalyptus-derived TENCEL Lyocell fiber, a natural material that wicks moisture three times faster than cotton.

Eucalyptus fiber is naturally thermoregulating. It pulls heat and moisture away from your skin and releases it into the surrounding air, keeping you in the temperature sweet spot all night.

But material alone is not enough — construction matters just as much

Even with the right fiber, a tightly woven blanket still traps warm air between you and the fabric. This is why many cooling blankets still feel stuffy after an hour.

The construction that allows airflow is an open-knit design. Think of it as a weighted blanket with built-in ventilation — air moves freely through the weave while weight stays evenly distributed.
