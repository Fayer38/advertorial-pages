After 34 Years Grinding Lenses, He Finally Sells Them Direct

A retired Rochester engineer rejected a wholesaler deal and quietly changed everything

By Daniel Keane, Contributing Writer, Rochester

Frank Hollister spent thirty-four years inside a Rochester, New York optics plant, grinding lenses for binoculars that left the loading dock wearing a luxury-tier hangtag most hobbyists would never touch. In 2022, at sixty-seven, he walked out with his final paycheck and a quiet anger.

That same week, his brother-in-law had just handed over a serious chunk of his retirement savings for a pair of Austrian binoculars to watch warblers behind his Finger Lakes cabin. Frank already knew that Austrian prism stack. He had ground identical glass for decades.

That night, at his kitchen table in Henrietta, Frank opened a yellow legal pad and started sketching a twelve-by-forty-two built on the same BAK4 prism stack he had polished under fluorescent light since the Carter administration.

No Bass Pro middleman. No sales rep commission. No glossy catalog photography. Just the optics, shipped out of a small warehouse a mile from the plant that had retired him. He called them Premium HD Compact Binoculars 12x42.

Thirty-Four Years at the Bench

Frank started at the Rochester plant in 1988, twenty-three years old, fresh out of the Rochester Institute of Technology's optical engineering program. His father had ground lenses for Kodak through the sixties. His grandfather had repaired surveyor scopes for the Army Corps of Engineers.

"A good binocular is ninety percent invisible," Frank says. "Everything that makes it worth the money happens at tolerances the buyer will never see. Prism angles aligned within arc-seconds. Coating layers measured in nanometers. That's the part catalogs never photograph."

For three decades, he ground objective lenses for one of the top three European houses on contract — the kind of glass that ends up branded with a small Austrian eagle and resold at American retailers for nearly the cost of a used car.

The markup didn't bother him at first. Luxury brands, he figured, had to pay for European labor, rent in Vienna, advertising in Audubon Magazine. Then, in 2019, a plant accountant left a costing sheet on the breakroom table.

The contract house was billing those Austrian brands roughly one-seventh of what the finished pair eventually sold for at Cabela's and Bass Pro. Five stops of markup. Distributors, importers, retailers, rep networks, glossy-catalog printing budgets. Six middlemen between Frank's bench and the birder's neck strap.

The Wholesaler Who Came Calling

Word traveled. Six months after Frank's first production run shipped in the spring of 2023, a regional sporting-goods distributor from Harrisburg drove five hours to his warehouse in Henrietta. He wanted to put the binoculars on a catalog spread next to brands selling for three and four times more.

The offer was simple. The distributor would buy the full production run at a wholesale figure that covered Frank's costs plus a modest per-unit profit. In return, the binoculars would sit on sporting-goods shelves beside mid-tier German and Japanese brands, marked up three or four times over.

Frank turned him down at the kitchen table. "I spent thirty-four years watching good retirees get fleeced by that exact markup," he said later. "If I let him put these on a shelf at triple my price, I become the very thing I walked away from."

The distributor left with a handshake and no order. Frank went back to his workbench and kept shipping orders one at a time — UPS boxes labeled in his own handwriting, to addresses in Maine, Montana, Oregon, Wyoming. He calls this his integrity price.

"I want these in the hands of the guy sitting on a stump at four-thirty in the morning waiting for a wood thrush," Frank says. "Not behind glass at a strip-mall outfitter, marked up so a rep can hit his quarterly number."

The math worked because Frank had cut out five of the six stops. No distributor. No importer. No retailer. No rep commission. No catalog printer. Just the contract optical house, a small warehouse in Henrietta, and UPS.

What Actually Sits Inside the Barrel

The prism stack is the tell. Cheap binoculars use BK7 glass, which clips the exit pupil into a squared-off oval and steals brightness at the edges. Frank specified BAK4 phase-corrected roof prisms, the same grade that sits inside the Austrian pairs his brother-in-law loves.

Then the coatings. Every air-to-glass surface — sixteen of them in a standard twelve-by-forty-two — receives a fully multi-coated treatment measured in layers of lanthanum, silica, and magnesium fluoride. The result is ninety-plus percent light transmission, which is what separates dawn birding from squinting at a blur.

The objective lenses are extra-low-dispersion glass — the ED designation. Without ED, anything bright against a dark background gets a purple or green halo, a distortion called chromatic aberration. With it, the edge of a cedar waxwing's wing stays sharp against a white winter sky.

The chassis is machined aluminum wrapped in textured rubber, nitrogen-purged and O-ring sealed. Waterproof to five feet. Fog-proof across a fifty-degree temperature swing. The kind of reliability you want when a late-October drizzle rolls across the Finger Lakes and the warblers finally come out.

Twelve-power magnification with forty-two-millimeter objectives — the sweet spot for general outdoor use. Close enough to pick out a red-breasted nuthatch at a hundred and fifty feet. Compact enough to hang from a neck strap for a six-mile trail day without your shoulders quitting.

Walter Brennick, seventy-one, a retired USGS hydrologist in Bozeman, Montana, bought a pair in the second production run. He had been carrying a high-end Austrian pair since 1994. "Frank's sit within three percent of them in brightness," he wrote. "I compared them off the back deck one Tuesday morning."

Marcia Tolbert, sixty-eight, a part-time county extension agent in Coos Bay, Oregon, uses hers on the Pacific coast for migrating shorebirds. "I paid a lot for a Japanese pair back in 2015," she wrote. "These are sharper. I cried a little when I figured out what that meant."

Don and Linda Ashworth, both seventy-four, have birded the Pine Barrens of New Jersey together since 1991. Don carried an older European pair for thirty-one years before rotator-cuff surgery made the weight hard to manage. "The Hollister pair is a hair lighter and optically indistinguishable," he wrote. "Should have called Frank years ago."

Frank's ledger shows a pattern. Roughly one in four buyers returns for a second pair — for a spouse, an adult child, a grandson who just got into ornithology at SUNY ESF. His brother-in-law, the warbler watcher, now owns three pairs: one for the cabin, one for the truck, one as a gift.

In twenty-three months of shipping, Frank has accepted zero optical-defect returns. Two pairs have come back — one crushed in a UPS hub, one dropped out of a hunting stand in Idaho. Both were replaced at his cost.

Every pair leaves Henrietta with Frank's initials laser-etched inside the hinge, beside the production number. The twenty-third pair of the fourth batch reads FH-4-023. It is a small vanity, he admits. Also an insurance policy against future counterfeit shops that might copy his work.

The Direct Price, Finally Named

Frank resists calling what he does a sale, a discount, or a liquidation. Those words imply a retail anchor he never used. "I'm not cutting a price," he says. "I'm shipping the pair at what it actually costs to make, plus a fair wage for the people who help."

An optical pair of this exact specification — BAK4 prisms, ED glass, fully multi-coated, nitrogen-purged metal body — retails elsewhere at the price of a used-car engine. Frank's price is the kind of figure retirees usually associate with a tank of gas and a Costco grocery run.

Production runs are small — Frank orders from the contract optical house in lots of roughly eight hundred. He is currently on the fourth batch. As of last Friday, the warehouse shelf in Henrietta held six hundred and thirty-four pairs, with more walking out every day.

Frank is sixty-seven. His hands, after three decades at a grinding wheel, do not cooperate the way they did in 1993. His cardiologist has suggested a lighter schedule. He has told his brother-in-law that when this production cycle ends, he is done.

There is no next generation learning the trade. Frank's son is a software engineer in Seattle. His daughter teaches in a Rochester suburb. "I'm not building a brand for someone to inherit," Frank says. "I'm finishing the job and going fishing."
