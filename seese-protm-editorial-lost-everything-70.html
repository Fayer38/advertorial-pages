He Lost Everything After a Car Crash — Then Built a 2lb Blower That Saved His Back and His Business

By Editorial Team | March 30, 2026

Nine months ago, David Chen couldn't lift a glass of water without wincing.

A head-on collision on I-95 had shattered his L3 vertebra, crushed two discs, and ended his career as a landscaping equipment distributor overnight. The hospital bills piled up — $187,000 and counting. His warehouse? Liquidated. His inventory of gas blowers, trimmers, and mowers? Sold at auction for pennies on the dollar.

The man who once moved 4,000 blowers a month couldn't even blow the leaves off his own porch.

"I was sitting on my front steps, watching dead leaves pile up against the garage door," David told me last Tuesday, his voice still catching. "And I thought — there has to be a better way. Not just for me. For everyone whose body can't handle these machines anymore."

The Hidden Epidemic Nobody Talks About

David's story isn't unique. It's just more dramatic than most.

Every autumn, millions of Americans over 55 face the same quiet battle: the yard needs cleaning, but their body is screaming no.

Maybe it's not a car accident. Maybe it's decades of wear and tear. A rotator cuff that never fully healed. Arthritis that creeps a little deeper every winter. A back that locks up after fifteen minutes of anything.

The result is always the same.

You stare at the garage. You see that 23-pound gas blower hanging on the wall. You remember what happened last time — the aching shoulders, the throbbing lower back, the two days of recovery for thirty minutes of work.

And you think: "Maybe I'll do it next weekend."

But next weekend never comes.

I Tried Everything. Nothing Worked.

After the accident, David went through the same cycle every homeowner with chronic pain goes through.

First, he tried pushing through it. "I strapped on a back brace and grabbed my neighbor's electric blower. Ten minutes in, I was on my knees in the driveway. My wife found me there."

Then he tried the alternatives.

A leaf rake? Forty-five minutes for a small patio. His physical therapist told him to stop immediately — the twisting motion was undoing months of recovery.

A broom? Twenty-five minutes of sweeping for what a blower does in three. And his shoulders paid the price for days.

Hiring a lawn service? $150 a month — money he didn't have. Not with $187,000 in medical debt staring him down.

A "lightweight" cordless blower from the hardware store? It weighed 9 pounds. Sounds light until you hold it at arm's length for ten minutes with a damaged spine. And the wind speed? Barely enough to move dry grass clippings, let alone wet autumn leaves stuck to concrete.

"Every single option was designed for someone with a healthy body," David said. "Nobody was thinking about the 70-year-old woman with arthritis. The guy recovering from surgery. The retired couple whose kids keep telling them to 'just hire someone.'"

That's when something shifted in David's mind.

He'd spent 22 years in the blower industry. He knew every motor, every spec sheet, every factory in Shenzhen. He knew exactly why traditional blowers were heavy — and he knew it didn't have to be that way.

"I Designed It From My Hospital Bed"

Here's what most people don't know about gas and corded blowers: 80% of the weight has nothing to do with power.

It's the engine housing. The fuel tank. The heavy-gauge wiring. The cast-metal motor casing designed in the 1980s and never updated.

David knew this because he'd watched the drone industry solve the exact same problem. Drones needed to be light enough to fly — but powerful enough to stabilize in 40 MPH winds. The answer was brushless motors: smaller, lighter, more efficient, and three times more durable than traditional motors.

"I called my old factory contact in Guangdong from my hospital room," David recalls. "I said: I need a brushless turbine motor that spins at 110,000 RPM, fits in the palm of my hand, and weighs under a pound. He said I was crazy. Six weeks later, he sent me the first prototype."

That prototype became the Seese Pro™.

A cordless blower that weighs 2.1 pounds — less than a bag of sugar — and delivers 200 MPH wind speed at 30,000+ RPM.

To put that in perspective: most gas blowers that weigh ten times more top out at 150 MPH.

David tested it the day it arrived. One-handed. Standing upright. No brace needed.

"I cleared my entire patio in four minutes. Then I sat down and cried. Not from pain — for the first time in months, there was no pain. I cried because I knew this would help people like me."
