I spent thirty years designing pressure circuits for industrial water systems. I never thought I'd build a consumer product. Then I walked into Home Depot.

What I saw on those shelves wasn't engineering. It was packaging. Heavy machines tethered to a wall, sold to people who already own hoses.

My cabin in Geauga County has no outdoor spigot. The deck was green with algae. The Honda was caked with road salt. And fifteen feet away sat a lake.

Forty thousand gallons of water. Not a single consumer machine that could use it. So I went home, opened my workbench, and started prototyping.

Why Every Pressure Washer On The Shelf Is Solving The Wrong Problem

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s1
"Crowded retail aisle of pressure washers at an American home improvement store, "

The category quietly stopped trying. Margins were strong, lawsuits were rare, and homeowners kept buying replacements every two summers. There was no incentive to disturb the model.

Look at any retail floor. Same plastic shrouds, same trigger guns, same fifty-foot hose requirement. Nothing has fundamentally changed since the late nineties.

The assumption underneath every machine is the same: you have a wall, you have a tap, and you have somewhere to drain. Most of the country doesn't.

I have neighbors with boats on gravel pads. I have friends with RVs at campgrounds. None of them have a hose bib within a hundred feet.

The industry treats those people as edge cases. From thirty years inside hydraulic engineering, I can tell you they are the actual majority of buyers.

The Five Frustrations That Send Most Pressure Washers Back To The Box

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s2
"A tangled garden hose wrapped around a tipped-over pressure washer on a suburban"

I keep a notebook of every complaint I've heard at hardware counters, neighborhood yard sales, and the parking lot of the rental yard near my cabin.

Five problems show up in nearly every conversation. Once you see them listed, the design failures become obvious.

The hose tether. You move ten feet, the hose snags, the machine tips, and you spend more time wrestling rubber than cleaning anything.

The flow requirement. If your municipal pressure is low or you're on a well, the machine starves and the pump cavitates within minutes.

The weight. Most machines run forty to seventy pounds. Carrying one to a boat slip or a second-floor balcony is its own afternoon project.

The noise. Gas units sound like a generator. Electric units rattle the deck. Either way, the neighbors know exactly what you're doing on a Saturday.

The drainage. All that runoff has to go somewhere. On a paver driveway it pools. On a gravel pad it cuts channels. Nobody plans for it.

None of these are exotic complaints. They're the predictable result of designing for a showroom rather than a backyard.

Every one of them disappears the moment you stop assuming the user has a wall, a tap, and a drain within reach.

What I Tried Before I Gave Up And Built My Own

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s3
"Equipment rental counter at an American home improvement store, rental pressure "

Before I started prototyping, I went through every reasonable alternative. Six months of testing what was already on the market.

Each one failed at the cabin for a different reason. None of them solved the basic problem: no spigot, no hookup, no patience for plastic.

The brand-name electric. Twenty-five feet of cord, fifty feet of hose. I needed two extension cords just to reach the deck.

The gas-powered model. Loud, heavy, and the carburetor gummed up after sitting through one Ohio winter without fuel stabilizer.

The cordless brand washer. Battery died after eight minutes. Charger took three hours. The math doesn't work for a real cleaning job.

Renting a unit from the home center. Forty-five minute drive each way, deposit, fuel, and a return window that ate the whole afternoon.

Hiring a mobile detail crew. Showed up with their own tank, charged what felt like a mortgage payment, and left a streak across the deck.

After the last attempt, I sat in the garage with a bucket of well water and asked a different question. What if the bucket was the source?

How A Hydraulic Engineer Rethinks The Whole Pressure Washer Category

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s4
"A workshop bench with hand-drawn schematics, a partial prototype of a portable p"

Industrial systems don't assume infrastructure. You build for the field, the rig, the mine. The water comes from where it comes from.

I borrowed five principles from my old work and applied them to a tool a homeowner could carry in one hand.

  • Self-priming pump. Pulls water uphill from any open container — a bucket, a cooler, a kiddie pool, a rain barrel, or the lake itself.
  • Lithium battery pack. Same chemistry as a cordless drill, sized for a real cleaning session rather than an eight-minute demo.
  • Pressure-balanced nozzle. Calibrated to lift dirt and algae without etching wood, chipping paint, or stripping the clear coat off a vehicle.
  • Single-handed trigger and balance point. The whole unit weighs less than a gallon of milk. You hold it like a garden hose, not a leaf blower.
  • No tether, no cord, no compromise. Walk wherever the dirt is. The water source follows you in a five-gallon bucket.
