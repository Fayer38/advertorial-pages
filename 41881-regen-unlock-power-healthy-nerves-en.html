It was a gray February morning in Minneapolis when I finally agreed to take the assignment. My editor wanted an honest, eight-week look at Regen+ by Nerve Renew, a supplement I had quietly heard about from three different readers in less than a month.

Like many women in their late fifties, I had started waking up with strange tingling in my left foot. My mother lived with diabetic neuropathy for years, and the idea of inheriting that slow burn was, frankly, the reason I said yes.

I am a reporter, not a believer. I have spent two decades writing about supplements that promised the moon and delivered, at best, a placebo. So I went in expecting another disappointment, and I told the company exactly that.

What follows is not a sales pitch. It is what I documented: the science I checked, the patients I interviewed, the days I logged my own symptoms in a spreadsheet, and the quiet shift I did not expect to feel.

Where This Formula Actually Comes From

Before testing anything, I wanted to know who was behind the bottle. Regen+ is the latest formulation from Nerve Renew, a small Wyoming-based company that has been working on peripheral neuropathy support for more than a decade.

Their lead formulator, a soft-spoken biochemist named Dr. Roberts, learned the field from his father, a pharmacist who spent forty years compounding nerve preparations for diabetic patients in rural clinics. The work, he told me, was not chosen so much as inherited.

"My father used to say nerves do not heal by accident," Roberts said over the phone. "You give them the right raw materials, in the right ratios, for long enough, and the body remembers what to do. That is the whole philosophy here."

Nerve Renew has quietly built a reputation among neuropathy support groups since 2011. Every batch of Regen+ is tested in-house and again by a third-party lab in Salt Lake City, with certificates of analysis available on request. I asked for three. They sent them.

But in 2024, everything about the formula changed. A single letter from the family of a long-time customer landed on Roberts's desk that February, and within ninety days the entire philosophy behind Nerve Renew would be quietly torn apart and rebuilt.

The Patient Who Changed Everything

In late 2023, Roberts lost a long-time customer named Margaret, a retired teacher from Bismarck who had been taking the original Nerve Renew formula for nine years. She died of unrelated heart failure, and her family wrote the company a letter.

"She told us your supplement gave her back the ability to feel her grandchildren's hands," Roberts read aloud, voice catching. "That hit me harder than I expected. I had been treating this work like chemistry. She reminded me it was something else entirely."

He withdrew from the lab for several weeks. His daughter flew in from Denver. The phones at the small office kept ringing with refill orders, but the formulator who answered them was, by his own admission, somewhere else entirely for a while.

Then one Sunday before sunrise, Roberts drove back to the lab alone. He turned on the overhead lights, pulled every binder of clinical research from the shelf, and started reading from page one. He did not leave for two days.

"It felt like making a promise," he told me later. "To Margaret, to my father, to every person who had trusted us. I owed them a formula built on what we actually knew now, not on what we knew in 2011."

From that February forward, he reviewed roughly forty published nerve-regeneration studies a week, for sixteen straight months, before allowing the new Regen+ formulation to be filled into a single capsule.

What Is Actually Inside the Capsule

"Macro photograph of a single supplement capsule split open showing fine cream-co"

To understand why Regen+ behaves differently from the dozen nerve supplements I have reviewed before, you have to look at one specific detail: the form of B1 it uses, called benfotiamine, a fat-soluble version that crosses cell membranes far more efficiently than ordinary thiamine.

A standard mass-market nerve supplement delivers around 50 milligrams of basic thiamine, much of which is excreted within hours. Regen+ delivers 300 milligrams of stabilized benfotiamine per serving, alongside a clinically used dose of methylcobalamin, the active form of B12.

"People do not buy this for how it tastes or how the bottle looks," Roberts said. "They buy it because in eight to twelve weeks they can feel the difference when they walk to the mailbox. That is the only metric we care about."

Each batch is hand-blended in small runs of roughly four thousand bottles, third-party tested for heavy metals, and capped within seventy-two hours of mixing to protect the methylcobalamin, which degrades quickly when exposed to light or oxygen.

Generic copycat brands cannot replicate this for one simple reason: stabilized benfotiamine at this dose costs roughly nine times what the cheap thiamine in drugstore formulas does, and most contract manufacturers refuse to slow their lines down for it.

Why You Have Probably Never Heard Of It

The reason a formula this carefully made remains relatively unknown is, frankly, brutal. The neuropathy supplement aisle is dominated by two or three large brands with massive television budgets, slick packaging, and formulas that have not been meaningfully updated in fifteen years.

When Nerve Renew tried selling through a major national pharmacy chain in 2019, the buyer offered them a fraction of what their bottles needed to retail for, then planned to mark it up several times over before it ever reached a shelf.

"We were being squeezed from both ends," Roberts admitted. "The retailers wanted a wholesale price that did not even cover our raw ingredients, and our customers were being asked to pay nearly triple what the bottle was actually worth at the register."

He realized one afternoon, sitting in his car in a Walgreens parking lot in Cheyenne, that the model itself was the problem. The formula was being designed around what survived the retail markup, not around what would actually help his customers' nerves.

He started asking a question that, in the supplement industry, is almost considered heresy: what would happen if we skipped the middleman entirely and shipped every bottle straight from our warehouse to the person who needed it?

That single question, asked aloud in a Cheyenne parking lot on an ordinary weekday afternoon, is what produced everything that followed: the reformulation, the direct-to-customer model, and the deliberate decision to walk away from every retail relationship the company had spent ten years building.

The Decision That Made Regen+ Possible

🖼️

"Small American supplement warehouse with neatly stacked shipping boxes ready for"

Within ninety days, Roberts had pulled Regen+ from every wholesale channel and every retail relationship the company had built over a decade. From that point forward, every bottle would ship from the small Wyoming warehouse directly to the customer's mailbox.

The arithmetic was simple but radical. Without the wholesale discount, the retailer's markup, and the chain's slotting fees, the same carefully made bottle could be offered to customers at roughly a third of what a comparable retail formula would have cost them.

Production is intentionally limited. The current run of Regen+ is capped at the inventory their small lab can hand-blend and verify each quarter, which means there are typically only a few thousand bottles available for new customers at any given time.

"When this batch is gone, it is gone for the quarter," Roberts told me plainly. "We will not rush the next one. We will not cut a corner to refill the shelves faster. People who need this formula deserve the version we promised them."

"I want this in the hands of the woman who can finally feel her husband's palm again," he said quietly. "Not on a pharmacy shelf gathering dust. That is the only audience I am building for now."
