A Letter From The Man Who Walked Away From The $1,650 Binocular Racket

After six weeks watching a reporter trace every dollar of a European flagship pair, I couldn't stay quiet anymore. Here's what I built instead — and why I'm writing you today.

Dear friend,

I want to tell you something that has been sitting on my chest for months now, and I think if you love being outdoors the way I do — if you have ever stood in a Cabela's or a Bass Pro and quietly put a pair of binoculars back on the shelf because the price tag felt like a slap — then you deserve to hear it from someone who actually knows the numbers.

My name is Daniel Hollis. I'm 61 years old. I've spent most of my working life in optics, the last seven of them in a small workshop north of Portland, Oregon. I'm writing this letter to you because a consumer-affairs reporter from New Jersey spent six weeks at my kitchen table last fall, and what she uncovered changed how I think about this whole industry. I don't want to sell you anything today. I want to tell you the truth about what you've been paying for.

The reporter, the receipts, and a foggy morning at Cape May

Her name was Ellen. She called me last August because someone on a birding forum had mentioned my little company, and she was writing a piece for a Sunday business section about the optics market. She had already spent three weeks tracing the supply chain of a $1,650 Austrian flagship binocular sold out of a specialty dealer in New Jersey, and she wanted my take as someone who had worked the factory side for decades. I told her to come out. I put a pot of coffee on and we sat down.

What she laid out on my table I will never forget. The prism glass in that Austrian flagship? Sourced from the same Chinese optical foundry that supplies three other "premium" European brands. The coating process — the fancy-sounding FMC and phase-correction stack they put on the website — is industry-standard, run on the same machines used by half the market. The actual manufactured unit cost, landed in a warehouse in Europe, was roughly $140.

The remaining $1,510 was split, she showed me, across four hands. The European brand took the first cut. The US distributor took another. The regional rep took his piece. And the authorized dealer on the floor in New Jersey took roughly 38% of the final retail price. Four layers of handshakes between the factory and you. I stared at those numbers for a long time. I wasn't shocked by the math — I've known the math for years — I was shocked to see it written down in a reporter's notebook, in ink, with names and percentages.

Mid-investigation, she'd heard about the little 12x42 pair I was making out of the workshop. Direct-to-consumer. Eighty-nine dollars. Same BAK4 prism glass, same FMC coating, same ED stack. She brought her Austrian flagship out to Cape May on a foggy October morning last year and set them side by side on a tripod. She tested them for three hours against warblers, a distant lighthouse, and a cormorant on a piling. She told me, on the phone that night, that she could not tell the images apart.

What I built in the workshop, and why

I should back up and tell you how I got here. I spent twenty-two years inside the industry — I won't name the companies, but you'd know them. I watched good glass leave the factory at a fair price and arrive at a retail counter marked up four and five times over. I watched salesmen push seventy-year-old men toward a $2,000 pair because the commission structure favored it. I watched grandfathers walk out empty-handed because they'd done the math in their head and couldn't justify it to their wife. That bothered me for a long time before I finally did anything about it.

In 2019 I left. I took my severance, rented a small space in the Pacific Northwest, and I started building one pair — just one model — the way I'd always wanted to. A compact 12x42 HD with a real metal chassis, full waterproofing, nitrogen purge against fogging, BAK4 prisms, ED glass, and the same multi-coating process the big brands charge you a mortgage for. I buy the components from the same reputable foundries the premium brands use — I know the people, I've walked the floors — and I assemble, inspect, and ship them out of a single building in Oregon.

No distributors. No regional reps. No authorized dealer margin. No retail showroom rent. Just the glass, the chassis, the coatings, and a man who gives a damn packing it in a box and writing a thank-you note. That's why the pair costs $89 and not $1,650. It is not magic. It is not a knock-off. It is the same optics — without the four hands in the middle taking their cut.

A retired ophthalmologist in Vermont wrote me in September. He had used German glass for forty years. He bought a pair of mine, took them to the marsh behind his house, and wrote me a two-page letter afterward. I kept it. Here is part of it:

"I walked out that first morning expecting disappointment. I came back expecting to call my wife into the kitchen so I could tell her I had been robbed for forty years. The red on the throat of a ruby-throated hummingbird at thirty yards — I saw it the way I saw it at twenty-five. That is not an exaggeration. I owe you a debt for that morning."
— Letter from Dr. Harold Ferris, 74, Woodstock VT

Why I'm writing you today, specifically

Here is the part that is hard for me to write. After Ellen's piece ran, my inbox broke. I had been quietly selling a few hundred pairs a month to people who found me through birding forums and word of mouth, and suddenly I had thousands of orders in a week. I am not a large company. I am a small outfit with a handful of people I trust. We are producing as fast as we honestly can without compromising a single coating or a single inspection, and even so, I have had to cap this run.

I went back and forth on whether to write this letter at all. My wife told me to. She said the people who are going to read it are the ones who have been feeling cheated for years, the ones who gave up on owning good glass because they refused to play the game, and that they deserve to know there is a door out. So I am writing it. The current production run I have set aside for readers of this piece is limited — I am being honest about that because I have nothing to gain by lying to you.

I am not asking you to trust me on faith. I am asking you to test the pair for thirty days on your own terrain. Take them out at dawn. Take them to the blind. Take them to the ridge behind your house where you have been squinting through a cheap pair for too many seasons. If the clarity does not make you sit down on a rock and say something out loud, send them back. I will refund you in full and I will pay the return shipping. I wouldn't make that offer if I didn't already know what you are going to see through them.
