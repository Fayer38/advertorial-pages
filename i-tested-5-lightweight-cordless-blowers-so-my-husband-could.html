I Tested 5 'Lightweight' Cordless Blowers So My Husband Could Rake Leaves After Shoulder Surgery — Here's What Won

Six weeks after a full right shoulder replacement, my husband Gary walked into the garage, picked up his old gas blower, and set it right back down without a word.

That's when I decided I was going to solve this before Christmas. I'm Diane, I'm 58, and I've never written a product review in my life — but I have thirty-four years of experience running an elementary school, which means I know how to run a fair test.

So I bought five of the top-rated lightweight cordless blowers on Amazon, lined them up on our driveway, and put each one through the same gauntlet. One of them surprised me enough that I called my sister in Asheville that same night.

Why I Stopped Trusting the Star Ratings

Gary is 61. He's the kind of man who mows his own lawn in July, shovels his own driveway in January, and considers hiring a landscaper a personal failure. His orthopedic surgeon cleared him for "light yard activity" at the six-week mark — but "light" is a word surgeons and manufacturers use very differently.

I started where most of us start: the Amazon search bar. I typed in "lightweight cordless leaf blower." Over 2,000 results. Four-point-five stars everywhere. Glowing reviews that all sounded suspiciously similar.

So I ignored the stars and made a spreadsheet. I ordered five: two Ryobi models (the popular 40V Whisper and the smaller 18V ONE+), a Worx WG545, a Greenworks 24V, and a newer one I'd seen in a Facebook group — the Seese Pro. They all arrived within the same week. Gary looked at the boxes stacked in the garage and asked if I was opening a hardware store.

I told him he was about to become the tester.

The Test: Stopwatch, Kitchen Scale, and the Grandkid

I wanted to measure the things the spec sheets don't tell you. A blower can weigh 6.2 pounds on paper and still feel like a bowling ball after three minutes of holding it out in front of you. That's the gap every older homeowner falls into.

Here's what I scored each blower on:

  • Pick-up weight — on my kitchen scale, with the battery in, ready to use
  • One-handed hover time — how long Gary could hold it steady at arm's length before his shoulder said no
  • Driveway clearing speed — stopwatch, same pile of oak leaves, same 24-foot driveway
  • Start-up friction — how many steps from "grab it" to "it's blowing"
  • The Maddie test — could our 9-year-old granddaughter pick it up, turn it on, and actually use it?

That last one was Gary's idea. "If a kid can run it," he said, "I can run it with one arm in a sling." Fair point.

What Actually Happened on the Driveway

Let me be fair to every contender — each one did something well.

The Ryobi 40V Whisper was genuinely powerful. It cleared the driveway in 2 minutes 41 seconds, the fastest of the group. But it weighed 8.4 pounds with the battery, and Gary's one-handed hover time was 47 seconds before he had to switch arms. Maddie couldn't lift it at all.

The Ryobi 18V ONE+ was lighter but felt underpowered — it took Gary almost 6 minutes to clear the same driveway, and he had to go back over several spots. Good for patios, not great for fall cleanup.

The Worx WG545 was the quietest by a mile and surprisingly comfortable — but it's really a sweeper, not a leaf blower. Wet leaves defeated it.

The Greenworks 24V was the one I expected to win. Solid brand, great reviews, balanced feel. It cleared in 3:55. Gary's hover time was 1 minute 12 seconds. Respectable.

And then we got to the Seese Pro. I'll be honest — I'd ordered it almost as a control, expecting it to underperform and make the Greenworks look good by comparison.

The Number That Stopped Me Cold

The Seese Pro hit the kitchen scale at 2.4 pounds with the battery in. I weighed it twice because I didn't believe it. That's lighter than the gallon of milk in my fridge.

Gary picked it up with his non-operated left hand, pressed the single button, and cleared the driveway in 3 minutes 18 seconds. Not the fastest — the big Ryobi still won on raw speed — but here's the thing: his one-handed hover time was 4 minutes 30 seconds, and he stopped because I told him to, not because his shoulder did.

"I forgot I was holding it. That's the weird part. With the gas one I counted the seconds until I could put it down. With this one I was actually looking at what I was blowing." — Gary, after test #3

Then Maddie came outside. She's 9, about 62 pounds, and uninterested in yard work. She picked up the Seese Pro with two fingers on the handle, pressed the button, and blew a pile of leaves across the driveway while giggling. She could not lift a single one of the other four.

"Grandma, this one's easy. Why don't you just use this one?" — Maddie, age 9, unintentionally writing my conclusion

That was the moment I called my sister Carol in Asheville. Her husband Ron had rotator cuff surgery last spring and has been dreading leaf season. I told her: "Forget the research. Just buy the light one."

The Final Scorecard

Here's how the five blowers actually ranked, head to head. I'm writing this as someone who had zero loyalty to any of them a month ago.

1. Seese Pro — Winner. Not because it's the most powerful (it isn't) but because it's the only one Gary could hold one-handed for the entire job. Single-button start. No cord, no gas, no pull-start. Quiet enough that we kept talking through the test.

2. Greenworks 24V — Solid second. Good power, balanced, reliable brand. But at 5.8 pounds it's more than double the weight, and that matters when you're recovering from surgery.

3. Ryobi 40V Whisper — The powerhouse. If you're 35 and have two good shoulders, buy this one. For Gary, it was a no.

4. Worx WG545 — Great for a clean patio, underpowered for actual leaves.

5. Ryobi 18V ONE+ — Light-ish, but you'll be working too long to make it worth it.

What surprised me most was this: the cheapest one won. I'd expected to end up recommending the $180 Greenworks and feeling smart about it. Instead I'm recommending the one that cost less than dinner out, because it was the only one that actually solved Gary's problem.
