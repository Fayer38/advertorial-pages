I Tested Six Cordless Pressure Washers In My Driveway — One Stayed In My Garage

Three weekends, a stopwatch, a bucket, and one filthy F-150.

Mark R., Cincinnati Homeowner Updated April 2026

My old Ryobi corded washer finally died in March, and I figured replacing it would be simple.

Two hours of YouTube reviews later, I had no idea which cordless unit was actually worth buying.

  • The cheap Mrliance units looked sketchy but kept showing up on top-ten lists with thousands of reviews.
  • The EGO Power+ at the high end had the PSI numbers but cost as much as a decent generator.
  • DeWalt and Greenworks landed in the middle with mixed reviews about battery life and real-world cleaning power.
  • Every spec sheet bragged about PSI, but nobody answered whether the thing could actually clean my truck.

So I did what any obsessive homeowner with a Home Depot card and three free weekends would do.

Worst case, I'd return five of them and write off the gas money as the cost of a hobby.

I bought six units, lined them up on my garage floor, and pulled out the stopwatch.

What I found surprised me — and one of these things ended up being the only one I kept.

The Setup

My test rig was simple: a 5-gallon Home Depot bucket, my mud-caked F-150, and the back patio set.

Four metrics mattered to me: setup time from cold, cleaning speed per truck panel, storage footprint, and the parking lot test.

The parking lot test was personal: could I actually wash mud off the kids' soccer cleats at the field?

/v2/media-slot/d2c57d7c-2788-4074-8ff4-0a0739d2058e/media_3__caption

No spigot, no extension cord, no garden hose — just whatever water you can carry in a bucket.

Here's where most of these so-called cordless units quietly fell apart on me.

The DeWalt and EGO both technically work from a bucket, but the suction hose is finicky and prone to losing prime.

The Greenworks needs a pressurized inlet to perform near its rated PSI, which means a real garden hose.

Round one was setup time, measured cold from the box to first trigger pull.

The DeWalt took just under nine minutes — battery charge, hose attachment, and finding the right inlet adapter.

The EGO Power+ ate eleven minutes because the wand assembly is genuinely confusing for a first-time user.

Round two was the truck panel test, the one that actually matters most.

I drove the F-150 through a construction site to coat it in proper Ohio clay before each session.

The EGO had the most raw stripping power, no question — that 3,200 PSI rating is real.

But raw PSI is not what most homeowners actually need on a Saturday afternoon.

The Job-To-Be-Done Reframe That Changed My Whole Testing Approach Here

I do not need to strip a deck or blast graffiti — I need to clean a truck.

I need to wash off the patio set before the kids get bored and migrate inside.

I need something that lives on a shelf and gets pulled out twice a month without an event.

Once I admitted that, the EGO and DeWalt started looking like overkill — beautiful overkill, but overkill.

And the unit I almost dismissed because of its modest spec sheet started making a lot more sense.

The Dark Horse In This Test Was Something My Brother-In-Law In Dayton Put Me Onto

Seese Pro™ Anywhere Washer

It is a portable, battery-powered washer that draws water directly from any bucket, pool, or rain barrel.

No spigot, no extension cord, no garden hose, no inlet adapter — drop the suction tube and pull the trigger.

The whole thing weighs less than a gallon of milk and runs one-handed off a single battery.

Setup from cold was forty-five seconds — drop the suction line into a bucket and you are spraying.

It does not have the raw stripping power of the EGO, and I am not pretending otherwise.
