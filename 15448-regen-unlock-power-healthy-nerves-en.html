How I Stopped Sleeping With Frozen Socks And Started Holding My Grandson Without Fear

After three years of burning feet at 2 a.m., a printout from my daughter-in-law changed everything I thought I knew.

For three years, I kept a pair of socks in the freezer next to the ice cream. That probably tells you more than I want to admit. The burning in my feet would start around 2 a.m., and frozen cotton was the only thing that made it bearable until sunrise.

I'm 62, and I've managed Type 2 diabetes for fourteen years without complaint. But the tingling, the pins-and-needles, the sudden numbness in my fingertips — that became the thing that decided my days. I was sleeping in three-hour blocks. I quit the morning pool group at the rec center because I couldn't feel the deck under my feet.

If any of this sounds familiar, you should know you're not imagining it, and you're certainly not alone. Millions of women my age in the U.S. are quietly hiding the same symptoms from their families, ashamed of something nobody warned them about.

How My Story Started

It crept in three years ago. A faint pricking under my big toe after my evening walk, the kind of thing you blame on new shoes. By the following summer, the pricking was a fire that woke me up. My doctor at the clinic mentioned gabapentin and Cymbalta in the same breath, then handed me a pamphlet. I went home and never filled the prescription.

The breaking point came on a Sunday in October. My grandson Jackson, who's seven and built like a fire hydrant, came barreling through the kitchen and launched himself at me. I caught him, but I couldn't feel his weight. I almost dropped him on the tile. He laughed. I made some excuse and walked to the bathroom, locked the door, and cried for a full hour. I had become someone I didn't recognize.

That night, my daughter-in-law Erin — a hospital dietitian who has watched too many diabetes patients lose their feet — came over with a folder of printouts. She sat me at the kitchen table and started talking about oxidative stress, glycation, and demyelination. For the first time in three years, somebody was actually explaining what was happening inside me.

The 4 Types Of Neuropathy You Should Know About

Erin explained that not all nerve trouble is the same. Knowing which kind you're dealing with changes everything about how you treat it. Most women my age have one of these four, often without ever being told.

  • PERIPHERAL NERVE DAMAGE This is the most common kind, the burning and tingling in feet and hands that creeps in slowly and steals your sleep before you ever name it.
  • AUTONOMIC NERVE TROUBLE This one hits the nerves running your blood pressure, digestion, and sweating. You feel dizzy standing up, bloated after small meals, or strangely cold in a warm room.
  • PROXIMAL NERVE PAIN Pain that settles deep in the hips, thighs, or buttocks, often on one side. It can make standing up from a chair feel like a small daily mountain to climb.
  • FOCAL NERVE COMPRESSION A single nerve gets pinched or squeezed, often in the wrist, hand, or face. You drop things suddenly, your eyelid droops, or one cheek goes oddly numb for hours.

What Are The Usual Solutions Doctors Offer?

Most of us are handed the same short menu. Gabapentin, pregabalin, or Cymbalta first. If those don't work, you graduate to opioids, lidocaine patches, or a slow conversation about a TENS unit. Some doctors will mention a B-vitamin shot, others will shrug and tell you to keep your blood sugar tight and hope. The pamphlets all look the same, and so does the resignation in your doctor's voice when you come back saying nothing is helping.

The trouble with that menu is that it manages how loud your nerves are screaming, but it does nothing for the underlying damage. Gabapentin made my friend Joyce so foggy she missed her own anniversary dinner. Cymbalta gave my walking buddy night sweats and a number on the scale she refused to discuss. Insurance copays add up. Side effects pile on. And every refill is a quiet admission that you've accepted this as the rest of your life.

I sat at that kitchen table with Erin's printouts and realized something had to give. I wasn't going to be the grandmother who couldn't lift her grandkids. I wasn't going to spend another Christmas pretending. There had to be something that worked on the cause, not just the volume knob.
