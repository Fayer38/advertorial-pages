I Tested Six Portable Pressure Washers — One Surprised Me

By Mark Ferraro Last Updated · April 23, 2026
4.8 verified ★★★★★ 6,153 reviews
IT DIDN'T WIN ON OUTPUT. IT WON ON SETUP.

A viewer named Greg sent me a DM in February asking which cordless pressure washer was actually worth the money. I had opinions. He wanted data. So I ordered six units to my garage in Naperville and cleared a weekend.

Every unit ran the same two tests: a soot-streaked driveway panel I dragged out from behind the snowblower, and the muddy Trek mountain bike my son rode through the forest preserve trail in March. Same nozzle distance, same dwell time.

I weighed each washer with its battery installed, timed setup from box to first spray, and tracked the all-in cost — because DeWalt and EGO sell their batteries separately, which inflates the sticker every reviewer quotes.

The Six Units I Bought

DeWalt's DCPW550B is the heavyweight on paper — 550 PSI, brushless motor, runs on the 20V MAX platform. With a 5Ah battery installed it tipped my scale just past nine pounds, and the trigger pull is genuinely satisfying.

EGO's HPW3200 is the spec sheet champion at 3,200 PSI peak. It's also the unit that needed the most assembly, the bulkiest 56V battery, and a foot-rest brace I had to actually plant on the concrete to absorb recoil.

The Greenworks GPW1800HYB is the corded-cordless hybrid. Useful concept, awkward execution — you still need an outlet within fifty feet, which defeats the point if you're rinsing a kayak at the cabin or washing the truck at a campsite.

THE WINNER WASN'T THE STRONGEST. IT WAS THE ONE I'D ACTUALLY USE.

The Mrliance unit was my budget control. It cleaned. It also rattled, the trigger lock stuck twice, and the nozzle threading stripped on the third swap. I'd be back at the hardware store before fall.

The corded Ryobi was my benchmark — what most homeowners already own. And the Seese Pro Anywhere Washer was the one my brother-in-law Tony had been texting me about since Christmas. He'd used his cousin's at a tailgate.

What Setup Time Actually Looked Like

Setup is where the cordless category lives or dies. Your weekend has a window. If a tool eats twenty minutes before it sprays water, you're not going to grab it for a five-minute cleanup — you'll just live with the dirt.

DeWalt averaged eleven minutes from box to spray, mostly because the inlet hose has to mate with a separate water source. EGO took fourteen with the foot brace assembly. Greenworks was eight, but you're tethered to that fifty-foot extension cord.

The Anywhere Washer was under three minutes. Drop the suction line into a bucket — pool, rain barrel, the lake at my in-laws' place — clip the battery, pull the trigger. No tap, no hose bib, no infrastructure.

What It Won't Do (Honestly)

I'm not going to pretend the Anywhere Washer competes with EGO or DeWalt on raw output. It won't strip flaking deck paint. It won't blast caked-on concrete from a contractor's mixer. If you're prepping a fence for stain, get a gas unit.

It also doesn't have a turbo nozzle. The included tips cover fan, jet, and soap — fine for cars, patios, lawn furniture, bikes, kayaks, and siding within reach. Ninety percent of what a homeowner actually points a wand at.

Used it on my Tacoma in the driveway and again at my brother's lake house the same weekend. Bucket from the dock, done. — Jason R., Naperville Truck owner and weekend lake visitor
Lighter than my old corded unit and I don't trip over a hose anymore. Wish I'd grabbed the second battery from the start. — Megan T., Aurora Suburban homeowner upgrading from corded

And the runtime is what it is — figure twenty solid minutes per battery on a continuous trigger. I drained one washing the SUV and the deck stairs in a single Saturday. A second battery solves it. Worth knowing upfront.

How I Scored Each Unit

I built a five-category scorecard with my audience in mind: setup time, weight at full charge, cleaning ability on the driveway and bike, all-in cost including separately sold batteries, and storage footprint in a typical two-car garage with a workbench.

Cleaning ability is where DeWalt and EGO ran away from the field — no surprise, that's what their pressure ratings are built for. But the gap closed dramatically on lighter jobs, where the extra PSI just blew water around without working faster.

The all-in cost category shifted the rankings hard. Once you add the second battery DeWalt and EGO require for any real session, the gap to the Anywhere Washer narrows enough that the convenience factor starts doing the math.

The Test That Actually Decided It

My driveway panel test was the easy one. The deciding test was a follow-up I added on Sunday: clean my buddy Dave's pontoon at the marina, where there's no spigot within four hundred feet of the slip.

DeWalt was useless without a hose connection. EGO same. Greenworks didn't even leave the truck — no outlet. Ryobi corded? Forget it. The Mrliance has a suction option but kept losing prime in the chop coming off the lake.

The Anywhere Washer dropped its line over the gunwale, pulled lake water for forty minutes, and got Dave's deck spotless. That's when I knew which one I'd recommend to viewers who clean things away from a tap.

Why It Works Without A Hose

The unit uses a self-priming pump and a flexible suction line you drop into any water source. The intake handles bucket water, rain barrel water, pool water, lake water, even a half-full cooler if that's what's at hand.

Pressure isn't generated from incoming line pressure the way a corded unit needs — it's generated by the onboard pump pulling from whatever source you give it. That's the engineering trade-off behind the lower output and the radical portability.

  • DeWalt DCPW550B Strongest output in the cordless field. Nine pounds with battery. Needs a separate water hookup, which kills the portable use case for me.
  • EGO HPW3200 Highest peak PSI on paper. Heaviest battery, longest setup, foot brace required. Best for stationary jobs, not weekend errands around the property.
  • Anywhere Washer Lowest output, fastest setup, lightest in hand. Runs from any water source. Won the portability and convenience scoring my audience cared about most.

Battery is a standard 24V lithium pack with a charge indicator. Recharge runs about ninety minutes from empty. The unit itself weighs roughly three pounds without the battery, which is half what the DeWalt comes in at.

The chassis is plastic — not a hardened polymer like the EGO. I wouldn't drop it off a ladder. But for the way I actually carry tools around the yard, the truck bed, and the boat trailer, it's fine.

Questions Viewers Asked Me

Will it actually clean my car or is it more of a rinse than a wash?

It cleans. I did the SUV bumper-to-roof in twelve minutes with the soap nozzle and a follow-up rinse. Bug splatter came off the grille.

Can I use it for stuck-on stains like dried mud, sap, or driveway oil from my car?

Mud and sap, yes. Oil stains need a degreaser pre-treatment regardless of what unit you own. Don't expect any portable washer to fix that.

How long does the battery last in a real session, not the marketing spec?

Roughly twenty minutes of continuous trigger time. Realistically that's a car or a small patio. A spare battery is what I'd order alongside it.
