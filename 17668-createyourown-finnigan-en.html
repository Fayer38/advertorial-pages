The Markup Chain

The reason your boutique wallet costs what it costs is not the leather, the thread, or the labor itself.

It's the wholesale-to-retail jump where the storefront keeps roughly three of every four dollars you hand over.

Veterans of the trade call it the boutique tax — the silent multiplier between the bench and the shelf.

🖼️

The same wallet, two very different tags.

Once you see it, every glass case in every airport terminal looks different — and you can't unsee the spread.

And here's the part nobody at the boutique counter wants to admit out loud to a paying customer.

The longer you keep buying through that chain, the more of your money funds rent in someone else's neighborhood.

You'll spend years replacing pieces, never realizing the maker cleared barely the cost of a sit-down dinner per wallet.

So what does an artisan actually do when he finally sees the spread on his own work?

He cuts the chain — severs the wholesale accounts and ships the wallet straight from his bench to your door.

That's how the markup disappears and the price you pay starts looking like what the leather actually deserves.

But how do you walk away from boutiques that keep your shelves stocked and your bills paid?

Most makers can't — boutiques drive the volume, and quitting them means rebuilding the customer base from scratch.

So most artisans stay quiet, keep stitching, and keep clearing pocket change while their work earns somebody else's mortgage.
