I Diagnosed Arthritis In Strangers For Forty Years. I Missed My Own

A retired charge nurse on the small thing that finally worked.

By Margaret Halloran, RN (Ret.)
Charge Nurse, Orthopedics · 41 years
Published April 18, 2026

I spent forty-one years on the orthopedic floor of a Catholic hospital outside Pittsburgh, watching arthritis quietly take other people apart.

I could spot it across a waiting room. The careful grip on a purse strap. The thumb that wouldn't quite straighten.

What I could not see, apparently, was my own pair of hands. I denied what was happening for nearly two years.

I am writing this because half my old patients are still on the same prescription conveyor belt I was, and I think they deserve options.

The Morning I Sat Down On My Own Kitchen Floor

It was a Tuesday in March. I had a blood pressure pill in front of me and a child-proof cap I could not move.

I tried both hands. I tried a dish towel for grip. I tried the edge of the counter. Nothing turned.

I sat down on the linoleum and cried for about ten minutes. Then I called my daughter, who is a PA in Denver.

She did not lecture. She just said, very quietly, Mom, you are the patient now, and we both knew it was true.

That afternoon I made the rheumatology appointment I had been postponing since the previous winter. The X-rays were not subtle.

The Symptoms I Had Quietly Been Calling Other, Safer Things

Looking back through my own little logbook, the pattern was embarrassingly clear. I had charted versions of it on patients for decades.

If any of the following sound familiar, please do not do what I did and wait two years before naming them honestly.

Waking at three in the morning with knuckles that feel hot, tight, and slightly larger than they were at bedtime.

Dropping coffee mugs, pens, and car keys without warning. The grip is simply not where you left it yesterday.

A finger that catches midway through closing, then snaps shut. Trigger finger, often the ring or middle digit.

Quiet morning stiffness that takes thirty to ninety minutes of moving around before the joints feel like yours again.

Hiding your hands under the dinner table, under a napkin, in a sweater sleeve, so your spouse will not ask.

I had four of those five. I told myself it was forty years of opening IV vials with my teeth finally catching up.

Which was true, of course. But true is not the same as benign, and pretending it was bought me exactly nothing.

What The Standard Prescription Conveyor Belt Looked Like For Me

Once I finally walked into the office, the protocol unfolded the way I had seen it unfold on hundreds of charts.

I am not against any of these. I have handed them out myself. They simply did not, on their own, give me my hands back.

Daily naproxen, then meloxicam. My stomach lining objected within a month, and the swelling came back between doses.

A short course of oral prednisone. Quick relief, then a steady creep of weight, sleep loss, and rebound stiffness.

Two cortisone injections into the worst MCP joint. The second one did less than the first, as the literature predicts.

Methotrexate on the table for the next visit. I had counseled patients onto it for years. I could not bring myself to start.

An eventual fusion consult for the right thumb. The surgeon was kind. He also confirmed it was a permanent trade.

Four interventions, two years, and my hands were still waking me up at three in the morning.

Why My Daughter Reached For Compression Instead Of Another Pill

She works in a sports medicine clinic and sees compression used every day on post-op knees, shoulders, and hands.

The mechanism is unglamorous. Sustained, even pressure on inflamed joints does three quiet things at once.

  • It encourages venous return, so the fluid that pools around an inflamed knuckle overnight has somewhere to go.
  • It provides gentle warmth, which keeps the synovial fluid that lubricates each joint more mobile, less syrupy.
  • It supplies proprioceptive feedback, so your hand knows where its fingers are without having to grip everything twice as hard.
  • It restricts the tiny, repeated micro-movements that aggravate trigger finger between your morning coffee and your evening dishes.
  • And, importantly for me, it does all of this without adding one more thing to my liver to process.
