I Was Scheduled For Knee Replacement In June — Here's Why I Pushed It Back

My surgeon said bone on bone. Then I read what researchers had been quietly publishing about who actually needs surgery.

April 18, 2026 by Margaret Holloway Sponsored Content

The surgery is scheduled for June. I'm seventy-one, I live alone in The Villages, and my orthopedic surgeon at the regional hospital used the words that everyone with arthritis eventually hears — bone on bone. He slid the X-ray across the desk and we should schedule, he said.

My neighbor across the cul-de-sac had the same operation in February. She spent eleven weeks with a home health aide her Medicare supplement only partially covered, and she was back in the emergency room once already with a blood clot. I have nobody to bring meals or help me to the bathroom. That is the part the surgeon does not write down.

  • Has a surgeon already shown you your X-ray?
  • Do you live alone with no caregiver nearby?
  • Does the thought of recovery keep you awake?

So I did what every nervous patient does at two in the morning. I started reading. What I found in the peer-reviewed literature surprised me — and quietly, patiently, I built a case for asking my surgeon to wait.

How My Story Actually Started

The ache arrived the autumn I turned sixty-four, not long after my husband died. First it was the stairs, then getting up from the kneeler at First Baptist on Sunday mornings. My primary doctor called it wear and tear and sent me home with ibuprofen. By the time I was sixty-eight, a cortisone shot every four months was the only thing keeping me on my feet for church.

I stopped volunteering at the thrift shop because I could not stand long enough to sort donations. I stopped driving my grandson's two-hour route to Gainesville for his soccer games. My son called on birthdays and holidays and heard the same sentence — I'm fine, honey, just the knee. The independence my husband and I had worked forty-two years to afford was shrinking, one cancelled plan at a time, and the shame of that was worse than the pain.

The referral came in September and the consultation in October. He was a decent man with a waiting room full of patients just like me. He said bone on bone and we should schedule, and I said thank you and drove home and pulled up PubMed on the laptop my grandson set up last Thanksgiving. That is when I started learning what the billing codes actually looked like.

The 4 Types Of Knee Pain Worth Understanding

Not every bone on bone finding tells the same story. What I learned from the BMJ and the Arthritis & Rheumatology journal is that surgeons group knee pain into categories that often respond very differently to conservative care.

  • MECHANICAL WEAR ARTHRITIS The classic osteoarthritis profile — cartilage worn thin, morning stiffness, pain that improves with gentle movement but flares after a long walk on uneven ground.
  • POST INJURY DEGENERATION An old meniscus tear, a ligament sprain from fifty years ago, a fall in the driveway — the joint never quite recovered and the cartilage has been paying for it since.
  • INFLAMMATORY JOINT PAIN Swelling and heat that comes and goes in waves, sometimes worse in both knees at once, tied to the body's immune response rather than pure structural wear of the cartilage.
  • REFERRED NERVE PAIN A pinched nerve in the lower back or hip radiating down into the knee — the kind of pain a replacement surgery will never actually fix because the source sits elsewhere entirely.

What Most Patients Are Offered First

The usual menu is familiar to anyone who has sat in a pain management clinic. You start with over-the-counter anti-inflammatories, graduate to prescription naproxen, then to a series of cortisone injections spaced a few months apart. When the injections stop working you get hyaluronic acid, then maybe a round of physical therapy that Medicare will reimburse for about six weeks, and then the surgical referral that everyone saw coming from the start.

Here is what nobody on the clipboard side of the exam room will tell you clearly. A knee replacement reimburses the hospital system at a figure that dwarfs what conservative care pays out, and device-maker consulting agreements with prominent orthopedic surgeons are a matter of public record on the CMS Open Payments database. The BMJ published a review suggesting a meaningful share of replacements performed each year in the United States may not actually have been clinically appropriate.

None of that means my surgeon was wrong. It does mean the incentive structure leans one direction, and a patient like me — a widow living alone with a June surgery date — needed a non-surgical bridge that could let the joint rest while I thought more carefully.
