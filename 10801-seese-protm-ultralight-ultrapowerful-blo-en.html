On a Tuesday morning in early April, inside a converted tobacco barn off Route 49 in Cabarrus County, Walter Seese was sanding the grip of what he quietly calls the last run he will ever build.

At sixty-eight, Walter is closing the workshop his father opened in 1962. The reason is not age, nor the economy, nor a lost contract — it is his own body, and a doctor's warning he can no longer ignore. 'I built these to save other people's shoulders,' he said. 'I forgot my own.'

Three hundred and eleven units remain. A national wholesaler offered to buy the lot and resell it through big-box retailers at a comfortable markup. Walter said no. The remaining blowers will go directly to homeowners, priced near cost — a moral choice, he says, not a sale.

What follows is the story of how a 2.1-pound blower built in a North Carolina barn ended up on the recommended-tools list of physical therapists across the Southeast — and why its maker decided this run would be the last.

The Workshop That Chose Him Before He Knew It

🖼️

"Older craftsman's hands milling aluminum housing on a vintage 1978 Bridgeport mi"

Walter is quick to say he did not choose this trade. 'The barn chose me,' he says, brushing sawdust from his palm. 'I was just the one still showing up when my father couldn't.'

His father, Ellis Seese, opened the Cabarrus County workshop in 1962, machining replacement parts for local nurseries. Walter grew up underfoot, sweeping brass shavings before he could read. He took over in 1989, the year his father's hands stopped cooperating, and never rebranded the sign above the door.

'My father told me something once that I pass on to every apprentice,' Walter recalls. 'A tool is a promise. If it's heavy, you're lying to the old man who has to use it. If it breaks in three years, you're lying to his son.'

His customers are rarely young. Retired firefighters, widowed grandmothers, tree-farmers in their seventies — people who know the weight of a tool matters more than its horsepower. Some of the Seese blowers on his books are still running after twenty-two years.

I bought my Seese in the autumn of 1999, right after I retired from the fire department. Twenty-six years later, same motor, same grip, same quiet whisper on a cold morning. My grandson uses it now. It will probably outlive me too.

Then, in the spring of 2021, everything changed.

The Empty Chair, and the Work That Kept Walter Breathing

🖼️
Emplacement média — loss_section.image
"Empty wooden kitchen chair at a rustic dining table with a handwritten invoice b"

On April 14, 2021, Walter's wife Margaret — his bookkeeper, his proofreader, his harshest critic on finish quality for thirty-seven years — died of pancreatic cancer after a six-month decline. She had handwritten every invoice the workshop ever issued.

'People told me grief gets lighter,' Walter says, looking at a row of unmailed orders. 'They were wrong. It gets heavier. You just get stronger for a while. Then one day you don't.'

For six weeks he didn't answer the phone. His daughter, Janelle, drove up from Asheville every Saturday with groceries he rarely touched. His son offered to take over the books. Walter thanked him and changed the subject. Friends stopped calling.

Then, on a Thursday in early June, Janelle arrived at dawn to find the barn lights on and the lathe humming. Her father was standing in Margaret's apron, measuring a housing with a pair of Vernier calipers.

'It wasn't about the blowers anymore,' Walter admits. 'It was about having something to do with my hands that didn't remind me of her — and something to do with my hands that did. You need both, some days.'

For the next three years he worked almost every day, often from five in the morning until the light in the barn went blue. He built more blowers than in any period of his career. He took almost no new orders. The phone stayed unplugged.

By the winter of 2024, the back room held three hundred and eleven finished blowers. Each one packed in its own muslin sleeve. Each one with Margaret's handwriting on the invoice card — because Walter had kept every blank she'd ever printed, and couldn't bear to use another.

The 2.1-Pound Weight That Took Twenty Years to Perfect

🖼️

"Macro close-up of hand-polishing a brass impeller inside an aluminum leaf-blower"

To understand why a physical therapist in Charlotte has quietly been recommending the Seese Pro to her patients over sixty for almost a decade, you have to understand what Walter builds, and how.

The Seese Pro weighs 2.1 pounds — about the weight of a loaf of sourdough. Most cordless blowers on the market weigh five to eight pounds. Held at arm's length for twenty minutes, that difference stops being a spec sheet and becomes a medical event.

'I didn't shave the weight to make it pretty,' Walter says. 'I shaved it because a seventy-year-old shoulder is carrying fifty pounds of torque for every pound at the end of the shaft.'

The way he brings a motor housing down to 2.1 pounds without sacrificing airflow is not a secret, exactly. It is just not something a factory would ever do.

The housing begins as a block of 6061 aluminum. Walter skims it cold on a 1978 Bridgeport mill for about forty minutes, then tempers the shell in a kiln at 340 degrees for three hours. He hand-polishes the impeller — a brass piece the size of a drink coaster — until it sings a clean tone when flicked with a fingernail.

Start to finish, a single Seese Pro takes eleven hours of his time. Each one is initialed 'WS' on the inside of the grip plate — where no customer will ever see it.

When the impeller is true, there's a moment — you pick it up, you squeeze the trigger, and it's like the blower is apologizing for how heavy the last one was. That's the sound my father called the honest whisper. You don't forget it.

Dr. Caldwell's Verdict: 'Walter, Either You Stop, Or It Stops For You'

🖼️

"Older American man sitting on an examination table in a physical therapy clinic,"

On February 6, 2026, Walter sat in the examining room of Dr. Marissa Caldwell, DPT, a physical therapist at Carolina Sports & Spine. The MRI showed severe rotator-cuff impingement on the right, early paraspinal atrophy across the lumbar, and grip strength below the fortieth percentile for his age.

'Walter,' she told him, 'you have been lifting what amounts to a fifty-pound load at arm's length, five days a week, for fifty-five years. Either you stop building now, or in eighteen months you will not be able to button your own shirt.'

He had been hiding it for eighteen months. The numb fingers at dawn. The ice packs wrapped around his deltoid before dinner. The way he gripped the steering wheel with only his left hand on the drive home.

Janelle had noticed the new thermos of black coffee replaced by decaf. The nightly heating pad. The way he now asked her to carry the milk in from the truck.

On the drive home from the appointment, she finally said it. 'Dad. Mom wouldn't want you to build yourself into the ground. You know that.'

Walter did not answer. The line hurt not because it was unkind, but because it was true.

That night, at his kitchen table at eleven-fifteen, under the same lamp Margaret had chosen in 1987, Walter wrote two sentences on a yellow legal pad. The first: close the workshop. The second: sell the three hundred and eleven blowers directly, to the people they were built for.

Why Walter Turned Down the Wholesale Check and Chose Direct-to-Homeowner Instead

Three days after his appointment with Dr. Caldwell, a national yard-tool distributor called the workshop. They wanted the entire remaining inventory for their own brand, shipped to big-box chains with a different name riveted to the housing — and the kind of markup that department stores build into every hangtag.

Walter hung up within four minutes. 'I didn't build these for shelves,' he told his daughter that evening. 'I built them for the retired preacher across the street who can't hold a five-pound blower anymore. Send them to him.'

So Janelle built a simple website. The remaining three hundred and eleven Seese Pros will go directly to homeowners, at a fraction of what the same units sold for at regional tool shows last fall — and well below whatever a big-box chain would have charged after its cut.

When the three hundred and eleven are gone, they are gone. There will not be another run. The kiln will cool. The Bridgeport will be sold to a young machinist in Winston-Salem.

'This isn't a sale,' Walter insists. 'It's a line in the sand. These blowers were built for the shoulders of people who still do their own yard work. That's who gets them. No one else.'
