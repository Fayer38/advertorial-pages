He came running across the yard — arms wide open, that huge grin only a four-year-old can make — and I froze. Not because I didn't want to scoop him up. Every cell in my body wanted to. But I knew what would happen if I bent down and lifted him. I knew the fire that would shoot from my lower back down through my legs. I knew I'd be on the couch for three days afterward.

So I turned to my daughter and said, "Can you get him for me?"

She didn't say anything. She just picked him up and brought him over. But I saw it in her eyes — that mix of worry and sadness that told me she'd been expecting this. That she'd already started planning around the fact that her mother was falling apart.

That was the night I went home and cried for the first time in years. Not from the pain — I was used to that. From the loss.

My name is Linda Moretti. I'm 63 years old, a retired 4th-grade teacher from Columbus, Ohio. And for three years, degenerative disc disease quietly dismantled every part of the life I loved.

The Life That Slipped Away One Thing at a Time

It didn't happen all at once. That's the cruel part about chronic back pain — it doesn't arrive like a broken bone or a sudden illness. It creeps. It takes things from you one at a time, so slowly that you barely notice until you look up and realize how small your world has become.

First, it was my garden.

For twenty years, I kept a raised-bed garden in the backyard. Tomatoes, basil, zucchini, sunflowers. My students used to bring me seeds as end-of-year gifts, and I planted every single one. That garden was my sanctuary — my hands in the dirt, the sun on my neck, the smell of basil in August.

By the second year of this pain, the garden had gone to weeds. I couldn't bend to plant. I couldn't kneel to weed. I couldn't even stand long enough to water it. I'd look at it through the kitchen window and feel something close to grief.

Then it was Sunday dinners.

Our family tradition for over thirty years. Every Sunday, I'd cook a big Italian dinner — my mother's recipes, the ones I grew up with. Lasagna, meatballs, fresh bread. My kids, their spouses, the grandchildren — everyone around the table. It was the heartbeat of our family.

But standing at the stove for an hour? Unbearable. Lifting a cast-iron pot of sauce? Out of the question. I tried sitting on a stool, but it wasn't the same. Eventually I stopped hosting. My daughter took over — and God bless her for it — but sitting at someone else's table eating someone else's version of my mother's recipe felt like losing a piece of who I am.

Warning: Degenerative disc disease is progressive. Unlike a pulled muscle or a temporary strain, DDD does not improve on its own. The discs in your spine continue to lose hydration and height over time, which means the longer you go without addressing the root cause, the worse the condition becomes — and the fewer options remain available to you.

And then there was Nicky's birthday. The moment I realized I wasn't just living with pain anymore. I was living around it. Every decision, every plan, every family gathering — filtered through the question: "Will my back let me do this?"

That's not living. That's managing a surrender.

I Spent $9,000 Trying to Fix My Back — Here's What I Got

I am not someone who gives up easily. I taught 4th graders for 34 years — trust me, I know how to be persistent.

So when the pain started, I did everything right. I went to doctors. I followed every referral. I showed up for every appointment.

Over two years, I saw three different chiropractors. Twice a week at first, then once a week, then "maintenance visits" that never seemed to maintain anything. Each session gave me maybe a day or two of relief — just enough to make me think it was working, just short enough to bring me back the following week.

Then came physical therapy. Twelve weeks of exercises, stretches, core work. My therapist was wonderful — kind, knowledgeable, genuinely trying. But the relief was always temporary. I'd feel better for 48 hours, then wake up on the third day right back where I started.

The total? Over $9,000 in two years. Chiropractors, PT sessions, copays, supplements, ergonomic pillows, a special mattress topper, a lumbar support chair. Thousands of dollars and hundreds of hours spent sitting in waiting rooms, driving across town, and hoping this time would be the one that stuck.

It never stuck.

I also tried two different TENS units — those little buzzing devices you see advertised everywhere. The first one felt like a tickle that did nothing. The second was stronger but the effect disappeared the moment I turned it off. Temporary relief on top of temporary relief. I was spending money to rent comfort by the hour.

And then, at my last orthopedic consult, the surgeon said the words I had been dreading for three years:

"Linda, I think it's time we talk about surgical options."

He said it gently. He explained the procedure — a spinal fusion, L4-L5. He told me the recovery would be 3 to 6 months. He mentioned the risks. He was honest. And I appreciated that.

But all I heard was: they're going to cut open my spine.

I drove home that day in silence. I didn't even turn on the radio. I just kept thinking — how did I get here? How did a little back pain turn into someone wanting to fuse my vertebrae together?

Like doing physical therapy exercises — but more precisely, more consistently, and without the strain of actually moving.
