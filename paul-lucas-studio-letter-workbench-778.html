It's Tuesday, a little past seven. I'm at my bench, coffee going cold, writing to you instead of drinking it.

The shop smells like the hide I opened at six — vegetable-tanned cowhide, warm and grassy, the way a saddle smells.

In front of me is a half-finished billfold in English Bridle, chestnut, with a spool of forest-green thread waiting beside it.

That wallet belongs to a man in Montana named Jack. He ordered it for his father's seventieth birthday.

Jack wrote three sentences. Chestnut leather. Forest-green thread. His father taught him to fish on the Gallatin.

That's all I had to work with, and honestly, that's all I needed. The rest was already in the choices.

Chestnut is a warm, honest brown — the color of river rocks in low sun. It ages into something deeper every year.

Forest green is quieter than you'd think. Against chestnut it almost disappears, then catches the light and reminds you it's there.

Jack didn't pick a feature from a list. He picked a memory, and asked me to put it in his father's pocket.

Most Men Tell Me The Same Thing About Their Old Wallet

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s2
"Worn nylon wallet next to cracked cheap leather wallet on wooden surface, end-of"

Before they write to me, they've usually been carrying the same tired billfold for a decade, maybe longer than that.

When I ask what's wrong with it, the answers are almost always a version of one of these five things.

The stitching blew out somewhere around year three and they've been living with a loose card pocket ever since.

The leather cracked along the fold, dried out from a hundred back-pockets worth of sitting, and nothing can bring it back.

It's bonded leather — which is really just ground-up scraps glued to fabric — and the surface has started peeling off.

It was a gift from somebody they've lost, and they've been patching it with tape because they can't let it go.

It works fine, technically. But they look at it every morning and feel a little embarrassed pulling it out.

I understand all five. I've carried wallets I was embarrassed by. I've taped a wallet rather than replace it.

This letter is really for the man who's ready to stop patching, and wants the next one to be the last one.

I Spent Years Making Wallets I Wasn't Really Proud Of

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s3
"Pile of discarded wallets in different materials and brands on a wooden table, p"

When I started out, I thought I had to compete on the things everyone else competes on, and I lost every one.

Here's what I tried, and why none of it held up to my own standards after a few years in the trade.

I tried cheaper leather from overseas — chrome-tanned splits that looked fine new and turned to cardboard inside a year.

I tried machine stitching to keep up with orders, and watched threads unravel the second a customer snagged one on a button.

I tried simplifying the lineup to one color, one thread, one size, thinking choice overwhelmed people. It just bored them.

I tried lifetime guarantees on work I wasn't sure of, and spent more time on repairs than I did on new pieces.

I tried matching prices with factory brands, cutting corners I could see, and I couldn't look at the finished wallet afterward.

So I stopped competing on their terms. I went back to American hides, hand stitching, and letting people choose their own wallet.

What It Actually Takes To Make One Of These Wallets

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s4
"Craftsman saddle-stitching leather by hand with two needles and waxed thread, cl"

A Finnigan wallet takes about three hours of my time, not counting the hide selection and the cutting that morning.

I'll walk you through it the way I'd show a friend who wandered into the shop and asked what I was up to.

  • I pick the hide by hand, from a shelf of full-grain American cowhide, looking for a section with even grain and character.
  • I cut the panels with a straight edge and a fresh blade — no clicker dies, so every piece gets my eyes on it.
  • I skive the edges down so the fold sits flat in your pocket, not like a hinge fighting to stay closed.
  • I saddle-stitch the seams by hand with two needles and waxed thread — the one stitch that won't unravel if it breaks.
  • I burnish the edges with beeswax and a wooden slicker until they're smooth enough that a thumb runs over them without catching.

Why The Stitching Is The Part I'll Stand Behind Forever

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s10
"Craftsman burnishing wallet edges with wooden slicker at workbench, photorealist"

Most wallets fail at the stitching first, not the leather. A machine stitch is one continuous thread looped through itself.

If it breaks anywhere, the whole seam can unravel like a pulled sweater. That's why cheap wallets fall apart all at once.

A saddle stitch is two threads locking against each other at every hole. Cut one and the rest still hold.

Why I Can Offer A Real Lifetime Guarantee Without Hedging

🖼️
Emplacement média — media.s11
"Finished wallet resting on wooden bench with order card specifying customer name"

When I say lifetime, I mean mine — for as long as I'm at this bench, I'll repair a Finnigan that comes back.

That's not a marketing line. It's a statement about the math, and here's how the math actually works out in practice.

  • The stitching is hand-done with a thread that's rated to outlast the leather. I'd have to be badly wrong about the thread.
  • The leather is full-grain, not coated or corrected, so it ages instead of flaking. There's no plastic layer to peel.
  • The edges are burnished, not painted. Paint chips off. A burnished edge just gets smoother with years of handling.
  • I make about ten of these a week. A handful of repairs a year fits inside a life I can actually live.

So yes — if the stitching ever lets go, send it back. I'll fix it at my bench and mail it home.
