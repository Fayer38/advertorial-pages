The October Sunday I Almost Dropped My Grandson — And What Finally Quieted My Burning Feet

A Tucson grandmother's honest account of finding relief after fourteen years

By Margaret Holloway
Retired school librarian, Tucson
Published April 19, 2026

My name is Margaret, I am sixty-two, and for three years my feet have been deciding how my days go.

I kept a pair of cotton socks in the freezer because the cold was the only thing that quieted the burning at night.

I stopped going to the pool where my walking group meets. I stopped gripping my coffee mug with my right hand.

Then one Sunday in October, my grandson jumped into my arms and I almost dropped him. That was the day everything changed.

What Fourteen Years Of Managing My Diabetes Never Warned Me About

I have lived with Type 2 diabetes since I was forty-eight. Metformin, careful eating, daily walks — I did everything the doctor asked.

Nobody ever told me the real cost of those fourteen careful years would show up first in my feet, then my fingertips.

The burning started small. A warm patch under my heel some evenings. Then pins and needles while watching television with my husband.

By last summer, I was sleeping in three-hour blocks. The clock read 2:14, 2:47, 3:12 — always the same story.

My husband Frank slept through it. I was alone with my feet at two in the morning, and nobody believed how bad it had become.

The Symptoms I Was Quietly Hiding From Everyone I Loved

I did not tell my daughter. I did not tell my friends at church. I certainly did not tell Jackson when he asked why Nana was tired.

If you are reading this, you probably know the list by heart because you are hiding it too.

The burning under the blankets that makes you kick your feet out from the sheets at two in the morning, every single night.

The tingling in your fingertips when you try to button a blouse, as if your hands belong to someone else entirely.

Dropping a coffee mug because your grip simply let go, then pretending to your husband it was clumsiness rather than numbness.

Turning down the swimming pool, the grandkids' splash park, the hiking trail — anywhere a single misstep could end in a broken hip.

Lying awake and wondering whether this is how your own mother's last years began, and whether you are next in line.

No, you are not imagining it. No, it is not simply aging. And no, you are not being dramatic about any of this.

What is happening inside your feet and hands has a name, and it has been progressing quietly for years.

Why Every Answer My Doctor Offered Kept Leaving Me More Afraid

My primary care physician was kind. She listened, she ordered the monofilament test, and then she reached for her prescription pad.

The two names she offered — gabapentin and Cymbalta — are medications I have watched friends take, and I did not want either.

Gabapentin — my neighbor Carol took it for a year and spent most afternoons sleeping in her recliner, foggy and forgetful.

Cymbalta — a medication that touches serotonin, and which a friend of mine could not come off for almost two years.

Capsaicin cream — the pharmacist's suggestion that burned worse than the neuropathy itself and stained every pair of cotton socks I own.

Epsom salt foot soaks every evening, which felt lovely for twenty minutes and then left me exactly where I started by bedtime.

Tighter blood sugar control — I was already doing this, and my feet kept burning regardless of what the meter said each morning.

None of it addressed what was actually happening to the tiny nerves running through my feet. I needed a different kind of answer.

What My Daughter-in-Law The Dietitian Finally Explained About These Tiny Nerves

Rebecca works at the hospital in Phoenix. That Sunday night, after Jackson had gone home, she sat at my kitchen table with a printout.

She told me — plainly, without medical jargon — what fourteen years of elevated blood sugar had actually been doing inside my feet.

  • Oxidative stress — she called it rust. Years of excess sugar leave the small nerves coated in tiny damage, like rust on a pipe.
  • Glycation — sugar molecules bonding to protein along the nerves, making them stiff, misshapen, and slower at carrying signals to the brain.
  • Demyelination — the protective coating around each nerve fiber wears thin, and the burning and tingling are the signal leaking through.
  • Poor blood flow to the tiniest capillaries in the feet, starving the nerves of oxygen and the repair tools they need.
  • B-vitamin depletion — metformin itself, the medication keeping my diabetes in check, had been quietly lowering my B12 for over a decade.
