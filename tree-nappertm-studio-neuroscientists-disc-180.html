If you wake up tired despite sleeping seven or eight hours, you are not alone. Researchers believe the issue is not sleep duration — but how your nervous system behaves overnight.

Dr. Megan Liu, a neuroscientist at a Boston university, has spent years studying why certain people never reach restorative deep sleep. Her findings suggest the issue is physiological, not psychological.

  • You fall asleep quickly but wake up feeling unrested
  • You experience racing thoughts or tension at bedtime
  • You rely on caffeine to function after a full night of sleep
  • You have dismissed poor sleep as a normal part of aging or stress

These are not signs of laziness or poor habits. According to Dr. Liu, they point to a nervous system locked in sympathetic dominance — fight-or-flight mode — even during sleep.

Left unaddressed, chronic sympathetic activation is linked to elevated cortisol, impaired immune function, cognitive decline, and long-term cardiovascular risk.

Most people who sleep poorly have normalized it, blaming their workload, age, or screen habits. They rarely consider that their autonomic nervous system may be physically preventing deep sleep.

Dr. Liu's work suggests that the solution is not sedation or behavioral modification. It involves directly shifting the nervous system from sympathetic to parasympathetic dominance — a measurable, physiological change.

The Science

Your autonomic nervous system has two modes. The sympathetic branch governs alertness, stress response, and cortisol production. The parasympathetic branch governs rest, digestion, and recovery.

In a healthy cycle, your body shifts to parasympathetic dominance as evening approaches. Cortisol drops. Serotonin converts to melatonin. Deep sleep follows naturally.

Dr. Liu's research shows that for millions of adults — especially those in demanding professions — this shift never occurs. Cortisol stays elevated and deep sleep stages are shortened.

This explains why you can sleep eight hours and still feel depleted. Your body was never in the physiological state required for restoration. You were unconscious but not recovering.

Why the Most Common Sleep Approaches Often Fail to Solve This Problem

Melatonin supplements address the endpoint but not the upstream cause. If your body still produces excess cortisol at bedtime, supplemental melatonin fights an uphill battle against your stress hormones.

Sleep hygiene helps at the behavioral level but does not shift the autonomic nervous system. The missing piece, according to Dr. Liu, is a direct physical input triggering parasympathetic activation.
