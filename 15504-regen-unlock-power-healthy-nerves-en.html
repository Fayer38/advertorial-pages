I Compared Five Nerve Supplements Against Each Other. One Genuinely Worked.

A Reporter's Methodology

Eight months, an independent lab, two test subjects, and a scoring rubric that refused to grade on a curve.

By By Laura Whitfield, Independent Health Journalist Updated April 2026

My father called me last October, six years into diabetic neuropathy, to tell me his neurologist at the Duluth clinic had finally written him a gabapentin prescription. He was 82, a retired electrician, and he did not want to take it.

He had read the side effects out loud to my mother the night before. Dizziness. Confusion. A warning about falls in older adults. For a man who still climbed the basement stairs three times a day, those words landed harder than the neuropathy itself had ever landed.

He asked me, as he sometimes does when something feels medical and unfamiliar, whether there was anything else worth trying first. I write about health for a living. I had covered supplements before. I told him I would look into it honestly, not as his daughter, but as a reporter.

What I meant was that I would compare the top-rated nerve supplements on the American market the way I would compare anything else: with a methodology, a lab, a control, and a scoring rubric I built before I bought a single bottle. No affiliate links. No free samples.

That was in late October. It is now April. Over the course of eight months I ran five supplements through staggered eight-week trials on myself and my father, logged daily symptom scores in a shared spreadsheet, and paid an independent lab in Illinois to verify the ingredient forms on each label.

One of the five, a product called Regen+, outperformed the other four on almost every axis that I cared about. I want to be clear that it is not perfect. The pill count is high. The bottle is not cheap. Availability was briefly an issue in February.

But when I laid out the symptom charts, the lab reports, and the rubric side by side, there was no honest way to rank anything else ahead of it. My father has been on it for twelve weeks now, and he has not filled the gabapentin prescription.

What follows is how I ran the test, what I found in the lab, how each contender scored, and why the winner won. I am not linking to Amazon. I am not being paid. I am telling you what a reporter who spent eight months on this concluded.

How I Built the Test

Before I bought anything, I wrote the rubric. Nine categories, scored one to ten: ingredient form quality, dosage versus clinical literature, label transparency, third-party testing, pill count and adherence burden, onset of symptom relief, sustained relief at week eight, side-effect profile, and value at the shelf.

I weighted ingredient form heaviest. The difference between cyanocobalamin and methylcobalamin, or between thiamine and benfotiamine, is the difference between a supplement that can cross into nerve tissue and one that largely cannot. This is not marketing copy. It is the peer-reviewed literature talking.

I picked the five contenders by searching the top Amazon results, Labdoor write-ups, and a pair of ConsumerLab summaries my editor had bookmarked. I paid full retail for each bottle from the brand's own site when possible. No review units. No discount codes from PR firms.

The Rubric

Nine weighted categories scored blind, including a lab verification step for the two most important ingredient forms. Daily symptom logs on a zero-to-ten scale, two subjects, eight weeks each, no overlap between trials to keep the washout period clean.

My father agreed to the protocol on one condition. He wanted to keep seeing his neurologist at the Duluth clinic throughout, and he wanted to be able to stop at any time without explanation. I said yes to both. A trial without an exit ramp is not a fair trial.

I took the same supplements he took, at the same doses, on the same days. I am 58 and I do not have neuropathy, but I figured a second data point on tolerability, digestion, and side effects was better than none. It turned out to matter more than I expected.

The washout period between each supplement was two weeks. During washout, my father tracked baseline symptoms only, no other interventions. His neurologist kept him on his standard diabetes management throughout, which meant his blood sugar stayed in the same range the whole study.

Symptom scoring happened every evening at roughly the same time, on a zero-to-ten scale, across four dimensions he defined himself: burning in the feet, tingling in the hands, numbness on walking, and night-time cramping. He did not see my running totals until each eight-week block ended.

That last detail mattered. I did not want his expectations shaping his numbers. He wrote his scores in a notebook; I transcribed them into the spreadsheet weekly. The lab work I commissioned was run blind, with bottles relabeled as samples A through E before shipping.

The Lab Work, and Why It Changed Everything

The independent lab I used, a small analytical chemistry outfit near Chicago my editor had worked with on a food-additive story two years ago, charges by the assay. I paid for two tests per bottle: ingredient identity verification and active-form confirmation, focused on the B-vitamin complex and alpha-lipoic acid.

I chose those two because that is where the bodies are buried in nerve supplements. A label can legally say vitamin B12 while containing the cheapest, least bioavailable form. A label can say alpha-lipoic acid and contain the racemic mixture rather than the stabilized R-isomer that the clinical trials used.

The results came back in early February, and they reordered my rubric in real time. Two of the five supplements, both with premium price tags and polished packaging, contained cyanocobalamin rather than methylcobalamin. Both disclosed this accurately on the label, but neither front-loaded the fact the way a buyer would want.

A third supplement claimed R-lipoic acid on the marketing page but contained standard alpha-lipoic acid in the capsule, with the R-isomer listed as a minor component. This is technically legal if the label is correct. The label was correct. The marketing page was, generously, incomplete.

Regen+ came back clean. Methylcobalamin in the active form, benfotiamine rather than plain thiamine, and R-alpha-lipoic acid at a dose within the range used by the German diabetic neuropathy trials from the early 2000s. Everything the lab found matched everything the label claimed, ingredient by ingredient.

I want to be careful here. Label accuracy is not the same as efficacy. A supplement can have perfect ingredient forms and still do nothing. What the lab work told me was which bottles were worth taking seriously enough to run an eight-week trial on, and which were essentially expensive B-complex tablets.

Two of the five fell into the second category for me at this stage. I still ran them on my father, because the protocol required it and because I wanted the symptom data. But my confidence in them going in was low enough that I documented it before the trial started.

The other three, Regen+ included, had ingredient forms that matched the clinical literature on peripheral neuropathy. That did not make them equal. It made them eligible. From there, the tie-breakers were dosage, adherence burden, and what my father's symptom scores actually looked like at week eight.

Meet the Five Contenders I Tested

The five products I tested were, in the order I tried them: a well-advertised Amazon bestseller I will call Contender A, a practitioner-brand formula marketed through chiropractic offices called Contender B, a direct-to-consumer subscription product called Contender C, a budget option from a national vitamin chain called Contender D, and Regen+.

I am using letter labels for four of them on purpose. This is not a takedown piece. Each brand I tested is legitimate in the sense that they exist, ship a product, and have real customers who are happy. The question I was answering was narrower: which one should my father take.

Contender A had the slickest packaging and the most aggressive claims, including a before-and-after sequence on the landing page that would not survive a close FTC read. The capsule itself was a reasonable B-complex with a light dusting of alpha-lipoic acid, cyanocobalamin-based, under-dosed for the target condition.

Contender B was the one recommended by my father's podiatrist. It scored well on label transparency and contained methylcobalamin, which surprised me. The dose of alpha-lipoic acid was on the lower end of the clinical range, and the pill count was the highest of the five, at six capsules a day.

Contender C, the subscription product, leaned heavily on herbal adjuncts rather than the B-vitamin and alpha-lipoic acid stack. That is a legitimate approach, and I kept an open mind. In practice, both my father and I saw the smallest symptom-score movement of the five on this one.

Contender D, the budget bottle from the vitamin chain, was the hardest one to rank. It was cheap, the dosages were low, and the ingredient forms were second-tier across the board. But on the adherence axis it was the easiest to take, and a few of the symptom scores did move.

What My Father's Symptom Scores Showed

I am going to walk through the numbers plainly. I am not a statistician, and I am not presenting this as a clinical trial. It is an n-of-one diary on my father, with a second n-of-one on me for tolerability, and the limitations of that design should be obvious to any honest reader.

At baseline, before any supplement, my father's four-dimension average was 6.8 on a zero-to-ten scale, with the burning-in-the-feet measure running highest at 8. That is consistent with what he had been telling his neurologist for the last two years, and with what the gabapentin prescription was responding to.

On Contender A, eight weeks in, his average had moved to 6.3. Most of that came from a small improvement in the tingling-in-the-hands score. The burning score, the one that kept him up at night, did not meaningfully change. He finished the bottle, took the washout, and moved on without enthusiasm.

Contender B moved him to 5.9. The night cramps in particular eased, which he noticed first, and which he told me about before I showed him the spreadsheet. He attributed it, probably correctly, to the methylcobalamin. The six-capsule daily burden was the reason he asked me not to put that one in regular rotation.

Contender C, the herbal formula, moved him to 6.6. Within the noise. I am not willing to call that a response. Contender D, the budget bottle, moved him to 6.2. That was a surprise on the upside, and I want to credit the product for it even though it did not make the final cut.

Regen+ was different. By week four his average had moved to 5.4. By week eight it was at 4.1, and the burning-in-the-feet number had come down from 8 to 5. The night cramps were gone. He slept through, which he had not done reliably in two years.

I want to note one thing clearly. At week twelve, after this review was drafted, his numbers continued to improve rather than plateau. His last average, a week ago, was 3.6. Whether that continues, I do not know. I am not going to extrapolate past the data I have.

Why Regen+ Won on Ingredient Form

The winning hand Regen+ held, and it was clear before my father took a single capsule, was that every ingredient on the label was in the form the peer-reviewed literature actually used. That sounds obvious. It is not. Three of the five contenders failed this test on at least one active ingredient.

The methylcobalamin question was the most important one. This is the active coenzyme form of vitamin B12, and the form the body can use directly without conversion. For a diabetic patient in his eighties, whose conversion capacity is reduced, taking cyanocobalamin is not wrong, exactly. It is just slower and less reliable.

Form Matters

Methylcobalamin, benfotiamine, and R-alpha-lipoic acid are the forms the nerve-health literature actually studied. Cheaper substitutes on the label are a yellow flag, not because they are inert, but because the clinical data does not quite apply to them.

Benfotiamine is the fat-soluble analog of thiamine, and it is the form used in the German clinical trials on diabetic neuropathy that the whole category effectively rests on. Plain thiamine, water-soluble, does not cross into nerve tissue in the same concentrations. Regen+ used benfotiamine. Three of the other four did not.

R-alpha-lipoic acid is the stereoisomer the body produces endogenously and the one the German trials used at 600 milligrams per day. Standard alpha-lipoic acid is a racemic mixture, half R and half S. The S-isomer is not harmful, but it is not what the research was done on.

Regen+ included the R-isomer at a dose within the clinical range, bundled with the methylcobalamin and benfotiamine, plus a smaller supporting cast of N-acetyl-L-carnitine, vitamin D, and B6 in its active P-5-P form. None of the supporting ingredients were at trivial doses. None were obviously there for marketing.

I want to be honest about what I did not love. The capsule count on Regen+ is four per day, taken with food, split morning and evening. That is a lot. It is less than Contender B's six, but it is more than my father wanted to manage without a weekly pill organizer.

The bottle is also not inexpensive, and I understand why a shopper on a fixed income might hesitate. Compared to the other premium contenders on the shelf, the formulation justifies the positioning. Compared to a budget B-complex, it does not. The question is which benchmark matters, and for nerve pain the answer is the first.

The last thing I want to flag is availability. In early February the product was briefly out of stock on the brand's own site, and I had to wait about ten days for my father's next bottle to ship. That is not unusual for a smaller brand. It is worth knowing before you commit.

Those three criticisms, pill count, price, and a brief stock-out, are the full list of things I can honestly say are wrong with Regen+. If the list were longer, I would have ranked something else first. It is not longer. That is what eight months of testing told me.

What the Scientists Actually Say About These Ingredients

I am going to spend a few paragraphs on the science, because the audience I am writing for reads carefully. If you are not interested in the clinical background, skip ahead. The short version is that three ingredients have serious peer-reviewed support for peripheral neuropathy, and Regen+ contains all three at reasonable doses.

The first is alpha-lipoic acid, specifically in its R-form. Multiple randomized controlled trials, most prominently the ALADIN series out of Germany in the late 1990s, showed statistically meaningful improvement in symptoms of diabetic peripheral neuropathy at a dose of 600 milligrams per day over eight to sixteen weeks.

Three Pillars

Alpha-lipoic acid at clinical doses, benfotiamine as a fat-soluble thiamine derivative, and methylcobalamin as active B12. Every credible nerve-health formula I have looked at is built on some combination of these three, plus supporting B-complex.

The second is benfotiamine. It was developed and studied heavily in Germany for diabetic complications, and the peer-reviewed data on symptom improvement is meaningful, though smaller in scope than the alpha-lipoic acid literature. The fat-soluble profile is what makes it effective at doses that water-soluble thiamine cannot practically replicate.

The third is methylcobalamin. This is where the Japanese and Korean clinical literature is stronger than the American, and where some of the best symptom-improvement data in peripheral neuropathy actually lives. The doses in the trials are typically higher than what a standard multivitamin delivers, which is part of why specialty formulations exist.

When I counsel patients considering nerve-support supplements, I tell them to focus on ingredient form and clinically studied doses. Methylcobalamin, benfotiamine, and R-alpha-lipoic acid are the evidence-backed trio. Brands that substitute cheaper forms are not delivering the same product the research actually tested. The Four Studies That Shaped My Scoring

I read more than four studies reporting this piece, but four ended up doing most of the work in how I weighted the rubric. I am summarizing them in plain language rather than reproducing the statistics, because the audience here is not a journal club.

Every one of these studies is publicly searchable, and every one has its limitations. I have tried to describe them honestly, including what they did not show. If any of this matters to your own decision, please read the abstracts directly rather than taking my summary as the last word.

Nothing in this section is a claim about Regen+ specifically. It is a summary of the literature on the three core ingredients. What Regen+ does is deliver those ingredients in the forms and approximate doses the studies used, which makes the studies relevant to the product decision.

Study 01
The ALADIN Trial on Alpha-Lipoic Acid

Published in Diabetologia in 1995, the first ALADIN trial randomized 328 diabetic patients with peripheral neuropathy to alpha-lipoic acid or placebo across three weeks of intravenous therapy. The treatment arm showed statistically meaningful improvement in the Total Symptom Score, including burning, paresthesia, and numbness.

The follow-up ALADIN II and III trials extended the work to oral alpha-lipoic acid and longer durations. The 600 milligram daily oral dose emerged as the benchmark, with symptom improvement generally observed over an eight-to-sixteen-week window. Longer-term benefit persisted in the open-label extensions.

The trials had real limitations. They were mostly short, mostly German, and mostly funded in part by the company that manufactured the active ingredient. But the effect sizes were not small, and the later independent replications held up reasonably well. The 600 milligram number is not an invention.

Diabetologia, 1995; follow-up trials 1999-2006

Study 02
Benfotiamine in Diabetic Neuropathy Complications

A 2005 German trial, BENDIP, randomized diabetic patients with peripheral neuropathy to benfotiamine or placebo over six weeks. The active group showed meaningful improvement in neuropathy symptom scores, with the benefit most pronounced in pain and burning measures. The adverse-event profile was essentially indistinguishable from placebo.

Earlier mechanistic work from the same research group had shown that benfotiamine blocks three of the four major biochemical pathways implicated in hyperglycemic nerve damage. That mechanistic story is part of why the ingredient held up in clinical trials and why it shows up in serious nerve formulations.

The limitation here is that the benfotiamine literature is smaller than the alpha-lipoic acid literature and is concentrated in a handful of European research groups. It is not as replicated. What is there, however, points consistently in the same direction, and the safety profile is strong.

BENDIP trial and related mechanistic work

Study 03
Methylcobalamin and Nerve Fiber Density

Japanese and Chinese clinical work over the last fifteen years has shown that high-dose methylcobalamin can reduce symptoms of peripheral neuropathy and, in some studies, improve nerve conduction measures. The doses used in these trials are typically well above what a conventional multivitamin provides, which is part of why formulation matters.

One smaller trial specifically looked at intraepidermal nerve fiber density as an endpoint, and reported a modest increase over months of treatment in the active arm. That is a more demanding endpoint than symptom scores, and the fact that it moved at all is part of why the ingredient has a strong reputation.

The limitations are the usual ones. The trials were smaller, mostly single-center, and in patient populations that do not perfectly mirror the American clinical picture. But the direction of effect is consistent, and the safety record of methylcobalamin over decades of use is excellent.

Journal of Neurology, multiple Japanese trials

Study 04
Combination Formulas Versus Single Ingredient Monotherapy

A smaller body of literature has looked at combination formulas that include methylcobalamin, benfotiamine, and alpha-lipoic acid together. The combined formulas generally outperform any one ingredient alone on symptom scores, which is consistent with the mechanistic story that the three ingredients target different parts of the nerve-damage cascade.

This is part of why I was skeptical of contenders that relied heavily on herbal adjuncts rather than the core trio. The herbal ingredients are not necessarily inert, but the clinical evidence for the combined B-complex plus lipoic acid approach is meaningfully stronger than the evidence for any single herb I looked at.

The takeaway from this literature, for me as a reviewer, was that a well-formulated combination product at clinical doses of the three core ingredients should outperform a less-complete formula. That prediction is exactly what my father's symptom scores ended up showing across the five contenders I tested.

Pooled analyses, European clinical literature

I am not cherry-picking the studies. There is negative and null literature out there, mostly on underpowered single-ingredient trials, and I read that too. On balance, the weight of the evidence points toward a well-formulated combination product with the three core ingredients as the most evidence-supported nerve-supplement approach available without a prescription.

How Regen+ Actually Made My Father Feel

The charts and the lab work told me which product to rank first. They did not tell me what it felt like to live on it for eight weeks. That information came from my father in short phone calls most Sunday evenings, and from his notebook, which I still have sitting on my desk.

The first thing he noticed, in week three, was that he could put his socks on in the morning without sitting down. I did not ask him about that. He volunteered it. For two years he had been sitting on the edge of his bed to pull his socks over feet that felt wooden.

The second thing, also in week three, was that the burning woke him up less. He was not sleeping well before the trial, even on the nights his blood sugar was in range. By week four he told me, slightly embarrassed, that he had slept straight through three nights in a row.

In week five he drove himself to the hardware store in Duluth for the first time in a while. He had been letting my mother do the errands when the neuropathy was bad, because the pedals and his feet no longer communicated. He did not make a big deal of it.

My mother did. She called me that Sunday, before he could, and told me the drive happened and he had looked like himself at the kitchen table afterward. She is not prone to overstatement, and I wrote that phone call into my notes because it was a better data point than any score.

In week seven he tried to help my uncle with a small electrical project at the cabin. He had been a working electrician for forty-three years, and he had stopped doing hands-on projects the year the tingling in his fingers got noticeable. He did not finish the job. He did, however, start it.

By week eight, the symptom average on his chart had moved from 6.8 to 4.1. That is a meaningful change, but the number understates it. What had actually changed was that he was engaging with his life again instead of arranging it around what his feet and hands could and could not tolerate.

I am trying to be careful not to overstate this. He is 82 years old. He still has diabetes. His neuropathy is not cured, and I do not think any supplement cures neuropathy. What he had was a meaningful reduction in symptoms that let him take back a handful of small daily freedoms.

Those small daily freedoms are, honestly, what the audience for this review is looking for. Nobody writing to a journalist about nerve pain is asking for a miracle. They are asking whether there is something, shy of a prescription that scares them, that will let them sleep and drive and tie their own shoes.

The answer, based on my father's eight weeks on Regen+, plus the four weeks he is now on it, is that there appears to be. I am not promising the same result for anyone else. I am telling you what I saw in my own house, with my father, on my spreadsheet.
