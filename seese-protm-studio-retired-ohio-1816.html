THAT WAS HIS DRIVEWAY. HIS JOB. AND SOMEONE ELSE WAS DOING IT.

It happens so gradually you almost don't notice. One Saturday you skip the driveway because your shoulder's acting up. The next week your back says no. Before long, somebody else is doing the job that used to be yours.

Nobody announces it. There's no single morning where a man decides he's done taking care of his own property. It just drifts away from him, one small concession at a time, until the routine belongs to someone else.

For a lot of men over sixty-five, that quiet shift is one of the hardest things about getting older. Not the pain itself, but what the pain takes away. The sense that your home is still yours to maintain.

The Morning Gene Watched From the Kitchen Window

Gene is seventy-one. He lives in a three-bedroom ranch in Dayton, Ohio, the same house he and Linda have owned for thirty-two years. He spent decades maintaining every inch of it. The gutters, the driveway, the garage floor.

After his second cortisone shot last spring, things changed. His shoulder wouldn't cooperate with the old gas blower anymore. The thing weighed close to twelve pounds, and even starting the pull cord had become a negotiation with his body.

Linda started quietly hiring the neighbor's teenage son to blow off the driveway and patio every other week. She didn't mention it. Gene noticed the kid out there but didn't say anything either.

HE PICKED IT UP WITH ONE HAND AND ALMOST LAUGHED. IT WEIGHED NOTHING.

Then one morning he stood at the kitchen window with his coffee and watched the boy clear the front walk. Something broke inside him. Not anger, exactly. More like a door closing. That was his driveway. His routine. And he was watching it from inside his own house.

He told himself it was fine. Plenty of people hire out yard work. But Gene had never been one of those people, and both he and Linda knew it.
