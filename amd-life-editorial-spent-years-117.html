The Cycle Nobody Talks About — But Millions Are Trapped In

My name is Linda Carpenter. I'm 61 years old, I live in Knoxville, Tennessee, and I'm a retired ER nurse.

I tell you that because I want you to understand something: I'm not someone who falls for gimmicks. I spent 28 years in medicine. I know how the body works. I know what's backed by evidence and what's nonsense.

And yet, for over a decade, I was completely lost when it came to my own back.

It started in my late 40s. A dull ache in my lower back after long shifts. Nothing dramatic. My doctor said it was degenerative disc disease — basically, normal wear and tear. "Welcome to your fifties," he joked.

But it didn't stay dull. Within two years, the pain had spread down my left leg. Sciatica. Some mornings I couldn't put on my own socks. I'd stand at the kitchen counter gripping the edge, waiting for a spasm to pass, pretending everything was fine when my husband walked in.

Warning: Chronic lower back pain affects over 65 million Americans. It is the single leading cause of disability worldwide — and most people cycle through treatments for years without finding lasting relief.

The worst part wasn't the pain itself. It was what it stole from me. I stopped hiking — something my husband and I had done every weekend for 20 years. I stopped volunteering at the animal shelter because I couldn't bend down to clean kennels. I stopped picking up my grandkids.

I was 52, and I was living like I was 85.

Everything I Tried — And Why None of It Lasted

Over the next decade, I tried everything. I'm not exaggerating. I kept a spreadsheet.

Physical therapy — three different clinics, twice a week for months. It helped while I was going. The second I stopped, the pain came right back. At $75 per session after insurance, I was spending over $600 a month for temporary relief.

Prescription painkillers — they dulled the pain but turned me into a zombie. I was foggy at work, snapping at my husband, sleeping 10 hours and still waking up exhausted. I hated who I was on those pills.

Cortisone injections — two rounds. The first one gave me three good weeks. The second one did almost nothing. My doctor said we'd "maxed out" what injections could do.

Chiropractic adjustments — I felt great walking out of the office. By the next morning, I was right back where I started.

A $4,000 ergonomic mattress — my husband agreed to it because he was desperate to help. It helped my shoulders. Not my back.

And then came the conversation I'd been dreading. My orthopedist sat me down and said the words: "Linda, I think it's time we talk about surgery."

Spinal fusion. A $80,000 to $150,000 procedure with a 6-month recovery, a 20-40% chance of "failed back surgery syndrome," and no guarantee I'd ever feel normal again.

I went home that night and cried in the shower so my husband wouldn't hear.

What My 28 Years in Medicine Never Taught Me About Back Pain

When the device arrived, the nurse in me took over. Before I tried it, I researched for three days straight.

Here's what I learned — and honestly, it made me angry that no one had explained this to me in 12 years of treatment:

Most chronic lower back pain treatments focus on managing symptoms. Pills mask the pain signal. Injections reduce inflammation temporarily. Even physical therapy, as valuable as it is, often strengthens the muscles around the problem without addressing the compression at the source.

Think of it like this: if someone is standing on your foot, you can take all the ibuprofen in the world. Your foot will still hurt — because the pressure is still there.

That's what's happening to your spine. Years of gravity, sitting, poor posture, and natural disc degeneration compress your vertebrae together. The discs between them get squeezed. Nerves get pinched. And your body screams at you every single day.

Spinal decompression — the kind that used to only be available in clinical settings at $150-$300 per session — works by gently creating space between those compressed vertebrae. It allows the discs to rehydrate, takes pressure off the nerves, and gives your body the room it needs to actually heal.
