My Uncle Wade Has Hated Every Gadget I've Brought Home — Until This One

He drank his Shiner, watched the dust vanish, and asked for one

By Mark Halloran, software engineer, Austin

My Uncle Wade is 68 years old and still wears the same Resistol he bought when Reagan was in office. He has run cattle outside Lampasas since 1978. He has never, in my entire life, asked me where I bought anything.

He still cuts firewood with his father's Stihl 028. He still sharpens his own chains. When I show up with anything that has a power button or a Bluetooth pairing screen, he watches like a man waiting for it to break.

That is the part you need to understand before I tell you what happened on his porch last month. Wade does not endorse things. Wade does not ask after things. Wade lets a tool prove itself, and most of them never do.

I drove out from Austin on a Saturday with my Tacoma caked in caliche dust from the ranch road. The barn well-pump was on a separate line that day. So I tried something I'd ordered a few weeks earlier — and Wade watched.

Wade Pulled A Chair Onto The Porch

He carried his coffee out, set it on the railing, and crossed his arms the way he does when he is about to be disappointed by something. He didn't say anything. He never says anything. That's how you know he's paying attention.

I had a five-gallon bucket of rainwater Wade had collected off the gutter that spring. I dropped the intake hose straight into the bucket. No hose bib. No long extension cord trailing across the dirt. No water hookup, no faucet, no fuss.

Wade tilted his head. Not approval — just registering that something unusual was about to happen. The Resistol cast a shadow over his eyes. He took a slow swallow of coffee. He has watched a lot of city gadgets fail on that porch.

I pulled the trigger on the wand. Ninety seconds later the dust and the bug splatter and the dried-on whatever from the cattle guard were just gone. The Tacoma's quarter panel went from chalk-white to truck-red while Wade stood there silent.

He didn't say anything for a while. He walked back inside. I figured I had failed some test I didn't know I was taking. I kept working on the front fender, not looking at the porch, the way you don't watch a hand you've folded.

He Came Back Out With Two Beers

Wade came back through the screen door with two cold Shiners. He set one on the porch railing next to me. He didn't hand it to me. That's a whole different gesture. The bottle on the railing means stay a minute. We have something to talk about.

He looked at the Tacoma. He looked at the bucket of rainwater that was now a little muddier than when we started. He looked at the wand in my hand. Then he said the longest thing he had said to me all weekend.

"Get me one of those for the barn." That was it. Eight words. From a man who once watched my dad's snowblower seize up in 1994 and only said, "Figured." Eight words is a parade in Wade's hand.

I want to be clear about what kind of man this is. He owns three trucks, none made this century. He has fired more chainsaws than most people have used phones. He thinks Bluetooth is what happens when you don't floss.

And here he was, on his own porch, asking me — the nephew who broke his John Deere mower in 2009 — to please get him one of these things. I drove home that night thinking about it. Then I ordered him one.

Wait — I should back up. I should tell you what this thing actually is, because if you only know it the way Wade knows it, you'll think it's a magic trick. It isn't. It's just an honest piece of gear that finally got designed right.

What Mark Brought Out Of The Truck

It's called the Seese Pro™ Anywhere Washer. The name is a mouthful and the marketing copy on the website is a little corny, and Wade would hate both of those things, so I am just going to tell you what it does.

It's a cordless pressure washer that pulls water from anywhere — a bucket, a rain barrel, a stock tank, a kiddie pool you forgot to drain. No spigot. No hose bib. No fifty feet of garden hose dragged across the gravel.

You drop the intake into the water source, point the wand, pull the trigger. That's the whole show. It runs on a battery that charges off any standard outlet. The whole rig is light enough to carry in one hand.

That last part is the part Wade noticed. He has bad shoulders from forty years of round-bale work and he is not going to fight a corded unit across a gravel drive. He picked it up off the porch step with two fingers and grunted.

A grunt from Wade is a five-star review. A grunt and a question is a standing ovation. He turned it over once, looked at the trigger, looked at the intake hose, and said, "Where's the gas tank?" When I told him there wasn't one, he just nodded.

What He Used It For The Next Weekend

I shipped Wade's unit to the ranch and drove out two weekends later to check on him. The dogs ran out to meet me. The Resistol came around the corner of the barn behind them. He didn't say hello. He just nodded toward the barn door.

The barn floor was clean. I don't mean tidy. I mean clean — the concrete was the actual color of concrete again. Forty-six years of cattle work, hay dust, oil drips, and Lampasas red dirt, and the floor was the color it had been when his daddy poured it.

He had run it off the stock tank. He told me he hadn't even bothered moving the cattle out — just stepped over the rail, dunked the intake, and went to work on the back wall where the calving pens get rough every spring.

"Used it on the trailer too," he said, walking me out back. The cattle trailer was sitting in the sun looking newer than it had any right to. "Used it on the lawn chairs. Used it on the dog." He paused. "Don't tell your aunt about the dog."

That was the closest thing to a joke Wade had told me since the funeral in 2017. I told him I would not tell my aunt about the dog. He nodded once. We drank a Shiner each. The sun went down over the live oaks.

Before I left, he handed me a piece of feed-store receipt with three names on it. Three of his neighbors. Roy at the place down the road. Doyle who runs the auction barn. His brother-in-law Carl. "Order them one apiece," he said. "I'll pay you back."

Why This Particular Tool Made The Cut

I have brought a lot of things out to that ranch over the years. A heated jacket. A laser-guided level. A weather app. Wade was polite about all of them, in his way, which is to say he never threw any of them at me.

None of them came home with him. None of them got a name on a receipt. The difference with this one is hard to explain unless you have spent time with the man, but I'll try. The Seese Pro doesn't ask anything of you.

It doesn't ask for a hookup. It doesn't ask for an extension cord. It doesn't ask for a neighbor's spigot or a hundred feet of hose snaking across the yard. It doesn't ask you to pair anything to your phone. It just works on whatever water you happen to have.

That is a Wade kind of design. Wade's chainsaw is from 1979 and still starts on the third pull because it doesn't have anything on it that doesn't belong. He respects that in a tool. He respects when somebody figured out what to leave off.

And he respects when a thing does the work it claims to do. The Tacoma went from caliche to clean in ninety seconds, on a bucket of rainwater, on a porch in Lampasas, in front of a man who has watched a lot of things fail. That counted.

What I tell them is the truth. I tell them my Uncle Wade, who has hated everything I have ever shown him, asked for one of these by name. I tell them I order it directly from the brand's website and it comes in a few days.

I tell them the company gives you a thirty-day window to send it back if you change your mind. I have not heard of anybody sending one back. The people I know who own one have, instead, ordered a second one for the in-laws.

The Five Things Wade Actually Cared About

I asked him later, at the kitchen table, what made him want one. He thought about it for a minute. Then he laid it out the way he lays out anything — short, in the order it mattered, no extra words. Here is the list, more or less verbatim.

No hose. Don't have to drag one across the yard.

Pulls water from a bucket. Or the stock tank. Either.

Light. I can carry it one-handed without the shoulder complaining.

No gas. No fumes. Battery charges off any standard outlet.

No app. No screen. No buttons asking me to update.

My version of the suspicion was watching Wade. Wade has been burned by trendy tools more times than I have. If a thing is junk, he'll know in thirty seconds, and his face will do the talking. His face didn't do any talking on that porch.

He went back inside for the beer. That was the tell. Wade only goes back inside for the beer when something has earned itself a sit-down. The Tacoma earned itself a sit-down. The Seese Pro earned itself a sit-down. That's about as far as Wade ever goes.

You don't get more than that from him. You won't get a quote you can frame. You won't get a five-star write-up. You'll get a Shiner on the porch railing and an unprompted request that he wants one for the barn, and that is the entire ceremony.
