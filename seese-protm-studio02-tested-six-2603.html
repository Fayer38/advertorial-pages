I Tested Six Cordless Pressure Washers — Here's What Won

Six units, two weekends, one driveway, and one very muddy mountain bike.

By Mark Ferraro, Naperville, Illinois

A subscriber emailed me three weeks ago asking the same question I'd been getting for months: which cordless pressure washer is actually worth buying? Not the rented-it-once review. The one you'd recommend to your brother-in-law.

So I did what any reasonable person with a YouTube channel and a credit card would do. I ordered six units, cleared a Saturday, and lined them up on the driveway behind my garage in Naperville.

The lineup: DeWalt's DCPW550B, EGO's HPW3200, the Greenworks GPW1800HYB, a budget Mrliance unit, the corded Ryobi as a benchmark, and the Seese Pro Anywhere Washer my brother-in-law kept texting me about.

Same dirty driveway panel. Same muddy mountain bike. Same stopwatch. I weighed each one with the battery installed and tracked the all-in cost, including the batteries DeWalt and EGO conveniently sell separately.

The Test Setup, Honestly

🖼️

"Close-up of a stopwatch and clipboard beside a DeWalt cordless pressure washer i"

I'm not a lab. I'm a guy with a stopwatch, a kitchen scale, and a driveway that hasn't been cleaned since the polar vortex. That's actually the point. Most reviewers test in conditions you'll never replicate at home.

My scoring sheet had four columns: setup time from box-to-spray, weight with battery, cleaning power on the driveway panel, and the metric my audience always cares about most — total real-world cost once you add the battery and charger.

That last column matters more than people admit. The DeWalt DCPW550B is a great unit, but it's a bare tool. By the time you add a 20V Max battery and charger, you're in a different price bracket than the box suggests.

Same story with EGO's HPW3200. Beautiful machine, real power, and a serious bill once you've added the 56V battery it needs to actually run. The Greenworks ships with its battery, which I appreciated.

I also gave each unit the same honest disclosure I'd want from a reviewer: none of these will replace a gas-powered Honda for stripping paint or cleaning a long fence line. We're comparing weekend tools, not professional gear.

How the Big Names Performed

🖼️
Emplacement média — media_3.url
"DeWalt and EGO cordless pressure washers side by side on a wooden workbench with"

The DeWalt DCPW550B was the heavyweight, literally. Strong PSI, premium build, and the kind of trigger response you'd expect from the brand. But at almost nine pounds with the 20V Max battery, my wrist felt it after fifteen minutes on the bike.

EGO's HPW3200 was the most impressive on the driveway panel. Real cleaning power, the kind that lifts ground-in tire rubber. It's also the heaviest in the group with the 56V battery, and the all-in cost made me wince.

The Greenworks GPW1800HYB is the corded-cordless hybrid, which is a clever idea on paper. In practice, I kept tripping over the cord I'd plugged in for backup. Decent unit, just couldn't decide what it wanted to be.

The Mrliance was the budget play. Cheap, plasticky, and the trigger felt like it was held together with hope. It worked. It's not going to last three summers in a Midwest garage that hits forty below in January.

The corded Ryobi was my benchmark, not a contender. It out-cleaned everything, as it should — it's plugged into the wall. But I had to drag a hose and an extension cord around the side of the house, which is exactly the chore I was trying to avoid.

So those were the heavy hitters. Strong on raw output, weak on the thing my audience scored highest in their replies: can I just grab it and go without setting up a job site first?

The Quiet Surprise In The Lineup

🖼️

"Cordless portable pressure washer with intake hose dropped into a five-gallon bu"

The Seese Pro Anywhere Washer is the one my brother-in-law wouldn't stop texting about. He'd bought it for his fishing boat and ended up using it on his deck, his bikes, his dog, and apparently the bottom of his lawnmower.

On paper, it shouldn't have made the cut. The PSI rating is lower than the DeWalt and the EGO. It's not going to strip paint. It's not going to replace a gas unit if you're cleaning a quarter-mile of concrete.

But here's what it does that none of the others do: it doesn't need a hose, a tap, or a water connection. You drop the intake into a bucket, a five-gallon pail, a cooler, the pool, a rain barrel — anything holding water — and you spray.

That changed everything for the way I actually use a pressure washer. My driveway spigot is on the wrong side of the house. Getting water to the bike I'd dragged off the trail in Palos meant a fifty-foot hose and a kink that always happens at minute four.

With the Anywhere Washer, I filled a bucket from the laundry sink, walked it out, and was spraying in under a minute. Setup time was the lowest of any unit in the group, by a wide margin.

Where The Anywhere Washer Falls Short

🖼️
Emplacement média — media_5.url
"Comparison shot of a portable pressure washer beside a heavy-duty DeWalt unit on"

I promised my audience I'd be honest about the trade-offs, so here they are. The Anywhere Washer is not the most powerful unit in this group. If you're looking at caked-on industrial grime, you'll want the EGO or a gas unit.

The flow rate is lower than the DeWalt, which means cleaning a full driveway takes longer per square foot. I cleaned my two-car driveway panel with it, but I wouldn't want to do the whole driveway in one session.

It also runs off a bucket, which sounds obvious until you realize you'll empty a five-gallon pail faster than you'd expect. For longer jobs, you'll want a bigger reservoir or a second bucket queued up.

The build quality is solid but it's not DeWalt-tier. The trigger has a plastic feel rather than the contoured grip on the EGO. It's clearly built to a price point that lets it stay accessible, and you can feel that choice when you pick it up.

And one more thing worth saying out loud: it's not sold at Home Depot or Lowe's, which threw me at first. You're buying it directly from the brand, which means no in-store warranty desk if something breaks. They do offer a 30-day return.

None of these are deal-breakers for the job most of my subscribers actually do — washing a car, rinsing a bike, cleaning patio furniture, hosing off a kayak. They're real if you were planning to clean the side of a barn.

The Scorecard, By The Numbers

After two weekends, the scoring sheet looked like this. EGO won on raw cleaning power. DeWalt won on build quality. The corded Ryobi won on uninterrupted output, as expected. The Mrliance won on price alone.

The Anywhere Washer won on the three columns my audience weighted highest in their replies: setup time, weight, and water-source flexibility. It was the lightest unit in the lineup with a comfortable one-handed grip.

On total cost of ownership — the bare-tool versus battery-included math — it landed in the middle of the pack. Not the cheapest, not close to the EGO once you've added a 56V battery and charger from the same brand.

And on the question my brother-in-law actually asked me — which one will I actually use, instead of leaving in the garage — the answer was unambiguous. The lighter, no-hookup unit got picked up four times more often during the test window.

That's the metric that matters in a tool you're buying for weekend chores. The best pressure washer is the one you'll actually pull out when the bike comes back caked, not the one with the impressive spec sheet that lives behind the snowblower.

If you have a spigot ten feet from where you work, a long fence line, or a contractor-grade job ahead of you, the EGO HPW3200 or DeWalt DCPW550B make sense. They're real tools and they earn their keep.

If you're like most of my audience — weekend cleaning, no convenient water hookup, want to grab it and go — the Anywhere Washer is the unit I'd recommend. It's the one I kept reaching for, even after the test ended.

That's the test I think most reviewers miss. Power matters until it doesn't. Convenience is what determines whether a tool earns its place in the garage or becomes another piece of gear collecting dust beside the snowblower.

He ordered one that night, directly from the brand. The 30-day return window made the decision easy — he figured if it didn't earn its place by Memorial Day weekend, he'd send it back. Two weeks in, he hasn't.
