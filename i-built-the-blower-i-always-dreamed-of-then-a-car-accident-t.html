I Built the Blower I Always Dreamed Of. Then a Car Accident Took Everything From Me.

By Editorial Team | March 31, 2026

I never thought I'd be writing this letter.Six months ago, I was the proudest man in Tampa, Florida. I had a small company, a warehouse full of the best cordless blowers ever made, and a future that finally looked bright after years of engineering and sacrifice.Today, I'm writing to you from a hospital bed. My left leg is held together by titanium rods. My company is bankrupt. And my warehouse? Almost empty.But before you feel sorry for me — please don't. Because what happened to me might just be the best thing that ever happened to you

.Let me explain.

LIFESTYLE

An older man grimacing while holding a heavy gas blower in a messy yard, showing physical strain and frustration, autumn leaves everywhere

My name is Albert, and for 31 years I designed turbine systems for Boeing. I know how air moves. I know how to compress tremendous force into impossibly small spaces. It was my life's work.When I retired, I thought I'd spend my weekends enjoying my yard. Instead, I spent them suffering.Every Saturday morning, the same routine. Drag the 23-pound gas blower out of the garage. Yank the starter cord. Feel my lower back scream after ten minutes. Spend Sunday on the couch with an ice pack, wondering why yard work had become a punishment.I'm 64 years old. My back has been telling me to stop for years. But the leaves don't care about your back, do they?So I asked myself the question that changed everything: Why does a leaf blower need to weigh more than a bowling ball?

Warning: Over 47,000 Americans suffer back injuries each year from heavy yard equipment. Gas blowers averaging 15–25 lbs are one of the leading culprits — especially for adults over 55.

I looked at every blower on the market. Every single one made the same trade-off: if you wanted power, you carried the weight. If you wanted something light, you got a toy that couldn't move a wet napkin.Nobody had solved this. So I decided I would.

PRODUCT

Workshop bench with multiple blower prototypes in various stages, engineering sketches pinned to the wall, the final Seese Pro gleaming among the rough prototypes

I spent my entire retirement savings. Fourteen months. Forty-seven prototypes. My wife thought I'd lost my mind — and honestly, some mornings I wondered too.But here's what I knew that the big blower companies didn't care about: the motor technology in most blowers is from the 1970s.

Brushed motors. Heavy. Inefficient. They generate heat, friction, and wear out in two seasons.Meanwhile, drones fly at 60 mph using brushless motors that weigh practically nothing. Tesla's engines use the same principle. So why wasn't anyone putting this technology into a leaf blower?I'll tell you why. Because the big brands make more money selling you a new $300 blower every three years. They have no incentive to build something that lasts.I did.After 47 failed prototypes, number 48 worked. A brushless turbine motor spinning at 30,000 RPM, packed into a housing that weighs just 2.1 pounds

— lighter than a bag of sugar.But here's the part that even surprised me: it pushes air at 200 MPH

with 1,200 CFM

. Those are numbers that rival professional gas blowers costing $300 or more.I called it the Seese Pro. And for a brief, beautiful moment, everything was going right.

PRODUCT

A warehouse with neatly stacked Seese Pro boxes, some shelves already empty, a bittersweet atmosphere of quality products being let go, warm overhead lighting

Orders started coming in. Real people — folks like you — were discovering that a 2.1-pound blower could actually replace the monster in their garage. I was shipping units every day, reading thank-you emails from customers with bad shoulders, arthritic hands, and aching backs.Then, on October 14th, a delivery truck ran a red light on Highway 41.I don't remember the impact. I woke up three days later in Tampa General with a shattered femur, four broken ribs, and a stack of medical bills that would make your eyes water.I couldn't walk. I couldn't work. I couldn't ship a single blower.Within eight weeks, my small company went bankrupt. My health insurance covered a fraction of the surgeries. The hospital bills kept arriving — $14,000 here, $8,500 there. Collection notices. Phone calls at dinner time.So I made a painful decision.I'm liquidating my remaining inventory of Seese Pro blowers at 55% below retail price.

Every unit that sells is a hospital bill that gets paid. Every order keeps my family one step further from losing our home. I'm not telling you this for sympathy — I'm telling you because I want you to understand why this price exists, and why it won't last.This isn't a marketing gimmick. This is a man selling his life's work to survive.
