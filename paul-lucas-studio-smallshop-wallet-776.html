EVERY WALLET IS SOMEBODY'S ANSWER TO A SPECIFIC QUESTION.

It's a Tuesday morning. My coffee has gone cold on the bench, the way it always does. In front of me is a half-finished English Bridle billfold and a note with a man's name on it — Jack, from Montana.

Jack wrote me last week. His father turns seventy next month, and he wanted chestnut leather with forest-green thread, because those were his father's colors growing up. I cut the hide for that wallet at six this morning.

I'm not going to sell you anything in this letter. I want to walk you through what happens here, between the hide I opened at dawn and the box that will leave the shop on Friday. Then you can decide.

What I Opened At Six This Morning

The hide I rolled out before sunrise was English Bridle — vegetable-tanned, stuffed with tallow and waxes the old way. It smells like a barn and a library had a long, slow conversation. That smell is the first honest thing about a wallet.

Most wallets in the world are made from leather that was finished in a rush, coated in plastic so it looks uniform on a shelf. What I work with is full-grain American cowhide, the top layer of the hide, where the grain and the character still live.

I buy the hides from two tanneries I've walked through personally. One in Pennsylvania, one in Chicago. I know the men who run the drums. I know what's in the liquor they tan with. That matters to me, and eventually it matters to the wallet.

SIX LEATHERS. NINE THREADS. FIFTY-FOUR CONVERSATIONS, NOT FEATURES.

A piece of Glazed Harness leather doesn't look the same on Tuesday as it will in four years. It darkens where your hand touches it most. The corners round off. It starts to carry the shape of your pocket instead of fighting it.

That's the kind of material you can build a thirty-year wallet from. It's also the kind of material that tells on you if you cut a corner. There's nowhere to hide a lazy stitch in a hide like this. That's part of why I love it.

Jack's Order, And Why It Took Me An Hour

Jack picked chestnut leather with forest-green thread for a reason. His father worked as a forest ranger outside Missoula for thirty-one years. The green is his uniform. The chestnut is the color of the barn his father rebuilt in 1978.

When Jack placed the order, he wrote three sentences in the notes field. I read them twice before I cut anything. That's not a process I invented — it's just what you do when somebody trusts you with a seventieth birthday.

People ask me why I offer fifty-four color combinations instead of three or four safe ones. The honest answer is that every wallet I've ever made was somebody's answer to a specific question. I'd rather have the right answer than an easy catalog.

The Thread Matters More Than People Think

The stitching on a wallet isn't decoration. It's what's holding the thing together every single day for the rest of your life. I use waxed polyester thread, saddle-stitched by hand with two needles, one pass at a time.

A machine stitch uses one thread. When it breaks anywhere, it unzips the whole seam. A hand-sewn saddle stitch uses two threads locking around each other — if one ever fails, the other holds. That's why I can put a lifetime guarantee on my stitching without hedging.

The Four-Year Photos My Customers Send Me

A few times a month, I'll open an email with a photo attached. It's usually a wallet I made three or four years ago, sitting on somebody's kitchen table, and a short note — something like, "thought you'd want to see how it's aging."

I save every one of them. There's a folder on my desktop called Patinas, and it has hundreds of them now. The chestnut ones go the color of an old penny. The black ones develop a soft bloom. Every one is different from the next.

That folder is the closest thing I have to a proof-of-work. I didn't pay for those photos. Nobody asked the customer to send them. They sent them because the object earned a place in their life, and they wanted the maker to know.

Why I Can Offer A Lifetime Guarantee

The lifetime guarantee on my stitching isn't a marketing line. It's math. I know exactly what thread I used, exactly how many stitches per inch, and exactly which awl made the hole. If anything I sewed fails, I fix it.

I can offer that because I'm the one who made it. There's no factory between us, no line worker who was having a rough Thursday. If your wallet needs repair in 2036, you'll send it back to this same bench, and I'll probably still be sitting at it.

The leather itself will do what leather does — it'll age, soften, darken. That's not a defect, that's the feature. But the stitching I put in it will outlive the both of us, and if it doesn't, I owe you a repair. Simple as that.

The Ones That Get Passed Down

I've had three customers tell me their Paul Lucas wallet was given to them by a father who had worn it for a decade. One of them, a man in Ohio, sent me his dad's original order slip, folded into quarters. I still have it.

That's not something I set out to build. When I started this shop I just wanted to make a wallet I wasn't embarrassed to put my name on. The inheritance piece came from the customers, not from me. They decided the object was worth passing down.

  • The Leather Full-grain American cowhide from tanneries I've walked. Not split, not corrected, not coated in plastic to look uniform.
  • The Stitching Hand saddle-stitched with two needles and waxed polyester thread. One stitch can fail without the seam unraveling.
  • The Maker One man, one bench, one wallet at a time. When you write with a question, the person who made it writes back.

I think about that a lot on mornings like this one. Jack's father will open a wallet next month, and maybe in 2056 somebody will open it again and wonder about the chestnut leather and the forest-green stitching, and the forest ranger from Missoula.

You don't set out to make heirlooms. You try to make one honest thing, and if you do it often enough and you don't cut corners, the object decides for itself whether it wants to become one. Some of them do. Some of them are just wallets. Both are fine.

Questions I Get Asked The Most

How long does it take to make one wallet once I order?

Usually around two weeks. I cut, skive, stitch and burnish each one myself. I don't hold inventory of custom combinations — your wallet starts as a flat hide.

What if I pick a leather and thread combination that doesn't look right?

Write me before you order and I'll tell you honestly. I've seen all fifty-four combinations. A few of them I'd talk you out of if you asked me.

Does the lifetime guarantee cover normal wear on the leather itself?

It covers the stitching and my workmanship, not how you age the leather. Leather is supposed to wear in. That's the part you're buying.

Before You Go

I won't tell you this is the best wallet in America. I don't know every wallet in America. I know the one I'm making for Jack this morning, and I know the hide I'll open tomorrow, and that's about the limit of what I can honestly claim.

What I can tell you is that every Finnigan that leaves this shop was cut, stitched and boxed by the man whose name is on it. If that matters to you, we'll probably get along. If it doesn't, there are faster wallets out there.

The customizer on the site will walk you through the six leathers and the nine threads. Take your time with it. Pick the combination that means something to you, or to the person you're thinking of. I'll do the rest from here.
