I Blamed Work Stress for Seven Years of Terrible Sleep Until a Neuroscientist Intervened

New research into deep pressure stimulation and the autonomic nervous system is quietly changing how clinicians treat chronic sleeplessness

April 10, 2026 by Rachel Simmons Sponsored

For seven years, I told myself the same thing every morning: this is just what happens when you work nights. I would come home from a twelve-hour shift in the emergency department, fall into bed completely drained, and then lie there wide awake — heart pounding, mind racing, watching the ceiling fan turn while daylight crept through the blinds.

It was not the kind of tired that coffee fixes. It was a heaviness behind my eyes and inside my bones, making every thought feel like it moved through wet sand. I started forgetting things at work — small details at first, then patient names. My reactions slowed. I snapped at my husband over nothing. At forty-one, I felt like I was borrowing energy from a body that had stopped recovering.

  • Exhausted but unable to fall asleep
  • Waking at 3 a.m. with racing thoughts
  • Brain fog and memory slips at work

I thought this was my problem alone. It was not. Researchers now estimate that tens of millions of working adults are caught in the same pattern — chronically underslept, running on cortisol, and convinced that exhaustion is just the price of a demanding life.

How It Started for Me

My name is Dana. I am forty-one and I work rotating shifts in a Cleveland emergency room. The sleep trouble started in my mid-thirties, after my second child and the permanent night rotation. At first it was just difficulty falling asleep after a late shift. Within two years, I could not sleep soundly on any schedule — not on my days off, not even when I was so exhausted I could barely keep my eyes open.

The worst part was not the tiredness itself — it was what the tiredness took from me. I stopped reading to my kids at night because I could not focus on the words. I canceled plans with friends because being social felt impossible. My husband and I sat in silence most evenings, both too drained to talk. I saw my doctor twice. Both times she said the same thing: reduce your stress, improve your sleep hygiene, maybe try a low-dose sleep aid.

I tried the sleep aid. It helped me fall asleep but the mornings were worse — groggy and foggy, like waking up underwater. That was when I started reading about what actually happens inside the nervous system during sleep. And that research led me to a name I had never encountered: Dr. Megan Liu, a behavioral neuroscientist in Boston whose work was beginning to change how clinicians think about chronic insomnia.

Four Patterns Behind Chronic Sleeplessness

Dr. Liu's research identifies four distinct neurological patterns that drive chronic poor sleep. Most people experience at least two simultaneously, which is why single-target interventions like melatonin supplements or sleep hygiene adjustments so often fail.

  • HIGH CORTISOL INSOMNIA Elevated cortisol keeps the sympathetic nervous system locked in a state of alert. The body physically cannot transition into deep sleep stages while cortisol remains high, regardless of how exhausted you feel.
  • SYMPATHETIC NERVOUS OVERDRIVE The fight-or-flight branch of the autonomic nervous system stays dominant even at rest. Heart rate remains elevated, breathing stays shallow, and the body never receives the signal that it is safe to sleep deeply.
  • SEROTONIN PATHWAY DISRUPTION Serotonin is the precursor to melatonin. When serotonin production is suppressed by chronic stress, the body cannot produce adequate melatonin naturally, no matter how dark or quiet the bedroom.
  • THERMAL REGULATION FAILURE Core body temperature must drop to initiate and sustain deep sleep. When thermoregulation is disrupted by stress, hormones, or bedding that traps heat, the body cycles back to lighter sleep stages repeatedly.

What Most People Try First

The standard advice is familiar to anyone who has searched for sleep help. Melatonin supplements. Prescription sleep aids like zolpidem. Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia. Blue light glasses. Weighted blankets filled with glass beads or plastic pellets. White noise machines. Magnesium. Lavender sprays. Most people I know, myself included, have tried some combination of these — spending money and time on solutions that address symptoms while leaving the underlying nervous system pattern completely untouched.

Prescription sleep aids carried side effects I could not afford in my line of work — next-day drowsiness is dangerous when you are making clinical decisions in an emergency room. Over-the-counter melatonin helped for a week, then stopped working. Traditional weighted blankets made me sweat through the sheets within an hour, which woke me up and defeated the entire purpose. Therapy helped me understand my stress but did nothing to change what was happening at the nervous system level while I slept.

I was running out of options when Dr. James Hartwell, a sleep clinician in Chicago, published a clinical commentary that caught my attention. He described a different approach entirely — one that worked not by adding chemicals to the body, but by changing how the nervous system itself behaved at rest.
