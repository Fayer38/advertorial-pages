I'm Carol, 66, a retired bookkeeper from Greensboro, North Carolina. I haven't worn open-toed shoes since 2019. Last month, Dale and I were supposed to take a Florida cruise — I told him no the night before.

The thought of that pool deck made me genuinely nauseous. Forty years of marriage, and I couldn't tell my own husband the real reason. He thought it was money. It wasn't money. It was my feet.

Two big toenails, yellow and thick as a quarter, the smaller ones starting to crumble at the edges. I'd tried Vicks. I'd tried tea tree oil. I'd tried vinegar soaks for an entire summer. Nothing changed.

My Sister Came Up For Easter

My older sister Pat — Patricia, 71 — drove up from Wilmington for Easter weekend. She spent 41 years on the geriatric floor at Moses Cone Hospital before she retired in 2020. She doesn't miss much.

Saturday night I was changing socks in the guest room. The door was cracked. She walked past, glanced down at my feet for half a second, and kept walking. She didn't say a word until Sunday morning.

We were drinking coffee at the kitchen table. Dale was in the den watching golf. Pat set her cup down and said, plainly, "Carol, how long have you been hiding those?" I started crying. Forty years of being her little sister.

The doctors didn't have anything. We did.

She didn't lecture me. She didn't tell me I should have seen a podiatrist years ago. She just asked what I'd tried, listened to the whole list, and nodded the way she used to nod at families when there was nothing more to say.

Then she said something I'll never forget: "The doctors don't have anything for this. We did." She meant the nurses. She meant the little brown bottle every floor kept in the staff drawer for the patients the doctors had given up on.

Week By Week, What I Actually Saw

Weeks one through three: nothing visible. The nail looked the same. I had to talk myself into continuing. Pat had warned me about this — she said the first month was psychological, not physical, and to keep brushing.

Weeks four through eight: a clear band of new growth at the base, maybe two millimeters wide. The yellowing started just above it. The line between old nail and new nail became something I could actually see.

Weeks nine through twelve: the thickening on my big toes started flattening. The crumbling at the edges of the smaller nails stopped. I clipped a healthy edge for the first time in I don't remember how long.

Why This Works When Other Things Didn't

Pat explained it the way she'd explained things to families for forty years. The fungus lives under the nail plate, in the bed itself. Most things you brush on simply can't get through that hard top layer.

Undecylenic acid is small enough and oil-soluble enough to actually pass through the keratin and reach the bed where the trouble is. That's why the FDA recognizes it as a topical antifungal. It isn't new. It's just been quietly effective for decades.

Vicks doesn't penetrate. Tea tree oil evaporates before it can do anything. Vinegar softens the surface and that's the end of it. None of those reaches what Pat called the actual address of the problem. They were treating the wrong floor.
