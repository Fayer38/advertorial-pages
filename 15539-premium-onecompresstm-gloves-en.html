My name is Margaret. I worked forty-one years as a charge nurse on an orthopedic floor outside Scranton.

I retired four years ago. I can still diagnose arthritis in a stranger from across a crowded waiting room.

  • Hands that wake you at three in the morning — knuckles hot, fingers curled, the sheet feeling like sandpaper against the skin.
  • Coffee mugs slipping from a grip that used to carry two patients at a time down a quiet hospital corridor.
  • A blood pressure cap you cannot twist, a jar of pickles you hand to your husband without saying why.
  • Trigger finger that locks at the worst possible moment, or a post-surgical stiffness no one warned you would linger.

If any of that sounds familiar, read on. I wrote this for the women I used to discharge home.

The worst morning came in February. I sat on my kitchen floor unable to open my own blood pressure medication, and I cried.

Then I called my daughter Julia. She is a physician assistant in Denver, and she listened without lecturing me.

What I Knew

Arthritis is not really about the bone. It is about the synovium, the soft lining that cushions every joint.

When that lining inflames, fluid pools. The joint swells. Circulation slows. The tissue starves quietly, and cartilage starts wearing away.

I watched this cascade in other people's hands for four decades. I charted it, medicated it, recommended splints and ice.

Then I went home, soaked my own hands in warm water, and told myself it was weather, overuse, stress, aging.

The hard part no one in the clinic tends to mention out loud

Untreated inflammation does not stall. It remodels the joint. Cartilage does not grow back, and fusion surgery eventually enters the conversation.

My rheumatologist put methotrexate on the table, then a fusion consult. I understood every word. I still wanted another door.

What my daughter, fresh out of training, quietly understood before I did

The honest problem with most compression gloves I had seen before

Hospital-issue compression is often rigid, itchy, and sized for men with larger hands. Patients stop wearing them by day three.

I owned two pairs from the drugstore. One bunched at the thumb. The other cut off the circulation it promised to help.

What quietly changes in the first two to three weeks

I kept a small nurse's logbook, the way I always have. These are the three shifts I noted the most.

  • Morning stiffness dropped from roughly an hour of uselessness down to fifteen minutes of mild tightness I could work through.
  • Grip strength returned enough to carry a full grocery bag from the car without switching hands three times down the drive.
  • The three a.m. knuckle fire, the thing no one outside that hour understands, quieted. I slept through the night by week two.

Sustained compression is not a cure. I want to be careful here. My cartilage loss from decades of lifting is permanent.

But inflammation is the engine of further damage, and reducing it slowed the downhill slope I had been quietly watching in my hands.

My rheumatologist, told about my experiment at the next visit, did not roll her eyes. She wrote it in the chart.
