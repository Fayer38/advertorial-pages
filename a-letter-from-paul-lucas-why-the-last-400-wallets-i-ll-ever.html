A Letter From Paul Lucas: Why the Last 400 Wallets I'll Ever Make Are Going Direct

I'm 67. My hands are giving out. I turned down a department store deal worth more than my workshop is worth. Here's why — and why I'd rather sell these to you.

Dear Friend,

Dear friend,

I've never written one of these before, so bear with me. My name is Paul Lucas. I'm 67 years old, and I've been cutting and stitching leather in the same 900-square-foot workshop in Weatherford, Texas, for most of my adult life. My grandfather bought the building in 1948. My father worked the bench until he was 70. I figured I'd do the same.

Three years ago, my hands started telling me a different story.

It started in the mornings. I'd wake up and my fingers wouldn't close around the awl the way they used to. By 6 a.m. I'd be running hot water over my knuckles in the kitchen sink, trying to get them limber enough to thread a needle. Linda — that's my wife of 41 years — she noticed before I did. She'd watch me from the doorway, sipping her coffee, not saying a word. She knew.

The doctor in Fort Worth gave it a name. Rheumatoid arthritis. Same thing that took my father off the bench at 70. He told me I had a few good years left if I was careful, fewer if I wasn't. I thanked him, paid the bill, and drove home the long way.

I won't pretend I took it well. There's a particular kind of quiet that fills a workshop when you realize you're working on a clock now. Every wallet I cut, every saddle stitch I pull, I'm aware it's one of a finite number left. Finite. That's a word I never used to think about. Now I think about it every morning.

I'm not telling you this to make you feel sorry for me. I'm telling you because what happened next is the reason I'm writing this letter, and I want you to understand it the way I do.

Last spring a man from Nordstrom drove out from Dallas in a rental SUV. He wore a nice jacket and asked good questions. He sat at my bench for about an hour, picked up three or four wallets, turned them over in his hands, and ran his thumb along the stitching the way someone does when they actually know what they're looking at. I appreciated that.

Then he made me an offer. $65 a wallet, two thousand units, paid up front. He told me they'd retail them at $229 with a hangtag that said "Handcrafted in Texas." He said it like it was good news. For a man my age, with a diagnosis like mine, it probably should have been.

I sat there for a minute. I thought about Linda, about the property tax, about the fact that two thousand wallets at $65 a piece is more money than I've made in three good years on the bench. And then I thought about the hangtag.

"Handcrafted in Texas." Marked up to $229. My name, or near enough to it, sitting on a shelf next to a $400 belt made in Vietnam. A man would walk in, pay the markup, and never know that the fellow who actually cut the leather got $65 and a handshake.

I told him no. Walked him out to his SUV, shook his hand, watched him pull out of the gravel drive. Then I came back inside and told Linda I'd rather sell the last 400 direct than see my name turned into somebody else's markup. She poured me a second cup of coffee and said, "Well, alright then." That's how she is.

So that's what this is. A letter from me to you, telling you about a wallet.

It's a bifold. Full-grain leather — which is the part of the hide closest to the hair, the strongest and the only grade that develops a real patina instead of cracking and flaking after a year or two. The kind they use for English saddles and the holsters my grandfather made before me. Every wallet is hand-stitched with waxed thread, the same saddle stitch I learned from my father when I was eleven years old. No machines. Two needles, one stitch at a time. About 90 minutes of bench work per wallet.

It's slim — sits flat in your back pocket, holds six cards and folded bills, and the leather is thick enough that it'll feel stiff for the first two weeks. That's normal. By month three it'll start molding to the shape of your hand and your pocket. By year five it'll be soft as a work glove and dark in the spots you touch most. By year thirty, if I've done my job right, it'll look like it belongs to nobody but you.

I don't put a logo on the front. I never have. There's a small P.L. debossed on the inside, the same mark my grandfather used. That's all.

Backed by a lifetime guarantee. Mine — the one I sign personally on a card that ships in the box. If a stitch ever pulls, you mail it back, I fix it. If I'm gone, Linda has instructions, and there's a young man in the workshop named Caleb who I've been training for two years. He'll honor it. That's in writing too.

Here's why I'm writing today, and I'll be straight with you about it.

I have 400 wallets left in me. Maybe a few more if my hands cooperate, maybe fewer if they don't. I've already cut the leather for most of them — it's stacked on the shelf above my bench in three neat piles. When those 400 are gone, the bench goes quiet. Caleb will keep small repairs going for the lifetime guarantees, but the new wallets stop. There's no reorder coming. There's no "next batch." This is it.

I'm pricing them at what I'd have to charge a friend who walked in off the road and asked. Not the $229 Nordstrom wanted. Not even close. I want each one to go to a man who'll actually carry it — not collect it, not flip it, not stick it in a drawer. A man who'll pull it out at a bar in Austin in 2034 and have the bartender say, "Now that's a wallet."

That's the deal I can look myself in the mirror over. And at 67, with hands like mine, the mirror is the only customer I've got left to please.
