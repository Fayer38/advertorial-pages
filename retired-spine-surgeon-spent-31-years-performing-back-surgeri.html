"I Started Noticing a Pattern I Couldn't Ignore"

In his first year of retirement, Dr. Ames began reaching out to former patients — people he had operated on five, ten, even fifteen years earlier.

"I expected to hear success stories," he says. "And some were. But an alarming number of patients told me the same thing: 'The pain came back.'"

Not immediately. Not from a failed procedure. The surgeries had technically succeeded. Discs were decompressed. Nerves were freed. Hardware was stable.

But within 3 to 5 years, many patients were right back where they started — stiff in the mornings, unable to sit comfortably, reaching for ibuprofen before breakfast, declining invitations that required standing or walking for any length of time.

"The question that kept me up at night was simple," Dr. Ames recalls. "If the surgery worked — and structurally, it did — why was the pain returning?"

Warning: Research shows that up to 40% of spinal surgery patients report a return of significant pain within 5 years — a phenomenon so common it has its own clinical name: Failed Back Surgery Syndrome (FBSS).

That question sent Dr. Ames on a research journey that would take him through hundreds of case files, peer-reviewed biomechanics studies, and conversations with rehabilitation specialists across the country.

The answer, once he found it, was painfully simple.

The Real Culprit: A Problem Hiding in Plain Sight

Deep along your spine, there is a group of small but critically important muscles called the deep paraspinal muscles — specifically the multifidus and the transversus abdominis.

You've probably never heard of them. Most patients haven't. But these muscles are the primary stabilizers of your lumbar spine. They act like a biological brace, holding each vertebra in proper alignment during every movement you make — bending, twisting, sitting, walking, even breathing.

"Think of them as the guy wires on a radio tower," Dr. Ames explains. "The tower is your spine. The guy wires keep it upright and stable. Now imagine those wires slowly going slack, one by one, over a period of years."

That slow slackening is exactly what happens. And Dr. Ames now calls it 'the silent collapse.'

Here's the mechanism: when you experience back pain — whether from an injury, poor posture, or age-related wear — your body instinctively shifts load away from the painful area. You compensate. You guard. You move differently.

The problem? Those deep stabilizing muscles stop being recruited. And unlike your biceps or quads, you can't see them atrophying. You can't feel them weakening. The deterioration is invisible — but its consequences are devastating.

"Within as little as 3 days of altered movement patterns, measurable atrophy begins in the multifidus," Dr. Ames notes, citing a widely referenced 2006 study published in Spine. "Within weeks, the cross-sectional area of these muscles can decrease by 20-25%. And once that stabilization is lost, every other structure in the lumbar spine — discs, facet joints, ligaments — begins to bear loads they were never designed to handle alone."

Why Everything You've Tried Addresses the Wrong Target

If you're reading this, chances are you've been managing your back pain for years. Maybe decades. And you've probably tried some combination of the following:

Over-the-counter painkillers — ibuprofen, acetaminophen, naproxen. They reduce inflammation and dull pain signals. But they do nothing — literally nothing — to activate or rebuild a single muscle fiber in your lumbar spine.

Heating pads and ice packs. Temporary comfort. Zero structural impact.

TENS units. These small electrical devices are enormously popular. But here's what most people don't realize: TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation) works by stimulating sensory nerves to interrupt pain signals. It's a distraction technique. It does not activate motor nerves, which means it cannot cause a muscle to contract, strengthen, or rebuild.

Chiropractic adjustments. Can provide short-term relief by improving joint mobility. But if the muscles that hold those joints in place are atrophied, the adjustment won't hold. The spine simply drifts back to its unstable position.

Cortisone injections. Powerful anti-inflammatories that can quiet a flare-up for weeks or months. But they don't address muscle deterioration — and repeated injections may actually accelerate tissue breakdown.

Even physical therapy — often considered the gold standard — has a critical limitation: it relies on voluntary muscle contraction. And research shows that once the deep paraspinal muscles have atrophied past a certain threshold, many patients simply cannot recruit them voluntarily, no matter how carefully coached they are.

A Different Approach: Reaching the Muscles That Pain Made Dormant

Dr. Ames's research eventually led him to a body of clinical evidence surrounding a technology called NMES — Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation.

Unlike TENS, which targets sensory nerves to mask pain, NMES targets motor nerves — the nerves that directly control muscle contraction. When applied to the lumbar region, NMES can force the deep paraspinal muscles to contract and engage, even when they've atrophied to the point where a patient can no longer activate them on their own.

"That distinction is everything," Dr. Ames explains. "You're not interrupting a pain signal. You're rebuilding the structural support system that prevents the pain from occurring in the first place."

The clinical literature supporting NMES for paraspinal muscle rehabilitation is not new — but it has grown substantially in the past decade. Five peer-reviewed studies are particularly relevant:

  • A 2015 study in the Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy demonstrated that NMES significantly increased multifidus cross-sectional area in patients with chronic low back pain over an 8-week protocol.
  • Research published in European Spine Journal (2017) showed NMES combined with exercise therapy produced greater pain reduction and functional improvement than exercise therapy alone.
  • A 2019 randomized controlled trial in Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation found that patients receiving NMES reported significantly lower pain scores and improved lumbar stability after 12 weeks.
  • A 2020 systematic review in Clinical Rehabilitation concluded that NMES is a safe, effective adjunct therapy for activating deep lumbar stabilizers that resist voluntary recruitment.
  • A 2022 study in BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders confirmed measurable paraspinal muscle hypertrophy in participants using daily at-home NMES protocols over a 6-week period.

"For decades, this technology was confined to clinical rehabilitation settings," Dr. Ames notes. "Patients would use it during supervised post-surgical therapy, three times a week, at $200 per session. It worked — but access was a barrier."
