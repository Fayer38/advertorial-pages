By Margaret Holloway ✓ April 28, 2026

Linda Carlisle, 68, spent 41 years on the med-surg floor of a hospital outside Akron, Ohio. She raised two kids on twelve-hour shifts and now babysits her grandson three afternoons a week.

Three winters ago, the burning started in her toes around 2am. She knew the script before her own doctor wrote it: gabapentin, follow-up in six months, brace yourself. She'd handed that same prescription to hundreds of patients and watched every single one shuffle slower the next visit. So she stalled. Refused the pill bottle. Sat with her feet on a heating pad and tried to think.

Then she dug out the biochemistry textbooks she'd kept since nursing school, and found a sentence that stopped her cold.

Damaged nerves aren't dead. Most are dormant — and dormant cells can be woken.

What 41 Years On The Ward Actually Taught Her

Forty-one years on a med-surg floor means thousands of patients. Linda saw the same arc again and again: the diabetic feet, the post-chemo hands, the 70-year-old who fell in his own driveway because he couldn't feel the curb.

Gabapentin doesn't repair anything. It quiets the alarm wire. Meanwhile the nerve fiber underneath keeps thinning, the myelin sheath keeps fraying, and the patient keeps losing ground. "We were managing decline," she told me, stirring her coffee. "Nobody on that floor was getting better. We just got better at not naming it."

The neurologists call it permanent. The pharmaceutical playbook, with its hundred-dollar prescriptions, calls it progressive. Linda knew from cellular biology that a different path existed, and that neither word was the whole truth.

She spent six months at her kitchen table — textbooks, library PDFs, a yellow legal pad — circling one finding: starve a nerve of specific cofactors, it goes dormant. Restore them, the signal returns. No clinic taught it. No script existed.
