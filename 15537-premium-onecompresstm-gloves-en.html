I Spent Forty Years Treating Arthritis, Then Two Years Quietly Denying It In Myself

A charge nurse's logbook on the compression approach that restored her grip when prescriptions and a fusion consult could not.

March 14, 2026 by Margaret Whitaker Sponsored

I spent forty-one years on an orthopedic floor outside Pittsburgh, and I can tell you the sound a swollen knuckle makes when a patient tries to hide it. What I cannot tell you, comfortably, is how I spent two years pretending I did not hear that same sound coming from my own hands.

The morning it broke me, I was trying to twist the cap off my blood pressure medication. A childproof lid. I had opened ten thousand of them for patients. I sat on the kitchen floor with the bottle in my lap and cried, not from the pain, but because I knew exactly how the conveyor belt ends.

  • Jar lids you used to open easily?
  • Waking at three a.m. to throbbing knuckles?
  • Hiding your hands when grandchildren ask for help?

I am not the only nurse who hid this. I am not the only woman in her sixties who buttoned her blouse crooked and blamed the light. Roughly one in four adults over fifty carries diagnosed arthritis in the hands, and most are quietly rationing what their fingers can still do.

How I Finally Admitted It To Myself

I was sixty-six. Four years retired. The hands that had squeezed a sphygmomanometer bulb a thousand times a week, that had opened IV vials with my teeth when my fingers refused, were swelling at the second joint. My wedding ring lived on a chain around my neck. I told my husband it was carpal tunnel. I told myself it was the weather.

The small losses stacked up before the big ones. I stopped baking my grandmother's rye bread because I could not knead. I stopped knitting the Christmas stockings. I switched to button-front blouses, then to pullovers, then to whatever my daughter could fasten for me. My husband noticed me turning coffee mugs down at the handle, and I saw him notice, and neither of us said a word about it for months.

My rheumatologist offered methotrexate, then a fusion consult at Allegheny. I had watched enough fusion recoveries to know what that road looked like. My daughter, a PA in Denver, drove the conversation back to basics: what, she asked, do you actually tell your patients to try first? I had not asked myself that question in two years.

The Four Hand Conditions I Saw Most On My Floor

Most people lump all hand pain under 'arthritis' and stop there. On the orthopedic floor we rarely did. Knowing which kind you're dealing with changes what helps, and it changes what a compression approach can and cannot do.

  • OSTEOARTHRITIS OF HAND The wear-and-tear kind, most common after fifty. Cartilage at the base of the thumb and the middle joints thins, aches, and slowly reshapes the knuckles you grew up with.
  • RHEUMATOID INFLAMMATORY ARTHRITIS An autoimmune version. The body attacks its own joint linings, usually symmetrically. Mornings are the worst; knuckles swell warm and stiff for an hour before the hands consent to work.
  • TRIGGER FINGER TENDONITIS The tendon sheath thickens and catches, so a finger locks bent and releases with a painful click. Often mistaken for arthritis until a nurse watches the hand under good light.
  • POST-SURGICAL CARPAL STIFFNESS What lingers after a carpal tunnel release or wrist fusion. The nerve is quieter, but the tendons and soft tissue stayed tight, and grip strength takes stubborn months to return.

What My Colleagues Reach For First, And Why I Didn't

The usual first line is well-known: over-the-counter NSAIDs for the pain, a prescription corticosteroid or methotrexate if the inflammation persists, a wrist brace at night, paraffin wax dips if you have the patience. Then a cortisone injection into the base of the thumb or the middle joint, repeated every few months. Then the conversation about joint fusion or replacement, the MRI, the surgical consult, the pre-op labs, the six-week recovery nobody tells you really takes ten.

Each step costs money my Medicare Part D doesn't fully absorb, and each step costs the hand something too. NSAIDs hammer the stomach and kidneys; the patients I discharged on long-term ibuprofen came back with ulcers. Cortisone works, until the fourth shot, when the cartilage starts thinning faster than it was already. Surgery fixes one joint and stiffens the four around it. And through all of it, the underlying circulation problem — the sluggish blood that lets inflammation pool overnight — is never actually addressed.

My daughter sent me a pair of medical compression gloves with a note that said, Mom, try the thing you would have told a patient to try thirty years ago. I almost laughed. Then I put them on before bed, and by morning my right hand closed without the audible protest.
