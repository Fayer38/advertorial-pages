He Ground Lenses for Luxury Binoculars for 34 Years — Then Built His Own

Frank Hollister's final batch ships directly from a Henrietta warehouse.

By Margaret Cullen
Consumer optics reporter · 18 years field experience
Published April 12, 2026

Frank Hollister retired in 2022 after thirty-four years grinding lenses at a Rochester optics plant most Americans have never heard of.

The binoculars he built ended up in European catalogs and big-box sporting stores with four-digit price tags stamped on the box.

He watched the same glass he polished leave his bench at one cost and reappear on retail shelves for roughly twelve times more.

Now he builds the same optic under his own name and ships it from a small warehouse in Henrietta to anyone who orders.

A Thirty-Four-Year Career Spent Grinding Glass for Someone Else's Label

Frank started at the Rochester plant in 1988, the year his first daughter was born.

He trained on prism alignment under a foreman named Walt Pickering, a Korea veteran who taught him to feel a lens's center with a thumbnail.

For three decades he polished BAK4 prisms and coated objective lenses that shipped overseas and came back wrapped in premium European marketing.

His name never appeared on anything. Neither did Walt's. Neither did any of the forty craftsmen on that line.

By the time he clocked out for the last time in November 2022, the markup on his work had quietly become his private obsession.

A Brother-in-Law's Warbler Purchase That Finally Pushed Him Over the Edge

Frank's brother-in-law Gene lives on Cayuga Lake and has been serious about warblers since the 1980s.

In December 2024, Gene showed up to Christmas dinner with a brand-new Austrian pair he'd bought through a specialty retailer in Ithaca.

Gene, a retired electrician, had saved for two years to afford his Austrian pair and considered it his last big outdoor purchase.

Frank held them, walked to the window, glassed a nuthatch on the neighbor's feeder, and handed them back without a word.

Every Pair He'd Owned Before Had Failed Him in the Same Way

Frank had spent years quietly testing every mid-tier pair that crossed his workbench.

He kept a notebook on what each brand cut to hit their price tier, the shortcuts no catalog ever mentioned.

Cheaper house-brand models used BK7 prisms instead of BAK4, losing edge sharpness in low-light woodland conditions.

Mid-tier imports skipped full multi-coating, applying only two of the five optical layers on every glass surface.

Plastic body housings cracked after the second or third Adirondack hiking trip, letting moisture inside the barrels.

Loose prism cages drifted out of alignment after one drop, creating double images that never quite resolved.

Nitrogen-purged marketing claims without the actual sealed chamber meant cold mornings fogged the insides permanently.

The flagship Austrians did everything right. They also required a check that represented two months of his retirement budget.

What Frank Kept From the Flagship, and What He Cut

He pulled out an old green notebook that same Christmas night and started listing what a fair pair would require.

The goal was the same prism stack as the Austrian model — stripped of every middleman and every catalog photograph.

  • BAK4 roof prisms with phase-correction coatings, identical to the spec sheet he'd polished for three decades.
  • Fully multi-coated ED glass on every air-to-glass surface, not just the front objectives.
  • Aircraft-grade magnesium alloy chassis instead of molded polycarbonate, rubberized for grip on cold dawn walks.
  • True nitrogen purging inside a sealed chamber, preventing the internal fogging that ruins most mid-tier units.
  • Direct shipping from a contract optical house to a Henrietta warehouse, skipping every showroom between them.

The Wholesaler Offer That Frank Finally Decided to Turn Down

Last spring, a Midwest sporting-goods distributor called Frank's landline offering to take the whole production run.

They'd pay him a wholesale number and then shelf the binoculars under a different house label at roughly four times that figure.

Frank said no. "I spent thirty-four years watching that exact arrangement," he told me. "I won't be the one signing the paperwork now."

What the Last Working Lens Craftsmen in Rochester Still Know

Rochester built the American optics industry. Bausch & Lomb, Kodak, dozens of contract shops grinding glass since the late 1800s.

Most of that knowledge walked out the door with men Frank's age and never came back to a production floor.

What Walt Pickering taught him in 1988 — how to feel prism alignment with a thumbnail, how to read coating thickness against a diffuse light — isn't written in any manual you can buy.

That discipline is what makes the difference between a pair of binoculars that resolves feather detail at dawn and a pair that goes soft the first time the light drops.

It's the quiet reason every unit in this Henrietta run performs the way it does.
