It was a Tuesday in March, a little past six in the morning, and I was standing at the kitchen sink trying to open a jar of coffee.

I couldn't. My fingers wouldn't close all the way. Linda — my wife of forty-one years — took the jar out of my hand without a word and set it down next to my mug. She didn't say anything because she already knew what I was about to admit to myself.

My name is Paul Lucas. I'm 67 years old, and I make leather wallets by hand in a 900-square-foot workshop in Weatherford, Texas — in the same brick building my grandfather bought in 1948. And that morning, standing at the sink, I finally understood I was running out of wallets.

Three generations of this bench, and the thing that took my father

My grandfather cut saddle skirts on the bench I still use. My father learned from him, and I learned from my father. The bench is maple, stained black in the middle where three generations of forearms have rested on it.

Three years ago my hands started locking up in the mornings. I'd wake up and my fingers would be curled like I was still holding an awl. It'd take forty minutes of hot water and stretching before I could grip a skiving knife.

I knew what it was before the doctor in Fort Worth confirmed it. Rheumatoid arthritis — the same thing that took my father off the bench at 70. He made it three more years after he stopped stitching, and I don't think he was ever quite himself again. A man who's worked with his hands since he was twelve doesn't know what to do with them once they stop.

So when Linda took that coffee jar out of my hand, I did the math I'd been avoiding. I had hide enough, thread enough, and mornings enough left in these hands for maybe 400 more wallets. Not 4,000. Not 40,000. Four hundred.

The man from Nordstrom who drove out in a rental SUV

Last spring a buyer from Nordstrom drove out from Dallas in a rental SUV. Nice fellow. Clean boots, the kind that have never been worked in. He'd heard about the shop from somebody in Austin and he wanted to place an order.

He sat at my bench and offered me $65 a wallet for a 2,000-unit run. Said he'd retail them at $229 with a little hangtag that said "handcrafted in Texas."

I sat with that for a long minute. Two thousand units at sixty-five dollars is $130,000. That's real money for a man whose hands are closing up shop. I could've put it in the ground and been fine.

But here's the thing that stuck in my throat. A man would walk into a Nordstrom in Plano or Seattle, pick up my wallet, see that hangtag, and pay $229 for it — thinking he was buying something direct from a Texas craftsman. He wasn't. He was paying $164 for the Nordstrom markup and $65 for my hands. That ratio bothered me.

I walked him to his car, shook his hand, and told him no. Came back inside and told Linda what I'd done. She said, "Good. I didn't like the way he sat at your bench."

Why I'm writing to you instead of going to stores

I decided that night I'd rather sell the last 400 direct than see my name turned into a markup. That's what this letter is.

I'm writing to the man who'll actually carry this wallet. Not to a buyer. Not to a magazine. To you.

If you're reading this, I'd guess you already know the difference between full-grain leather and the bonded stuff they glue up and call "genuine." You've probably had a cheap wallet peel on you at the corners and swore you wouldn't do it again. Maybe you've bought one good thing in your life — a watch, a pair of boots, a knife — and you still remember the weight of it the first time you held it.

This wallet is cut from a single piece of American full-grain, the top cut of the hide. It's stitched by hand with waxed linen thread. The edges are burnished with beeswax and a piece of canvas — the way my grandfather did it, because a machine can't feel when the leather is ready. It'll look a little plain when it arrives. Within a year it'll be the color of strong tea. In five years it'll be soft as a work glove and hold the shape of your back pocket like it grew there.

What it feels like, five years in

I still carry the wallet I made for myself in 2006. Nineteen years this June. The leather has gone the color of an old saddle and the stitches are still tight. I've replaced zero wallets in those nineteen years.

A customer named Bill from Billings, Montana wrote me a card last Christmas. He'd carried his Paul Lucas for eleven years. "It went to my son's wedding in my hip pocket," he wrote. "He asked if he could have it when I'm gone. I told him he'd have to wait, but yes." That card is pinned above the bench.

Another fellow, a surgeon in Nashville named Dr. Michael Ortega, sent me a photograph of his wallet next to the one his father had carried — my father made his father's wallet in 1981. "They look like brothers," he wrote. I looked at that picture a long time.

I don't say any of this to puff up the product. I say it because when a man asks me "how long will it last?" — the honest answer is longer than the man carrying it, if he takes even halfway decent care of it.
