The Morning Everything Changed

I was doing what millions of homeowners do every fall weekend. Grabbed my 23-pound Stihl gas blower from the garage. Yanked the starter cord.

The crack I heard wasn't the engine turning over. It was my L4-L5 disc.

I was flat on my garage floor for what felt like an eternity. My wife found me twenty minutes later. Three days in bed. Two months of physical therapy. Six cortisone injections over the following year.

And the worst part? The doctor looked me in the eye and said: "Albert, if you keep using heavy equipment like that, you're looking at surgery. Maybe a fusion."

I'm an engineer. I solve problems. But lying in that hospital bed, staring at the ceiling, I realized I couldn't solve this one — not with the tools that existed.

I Tried Everything. Nothing Worked.

During my recovery, I still had a property to maintain. A 2,500 sq ft yard. A two-car driveway. A patio that collected oak leaves like a magnet.

So I tried the alternatives. Every single one.

The rake? Twenty-five minutes of bending and twisting for a job that should take five. My back screamed after ten.

The push broom? Marginally better. Still required torso rotation that my therapist explicitly told me to avoid. And wet leaves? Forget it. The broom just pushed them around.

A lighter cordless blower from the hardware store? I bought a $180 model that weighed "only" 9 pounds. Nine pounds doesn't sound like much — until you're holding it at arm's length for 15 minutes with a compromised spine. I lasted two weekends before the pain came back.

I even hired a landscaping crew. $150 a month. They came every two weeks. But between their visits, the leaves piled up again. The patio looked terrible. And I felt something I hadn't felt in 31 years of solving complex engineering problems:

Helpless.

That was the moment I stopped looking for a solution on a shelf — and started designing one on my workbench.

"Why Are We Still Using 1970s Motor Technology to Blow Leaves?"

Here's what most people don't realize: the motor inside your gas blower is essentially the same design from 50 years ago. Brushed motor. Heavy magnets. Massive housing to contain the vibration. It's why these things weigh 15 to 25 pounds.

But for three decades at Boeing, I compressed enormous volumes of air through impossibly small turbine systems. At 40,000 feet. In spaces smaller than a shoebox.

Lying in that hospital bed, the question hit me like a freight train:

"If I can compress air at 40,000 feet inside a jet engine — why can't I do it in my driveway?"

That question became an obsession. Eight months. 47 prototypes. My garage looked like a wind tunnel testing lab. My wife thought I'd lost my mind.

But on prototype #47, something happened. I paired a brushless turbine motor — the same technology used in racing drones and Tesla's drivetrain — with a proprietary air compression chamber I designed from scratch.

The result: 200 MPH of airflow. 30,000+ RPM. 1,200 CFM.

Weight? 2.1 pounds. Less than a bag of sugar.

I held it in one hand. Pointed it at a pile of wet, matted oak leaves stuck to my patio concrete. And watched them scatter like they'd been hit by a hurricane.

My wife came running out. "What was THAT?"

That was the Seese Pro.

I get this question every single day. "How can something that weighs less than a bag of sugar produce 200 MPH winds?"
