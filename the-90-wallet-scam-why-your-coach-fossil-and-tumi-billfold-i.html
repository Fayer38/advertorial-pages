The $90 Wallet Scam: Why Your Coach, Fossil, and Tumi Billfold Is Held Together By Glue

An investigation into America's department-store leather racket — and the one Texas workshop still doing it the old way

ET
Editorial Team
April 17, 2026 · 8 min read · 2,194,477 views

BROOKLYN, NY — A third-generation cobbler opened his vise around eight brand-name wallets this month. Six of them fell apart before he finished inspecting them. The brands on the tags read like the mall directory at any American shopping center: Coach. Fossil. Tumi. Cole Haan. Michael Kors. Shinola. Brooks Brothers. Johnston & Murphy.

Only two of the eight were actually sewn together. The rest were glued.

What Our Investigation Found Inside Eight Department-Store Wallets

The shop sits on a side street in Carroll Gardens, wedged between a bakery and a dry cleaner. Anthony Russo, 58, has been repairing leather goods for thirty-four years. His grandfather opened the store in 1951. His father ran it after that. Anthony agreed to examine eight wallets we purchased at full retail from department stores in Manhattan earlier this week.

He didn't need ten minutes with any of them.

  • Finding #1: Six of the eight wallets were bonded with industrial adhesive, then topped with a decorative chain stitch that carries no structural load.
  • Finding #2: The average retail price of the eight wallets was $148. The estimated factory cost, per Russo and two importers we spoke with, was $9 to $14.
  • Finding #3: Five of the wallets were labeled "genuine leather" or "full-grain Italian leather." Only one was actually full-grain. The others were corrected-grain or split leather — the lower layer of the hide, sanded smooth and sprayed with a polymer finish to mimic the real thing.
  • Finding #4: The "Italian leather" tag on a $190 bifold traced, through customs filings, to a tannery in Chennai, India. It was finished in Italy for four days before shipment.

"This one," Russo said, pulling lightly at the edge of a $175 bifold with his pick, "won't see two winters." The top-stitch lifted away from the leather like a loose thread on a sweater. Underneath, a thin yellow line of glue held the panels together.

The Cobbler's Verdict: "The Whole Category Is a Markup Fiction"

Russo spoke to us for nearly two hours. What follows is edited for length only.

"People bring me these wallets and they're embarrassed. They paid $120, $180, $250 at a department store. They think they bought quality. They didn't. They bought a brand.

The construction you want is called a saddle stitch. Two needles, one thread, pulled by hand through every hole. If one stitch breaks, the next twenty hold. It's how saddles are made — because a saddle has to last thirty years under a thousand pounds of working horse.

What these brands do is a chain stitch laid on top of a glue line. One stitch breaks, the whole seam unzips. And the glue? Glue dries. Three years, maybe four, and it turns to powder. That's why your wallet blows out at the corner at exactly eighteen months. It's not bad luck. It's the design."

Russo paused, then added: "The whole category is a markup fiction. You're paying $200 for a $12 wallet with a famous name stamped on it."

The math is uncomfortable. An American man spending $150 every eighteen months on replacement wallets will spend more than $3,000 over a working lifetime — and throw away dozens of pieces of fake leather in the process. Most of it never biodegrades. Polymer-finished split hide behaves, in a landfill, roughly like vinyl upholstery.

What The Industry Doesn't Want You To Know

We reached out to each of the eight brands in our sample. Three did not respond. Four declined to comment. One — we agreed not to name them — responded off the record to confirm, reluctantly, that "adhesive construction with decorative stitching is standard in the category above $80 and below $400."

Translation: almost everything on the mall floor.

The supply chain looks like this:

  • Hides are tanned in India, Pakistan, or Bangladesh, where environmental and labor costs are a fraction of European rates.
  • They're shipped, sometimes through a brief stop in Italy for paperwork, to factories in Dongguan, Guangdong Province, where a single line can produce 4,000 wallets a day.
  • Construction is glue-bonding because it's fast: a full wallet assembled in under a minute versus roughly forty-five minutes for a hand-pulled saddle stitch build.
  • The finished wallet lands at a U.S. distributor for $11, ships to a department store, and sells for $90 to $220.

The markup isn't paying for craftsmanship. It's paying for a logo, a display case, and a mall lease.

The One Workshop Still Doing It The Old Way

Two thousand miles from Russo's shop, in a converted feed store outside Austin, Texas, there is a workshop that still pulls a saddle stitch by hand through every hole.

The operation is small. Six leatherworkers. A clicker press older than any of them. Racks of full-grain American cowhide from a Chicago tannery that has been in business since 1905 — the same tannery that supplies working saddlemakers and the U.S. military's parade belts.

The wallets take roughly forty-five minutes each to construct. Every hole is punched with an iron, then stitched with waxed polyester thread, two needles crossing through each stitch. If you cut a thread, the seam holds. If you cut ten, it still holds.

The workshop operates under the founder's own name: Paul Lucas, a former aerospace contractor who quit his job in 2016 after his father's hand-stitched billfold outlasted four of his own store-bought replacements. He built the operation on one premise: "If it ever fails, we fix it or replace it. For as long as you're alive."

The wallets carry a lifetime guarantee. Not a year. Not five years. Lifetime. And because they're made correctly, the guarantee is almost never claimed.
