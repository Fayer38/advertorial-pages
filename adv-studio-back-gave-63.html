I Was on My Knees in the Driveway, and Not Because I Was Praying

LIFESTYLE
A man in his early 60s standing in a leaf-covered driveway, one hand on his lower back, looking exhausted and frustrated. Autumn morning light, suburban home in the background.

It was the first Saturday of October, and I was standing in my driveway holding a rake in one hand and pressing the other into my lower back. The oak tree had dumped an entire summer's worth of leaves onto my patio overnight, and I hadn't even started yet.

I looked at the pile. I looked at my old gas blower sitting in the garage — 15 pounds of machine I hadn't touched in three months because the last time I used it, I couldn't get out of bed the next morning. And I thought: when did taking care of my own home become something I dread?

That morning, I made a decision I never thought I'd make. I almost called a landscaping service. At 61 years old, for a job I'd done myself since I bought this house in 1998. That's the moment I knew something had to change.

The Weekend Routine That Was Breaking Me Down

Let me tell you what my Saturdays used to look like. Wake up, coffee, stare out the kitchen window at whatever mess the week left behind — leaves on the patio, dust on the garage floor, pine needles in the gutter. Then spend the next 20 minutes convincing myself to go out there and deal with it.

The gas blower was the worst of it. Hauling that thing out of the back of the garage, past the bikes and the lawnmower. Filling it with fuel. Yanking the starter cord three, four, five times before it finally roared to life. And then — the weight. Fifteen minutes in, my right shoulder was on fire. Twenty minutes, my lower back started screaming. By the time I was done, I'd sit on the porch steps and just… stay there. Sometimes for half an hour. My wife Linda would bring me ibuprofen without me even asking. She already knew.

Some weekends, I just didn't do it. I'd look at the leaves piling up against the fence and tell myself I'd get to it tomorrow. Tomorrow became next weekend. Next weekend became "maybe when it stops being so bad." The patio looked neglected. The neighbors' places were spotless. I felt embarrassed. Not because I'm vain — because I've always taken pride in this house.

The hardest part wasn't the pain. It was the feeling that my own home was slowly getting away from me.

I tried alternatives. A leaf vacuum — heavy, clunky, constantly clogging. A cordless blower from the hardware store — barely strong enough to move dry grass, let alone wet leaves. The broom and dustpan, like I was 30 years younger and had knees that cooperated. Nothing worked without punishing my body for it.

A Conversation at the Car Wash That Changed Everything

Three weeks later, I was at the self-serve car wash, drying my truck with a stack of microfiber towels. It takes forever. You wipe one panel, water runs down from the roof onto the spot you just dried. You chase drips around the car like a fool. And no matter how careful you are, the towels leave tiny swirl marks on the paint if you look closely enough.

The guy in the bay next to me was drying his black SUV. Except he wasn't using towels. He was holding something small — looked almost like a hairdryer — and blasting water off the car in sheets. No touching the paint at all. He was done in about three minutes. I was still working on my hood.

"What is that thing?" I asked, probably sounding a little too eager.

He held it up. Compact. Light green and black. Looked like it weighed nothing. "Seese Pro," he said. "Best purchase I made all year. I use it for everything — my car, my deck, my workshop, snow off the windshield in January. My wife uses it to blow dust out of the garage. She's 58 and uses it one-handed."

He handed it to me. And I'm not exaggerating when I say this: I almost laughed out loud. It weighed nothing. I mean, genuinely nothing. It felt like holding a water bottle. "This weighs less than two pounds," he said. "But hit the trigger."

I hit the trigger. The force nearly made me flinch. A jet of air came out so strong it blew the cap off the trash can six feet away. This tiny thing in my hand was pushing 200 miles per hour of airflow. I didn't believe it until I felt it.

I went home and ordered one that night.

The First Time I Used It, I Stood There in Disbelief

The package arrived five days later. I opened the carrying case — neat, compact, everything organized inside. Battery, charger, the blower itself with two nozzle attachments. I charged the battery, snapped it in, and walked outside to my leaf-covered patio.

I held the Seese Pro in my right hand. Just my right hand. I hit the trigger on the highest setting — and leaves started flying off the concrete like they'd been launched. Wet leaves, stuck-down leaves, the stubborn ones that usually need a scraper. Gone. All of them. I cleared my entire 400-square-foot patio in under five minutes. Five minutes. No bending. No sweating. No pain.

I stood there looking at the clean patio and genuinely didn't know what to do with myself. I'd budgeted 40 minutes for this. I had 35 minutes of my Saturday back. My back felt fine. My shoulder felt fine. My hands weren't even tired.

The brushless motor inside this thing spins at 30,000 RPM — the same technology they use in drones and electric vehicles. That's what gives it 200 MPH of wind speed and 1,200 CFM of airflow. For context, most full-size gas blowers max out around 500 CFM. This 2-pound device outperforms machines five times its weight.

Over the next two weeks, I found myself using it for things I never planned on. Sawdust in the workshop after cutting shelves — 90 seconds and the floor was clean. Grass clippings stuck to the walkway after mowing — gone in a minute. Dust that had accumulated on the patio furniture over winter — blasted away without lifting a single chair. I even used it to dry my truck after washing it. Three minutes. No towels. No swirl marks. Just dry.

Linda watched me from the kitchen window one morning and later said, "You're actually smiling out there. When's the last time that happened?" I honestly couldn't remember.

I'm Not the Only One Who Noticed the Difference

About a month after I got the Seese Pro, my neighbor Dave walked over while I was clearing the front walkway. Dave is 67, retired electrician, bad rotator cuff. He'd been paying a kid $40 every other week to blow his yard. He watched me work for about 30 seconds.

"That's the thing you were telling me about? Hand it over." I gave it to him. He cleared his own front steps in two minutes, came back grinning, and ordered one on his phone before he even walked home.

Two weeks later, Dave told me something that stuck with me: "You know what I like most about it? I don't have to plan around it. I see leaves, I grab it off the shelf, I'm done in five minutes. No gas, no cord, no warming up, no dreading it. I just do it and move on with my day."

That's exactly it. That's the thing that's hard to explain until you experience it. It's not just that the Seese Pro is light or powerful — though it's both. It's that it removes the mental weight of yard work. When the tool is easy, the task stops being a chore. You just do it. Like wiping a counter. Like sweeping a floor. Effortless.

My daughter borrowed it last month to blow out her garage after a renovation. She called me that evening: "Dad, why didn't you tell me about this sooner? I've been sweeping that garage for three hours over two days. This thing did it in ten minutes." She has her own now.

The 40-minute battery life sounds modest on paper. But when a full patio takes five minutes and a car takes three, forty minutes is more than enough to do everything — the walkway, the garage, the car, the porch — and still have battery left over. I've never once run out mid-job.
