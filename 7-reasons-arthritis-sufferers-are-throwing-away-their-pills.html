What Most People Try First (And Why It Fails)

If you've been dealing with hand or joint pain, you've probably tried the usual lineup:

Over-the-counter painkillers. They dull the pain for a few hours, but they don't fix anything. And long-term use? Your doctor will tell you about the stomach problems, the liver risks, the kidney concerns. You're trading one problem for another.

Prescription medications. Stronger, yes. But the side effects read like a horror novel. Nausea. Dizziness. Immune suppression. And the cost — $200, $400, sometimes $600 a month — with no end in sight.

Topical creams and ointments. They feel nice for about 20 minutes. Then the pain comes roaring back. You end up reapplying six times a day and smelling like a medicine cabinet.

Cortisone injections. Temporary relief at best. Each shot wears off faster than the last. And there's a limit to how many you can get before the joint starts deteriorating further.

Here's what none of these solutions address: they treat the symptom, not the cause. Your joints are inflamed, stiff, and lacking proper circulation. Pills and creams can't fix that. But something else can.
