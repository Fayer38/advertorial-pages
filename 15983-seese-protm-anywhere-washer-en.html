ANYTHING ABOVE 800 PSI IS DAMAGING THE OPERATOR, NOT THE DIRT

When Dr. Raymond Holcomb posted his findings on a regional engineering forum last spring, he wasn't trying to start a fight with the pressure washer industry. He had simply spent a Saturday measuring what most homeowners never think to question.

I drove out to Akron, Ohio to meet him because his post had unsettled me. I own a gas washer that lives under a tarp behind my garage, and I had begun to suspect, quietly, that the thing was overkill for what I actually clean.

Holcomb is sixty-seven, soft-spoken, and built like a man who still walks his dog twice a day. He spent thirty-one years at Goodyear designing industrial fluid systems before consulting for two pressure washer manufacturers in the early 2000s.

What Triggered The Investigation

It started with a torn rotator cuff. Holcomb's son-in-law, fighting a 3,000 PSI gas unit on a Saturday driveway project, took the kickback wrong and ended up in surgery the following week. Holcomb watched the recovery from the sidelines, troubled.

He has a workshop behind his house with a concrete floor, a metal lathe, and six pressure washers accumulated over twenty years of consulting work. The Saturday after the surgery, he pulled all six out and started running tests.

What he measured was flow rate, surface impact, and kickback force across four common home surfaces — automotive clear coat, composite decking, vinyl siding, and concrete pavers. He documented each test in a notebook, the way he had for three decades at Goodyear.

YOU DON'T HAVE A POWER PROBLEM. YOU HAVE A MARKETING PROBLEM.

By Sunday afternoon he had a result that surprised him, then made him angry. For every common home surface he tested, anything above 800 PSI was wasted energy. The dirt was already gone. The remaining force was traveling backward into the operator's shoulder.

He called this the spec-sheet con. Manufacturers compete on PSI numbers because they are the easiest thing to print on a box. But for the surfaces homeowners actually clean, those numbers crossed the threshold of usefulness somewhere around 1995.

The Physics Most Homeowners Never Hear

"Pencil sketch on a yellow legal pad showing water flow physics and surface tensi"

Holcomb walked me through it at his kitchen table with a pencil and a yellow pad. Dirt does not stick to your driveway through some superglue bond. It clings through static charge, surface tension, and the simple wedging of particles into porous concrete.

Break those bonds and the dirt lifts. The bonds break when water moves fast enough to disturb the boundary layer at the surface. That happens at far lower pressures than the industry advertises, provided the wand stays close to the surface.

What happens above 800 PSI on a driveway, Holcomb explained, is mostly noise and recoil. The water still cleans, but the additional force does not improve the cleaning. It does, however, scale the kickback proportionally — which is how shoulders end up torn.

Where Clear Coat And Vinyl Lose

The damage doesn't stop at the operator. Holcomb showed me a piece of automotive clear coat he had mounted on a steel plate, riddled with what looked like fine scratches. They were stress fractures, he said, from a 2,400 PSI wand held at the wrong angle.

Composite decking fares no better. The high-pressure stream lifts the polymer cap, leaving a fuzzy texture that holds dirt worse than before. Vinyl siding flexes under the impact and pulls away from the fasteners. Pavers lose their joint sand and start to shift.

Why The Industry Won't Talk About This

I asked Holcomb why nobody in the industry had said any of this publicly. He smiled the way an old man smiles when you ask an obvious question. The PSI war is the only marketing lever the category has, he said. Every brand needs a bigger number.

The retailers, in turn, stack the boxes by the highest PSI on the front. The customer reads Consumer Reports and checks the spec sheets and walks out with the loudest, heaviest, most kickback-prone unit on the shelf. Then spends ten Saturdays a year regretting it.

  • Gas Pressure Washer Two hundred pounds, gasoline, oil changes, kickback that ended my brother-in-law in surgery. Specs that exceed home surface requirements by roughly 400 percent.
  • Corded Electric Lighter than gas but still requires a hose, an outlet, and a fixed water source. The cord becomes the new tripping hazard. Kickback remains real on higher-spec units.
  • Seese Pro Anywhere Battery-powered, draws from any open water source, weighs less than a full grocery bag. Specs matched to home surfaces. One-handed operation. No setup, no infrastructure, no kickback.

Holcomb is not, he is careful to say, accusing anyone of fraud. The numbers on the boxes are accurate. The omission matters — nobody mentions that the surfaces in your home stop benefiting from added pressure long before you reach the rated maximum.

Which is why the Anywhere Washer's specs read modest on paper. They are matched, deliberately, to what residential surfaces require. Not what the marketing department needs to outrank a competitor on a Home Depot endcap.
