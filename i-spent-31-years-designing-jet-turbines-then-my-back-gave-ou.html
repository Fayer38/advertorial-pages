I Spent 31 Years Designing Jet Turbines — Then My Back Gave Out Blowing Leaves

By Editorial Team | March 31, 2026

LIFESTYLE

A man in his early 60s lying on a garage floor next to a large gas-powered backpack blower, grimacing in pain, clutching his lower back. Autumn leaves visible through the open garage door.

The crack I heard that October morning wasn't my gas blower starting up.

It was my L4-L5 disc.

I was lying on my garage floor, staring at the ceiling, with a 23-pound Stihl still strapped to my back — and I made myself a promise I've kept ever since: never again.

My name is Albert Renaud. I'm 64 years old. I spent 31 years designing turbine compression systems for Boeing's 787 Dreamliner. And the thing that finally broke my body wasn't a factory accident or a car crash.

It was blowing leaves off my driveway.

The Problem Nobody Talks About — Until It's Too Late

Here's what I learned from my hospital bed: I wasn't alone. Not even close.

Every year in America, tens of thousands of adults over 50 injure their backs doing routine yard work. Not construction. Not heavy lifting. Yard work. The thing we do every weekend without thinking twice.

The problem isn't willpower. It's physics. A 23-pound gas blower vibrating at full throttle puts roughly 40 pounds of dynamic force on your lower spine. Do that for 30 minutes every Saturday, and you're not maintaining your yard — you're grinding down your vertebrae.

Warning: Repeated use of heavy power tools is a leading cause of chronic lower back pain in adults over 50. Once disc damage occurs, it rarely heals completely. The cumulative stress from vibrating equipment accelerates spinal degeneration — often silently, until the day something snaps.

My orthopedic surgeon told me something I'll never forget: "Albert, your spine looks like it belongs to a 78-year-old. And I see this every week — men your age who think yard tools are harmless."

That was the moment I stopped blaming my age. And started blaming my tools.

Everything I Tried — And Why None of It Worked

After three days flat on my back, I did what any stubborn engineer would do: I tried to solve it with what was already on the market.

First, I tried a lighter cordless blower. The battery-powered ones from the big box stores. Sure, they weighed less — maybe 9 or 10 pounds. But they also blew air like a hair dryer. Wet leaves on concrete? Forget it. I spent more time pushing the broom than using the blower.

Then I tried hiring a lawn service. $45 a week. That's $2,340 a year — just to blow leaves off my driveway and patio. And they only came once a week. In between? My property looked neglected.

I even tried just using a rake and broom. The bending. The sweeping. Twenty-five minutes to clear a 400 square-foot patio. My back screaming by minute ten.

Nothing worked. Either it was powerful enough but destroyed my body, or it was light enough but completely useless.

And that's when my engineering brain asked a question that changed everything.
