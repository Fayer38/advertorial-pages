A Letter From Paul Lucas — Weatherford, Texas

By Editorial Team | April 17, 2026

My name is Paul Lucas. I'm 67 years old, and I'm writing this letter from the same 900-square-foot workshop in Weatherford, Texas that my grandfather bought in 1948.

If you'll give me a few minutes of your time, I'd like to explain why the last 400 wallets I'll ever make are going straight to men like you — and not to a department store in New York City.

This isn't a sales pitch. I've never written one, and I'm too old to start. It's just the truth about how I got here.

LIFESTYLE

Three years ago, my hands started telling me the truth

I woke up one morning and my hands wouldn't open.

I sat on the edge of the bed and flexed my fingers the way you'd squeeze an orange, and nothing happened for about forty seconds. My wife Linda brought me coffee and didn't say anything. She didn't have to. My father had the same thing. Rheumatoid arthritis took him off the bench at 70 — and from the way my knuckles were starting to look, I wasn't going to make it that far.

I've been cutting leather in this building since I was fourteen. My grandfather bought it in 1948 for $4,200 cash. My father ran it after him. I've been stitching saddles, belts, and billfolds here for forty-three years. I know what every tool on this wall sounds like when it's working right. I know how full-grain vegetable-tanned shoulder smells in August when the humidity hits.

And I know I've got maybe eighteen months of good hands left in me. After that, the stitching won't be tight enough to sell, and I won't put my name on work I can't stand behind.

Once the last 400 are sold, there won't be another run. My hands won't allow it, and I won't outsource my name to a factory overseas. When they're gone, the shop closes.

A buyer drove out here last spring in a rented SUV

Last spring a buyer from Nordstrom drove out to the shop in a rented SUV. Nice fella, polite. Wore boots he'd clearly bought that morning. He'd heard about my work through a magazine piece somebody wrote about Texas saddle makers a few years back.

He sat down across from me at the bench and told me he wanted 2,000 wallets. A full production run. He'd pay me $65 per wallet, half up front.

Then he told me what they'd sell for.

$229 on the shelf. With a hangtag that said "Handcrafted in Texas."

I want you to sit with that for a second. $65 to me. $229 to you. That gap — a hundred and sixty-four dollars per wallet — goes to the distributor, the buyer, the store, the marketing department, and whoever designed the hangtag. Not a single one of them has ever held an awl or burnished an edge.

I thought about it for maybe ten seconds. Then I stood up, shook his hand, and walked him to his car.

Back inside, I told Linda what had happened. She asked if I was sure. I told her I'd rather sell the last 400 I make at a price I can look at in the mirror than watch my name get turned into a markup on somebody else's rack.

So I'm doing something I've never done in my whole life

I've never sold a wallet direct to a stranger before. For forty-three years my work has moved through saddleries, a couple of ranches, and a small handful of dealers who actually knew the trade. The phone in this workshop rings maybe twice a week.

But this time, I'm cutting the middle out.

The last 400 wallets are going to men who'll actually carry them. Not men who'll glance at them on a department store shelf between a logo bifold and a nylon cardholder. Men like you — the kind who already know the difference between corrected-grain and full-grain, who've probably owned one good thing in their life and still remember how it felt the first time they picked it up.

I don't know you. But if you've read this far, I think we'd probably have something to talk about.

PRODUCT

Overhead shot of a single bifold wallet on weathered oak workbench, edge burnishing tools, waxed thread spool, and curled leather scraps around it, dramatic side-lighting showing the grain

What you'll actually be carrying

The wallet itself is nothing fancy. I don't go in for fancy.

It's a four-pocket bifold cut from a single hide of Horween full-grain steerhide, vegetable-tanned in Chicago, about 4-5 oz weight. I hand-stitch it with waxed Irish linen thread — the same thread my father used — at eight stitches to the inch. The edges are beveled, burnished with beeswax, and finished by hand. No glue. No heat-press. No machines. Just my hands, an awl, and time.

It'll take a patina the way a good saddle does. In three years of pocket wear it'll look like yours and nobody else's. In ten years it'll look like something you pass down.
