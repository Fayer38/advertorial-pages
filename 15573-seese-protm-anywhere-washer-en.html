Adam Foster sat on his deck in Geauga County last spring, looking at forty thousand gallons of clean lake water.

Fifteen feet from his algae-covered planks. And not a single piece of consumer equipment in his garage could touch it.

  • His Honda was caked with road salt from another brutal Ohio winter, and the cabin had no outdoor spigot anywhere.
  • The deck boards were green with two seasons of algae nobody had bothered to address since his wife passed.
  • His RV sat in the driveway behind a layer of pollen, untouched since the trip back from Tennessee.
  • The pontoon boat needed a wash before launch, and the nearest gas station car wash was twenty-three minutes away.

He had owned four pressure washers in his life. Every single one required a hose, a wall outlet, and a working tap.

And the cabin had none of those things. Just a quiet lake, a frustrated retiree, and a cleaning job nobody wanted to drive into town for.

Adam spent thirty years designing pressure circuits for industrial water systems. He knew exactly why every consumer machine on the market was overbuilt and underdesigned.

The Real Problem

Adam will tell you the pressure washer industry quietly stopped trying somewhere around 2008. The margins were too good to disturb.

Walk into any Home Depot today and look at the wall. Plastic boxes with a motor, a hose port, and a sticker that pretends it's progress.

They've added Bluetooth. They've added cosmetic redesigns. They've added a brand-name compressor inside the housing. What they have not solved is the tether.

Every consumer pressure washer ever sold in this country still requires the same two things: a working spigot and a wall outlet within reach.

And that single design choice is why most of your outdoor cleaning jobs never get done.

The boat at the dock. The RV at the cabin. The car parked at the lake house. The shed roof. The patio behind the rental.

None of these places have a tap nearby. So they stay dirty, year after year, while the equipment in your garage collects dust.

Adam approached the problem the way he approached every industrial circuit he ever designed.

He stripped the machine down to its actual function. Force water through a nozzle at high pressure. Everything else was packaging.

The hose, the cord, the wheels, the bulky plastic shell — all of it existed because the original designs assumed a residential plumbing connection.

Remove the assumption, and suddenly the entire machine could fit in one hand.

The first prototype was crude. A modified pump, a lithium battery, a draw tube long enough to reach into a five-gallon bucket.

He brought it down to the lake, dropped the intake into the water, and pulled the trigger. The deck was clean in eleven minutes.

Six months and forty-three iterations later, the design was ready for production.
