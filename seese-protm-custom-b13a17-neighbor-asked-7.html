My Neighbor Asked Me Three Times What I Was Holding — He Couldn't Believe This 2lb Device Outblew His Husqvarna

Home & Garden Contributor

I used to love Saturday mornings. Coffee on the porch, quiet neighborhood, the smell of cut grass. But somewhere around my 50th birthday, Saturdays became the day I dreaded most. Not because of anything dramatic — just the slow, creeping reality that my body couldn't keep up with my yard anymore. Every weekend, it was the same ritual: drag the 22-pound gas blower out of the garage, yank the starter cord three or four times, strap it on, and spend 45 minutes fighting leaves, dust, and my own lower back. By the time I was done, I'd be hunched over the kitchen counter, popping ibuprofen, wondering why maintaining a home had to feel like punishment. My wife Linda started calling it my "Saturday limp." She wasn't joking.

The worst part wasn't the pain. It was the procrastination. I'd look out the window at the leaves piling up on the driveway and think, "I'll do it tomorrow." Tomorrow turned into next weekend. Next weekend turned into a month of mess. The gutters backed up. The patio looked abandoned. I tried switching to a leaf rake — easier on the back, but it took twice as long and left my shoulders burning. I bought a cordless blower from a big-box store: lightweight, sure, but it couldn't move a wet napkin, let alone matted oak leaves stuck to concrete. I returned it within a week. It felt like the industry was forcing a choice: you either got power and paid for it with your spine, or you got lightweight and paid for it with your time. Neither option worked for me anymore.

Then one Sunday in October, I was standing in my driveway looking at the mess from the night before — a storm had dropped half the oak tree onto my walkway. My neighbor Dave was outside too, working on his yard. But something was different. He was holding this small, compact thing in one hand. It looked like an oversized hair dryer. And it was launching wet leaves off his driveway like they weighed nothing. I watched him clear his entire front path in maybe four minutes. No cord. No gas can. No backpack harness. Just one hand, a slight grin, and a clean driveway. I walked over. "Dave, what is that thing?" He laughed. "Third person to ask me this week." He held it out to me. I almost dropped it — not because it was heavy, but because it was so light I overcompensated. It weighed less than a bottle of wine.

Dave told me it was called the Seese Pro. A brushless motor blower — the same turbine technology used in commercial drones and, apparently, Tesla motors. He'd found it online a month earlier after his physical therapist told him to stop using his gas blower or risk a herniated disc. He was skeptical at first, just like I was standing there holding it. But then he showed me the specs: 200 miles per hour of air velocity. 30,000 RPM. 1,200 CFM of airflow. Those are numbers you'd expect from a professional-grade gas blower — the kind that weighs 20 pounds and costs north of 300 dollars. This thing weighed 950 grams. About two pounds. I asked him to let me try it on my walkway. He said, "Go ahead, but you're going to want your own." He was right.

I pointed the Seese Pro at the pile of wet oak leaves cemented to my concrete walkway. I pulled the trigger. The leaves didn't just move — they flew. It was like someone had turned on a jet engine in my hand. In under five minutes, my entire walkway was clean. No bending. No sweating. No pain. I stood there, genuinely confused. How was something this small doing this? I've owned gas blowers, electric blowers, shop vacs with blower attachments. None of them felt like this. The brushless motor spins so fast it compresses air the way a turbine does — Dave explained it's why drones can hover despite weighing so little. The engineering is the same, just pointed sideways instead of down. I handed it back to Dave. That night, I ordered my own.

The Seese Pro arrived three days later in a compact carrying case. Inside: the blower, a 21-volt lithium-ion battery, a charger, and — this made me laugh — a pair of ear plugs. I charged the battery, walked outside, and did something I hadn't done in years: I cleaned my entire property in one session. The driveway. The patio. The garage floor. The porch steps. Forty minutes of battery life, and I used maybe twenty-five. But here's what I didn't expect: this thing isn't just a leaf blower. The next morning, I washed my truck and used the Seese Pro to dry it — completely contactless, no chamois, no water spots. In January, I used it to blast frost off my windshield in about 90 seconds. I've used it to clear sawdust from my workshop, blow out the dryer vent, and clean between the seats of my car. Four tools I used to own separately, replaced by one device that fits in a kitchen drawer.

I'm not an engineer. I can't explain the physics beyond what Dave told me. But I can tell you what I've noticed after three months of using it. My back doesn't hurt on Saturdays anymore. That's not a small thing — Linda noticed before I did. I'm not avoiding the yard. I actually look forward to grabbing the Seese Pro after my morning coffee and doing a quick five-minute sweep. The leaves don't pile up. The gutters stay clear. The patio looks like it belongs to someone who cares. And the weight — 950 grams — means Linda uses it too. She's 62, has mild arthritis in her wrists, and she asked me to stop helping her clean the back deck because she'd rather do it herself now. That sentence alone would've sounded insane six months ago.

I've since learned a few things about the Seese Pro that I didn't know when I bought it. The brushless motor has no internal friction, which means it lasts roughly three times longer than a standard motor — no brushes to wear down, no carbon dust clogging the internals. The company offers a lifetime replacement warranty, which tells me they trust the engineering. And there's a 30-day trial: if you go back to your old blower, they refund you, no questions. I haven't looked at my gas blower since October. It's still sitting in the corner of my garage, collecting the dust it used to blow. I keep meaning to sell it. But honestly, I think I'll keep it as a reminder — a 22-pound monument to how unnecessarily hard I was making things.

If you're reading this and it sounds familiar — the heavy tools, the sore back, the weekends you keep putting off — I get it. I was there for years. I'm not going to tell you the Seese Pro will change your life. That sounds like a commercial, and this isn't one. But I will tell you this: the first time you hold it, you'll think it's a toy. The first time you pull the trigger, you'll realize it isn't. And the first Saturday morning you finish your yard in ten minutes with no pain, you'll wonder why you waited so long. Dave still asks me if anyone else on the street has bought one. Last count: four of us. The Husqvarna owners are the hardest to convince. But they always come around after they see it work.
