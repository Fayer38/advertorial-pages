I Tested 5 'Lightweight' Cordless Leaf Blowers On My Husband's Bad Shoulder — The Cheapest One Won

A retired principal from Raleigh turned her driveway into a testing lab to find a cordless blower her husband could actually use after shoulder replacement surgery. This is what she found — and why she called her sister the same night.

April 21, 2026 · From the desk of Diane Hollister

Dear friend,

If you've landed on this page because someone you love had surgery — or because your own shoulders and back aren't what they were at 45 — please take five minutes and read this before you add another "lightweight" cordless blower to your Amazon cart.

I spent three weeks, about $600 of my own money, and one very confused 9-year-old granddaughter on this test. I genuinely wish somebody had done it for me first.

I'm Diane. I Was Not Supposed to Become a Leaf Blower Expert.

My name is Diane Hollister. I'm 58, a retired elementary school principal in Raleigh, North Carolina, and I am absolutely not the person you'd expect to turn her own driveway into a power tool testing lab. But last October, six weeks after my husband Gary had his right shoulder replaced, I stood at the kitchen window and watched him fight his way through a pile of wet oak leaves with the same 11-pound gas blower he's been hauling out of the garage since 2004. He wouldn't ask for help. He wouldn't stop. He just kept switching hands and lifting the nozzle with his good arm, trying to pretend his body hadn't just been through a surgeon's saw forty-two days earlier.

That night I told him — calmly, because principals don't raise their voices — that the gas blower was going to the church yard sale, and that I was going to find him a replacement he could actually hold. He looked at me over his reading glasses and said, "Good luck, Di. They're all too heavy or too weak."

He wasn't wrong, and that was the problem. If you've shopped for a cordless blower recently, you know what I'm talking about. Every listing on Amazon calls itself "ultra-light," "ergonomic," "senior-friendly." And when you actually hold them, half feel like a bag of wet sand, and the other half couldn't move a damp napkin off the sidewalk. I wanted Gary to have his independence back before Christmas — to be able to keep doing the yard he's been doing for thirty years without me hovering like a nurse. So on a Tuesday night, I ordered five of them at once.

The Driveway, a Kitchen Scale, and a 9-Year-Old Named Maddie

I'm methodical by nature — twenty-eight years as a principal will do that to you — so I decided I wasn't going to rely on marketing copy or four-star averages from strangers on the internet. I was going to measure.

I ordered the five most-recommended cordless leaf blowers for people with shoulder or back issues: two Ryobi models (the 40V Whisper and the 18V ONE+), a Worx WG545, a Greenworks 24V, and a brand Gary had never heard of called the Seese Pro. The total damage to my credit card was about $600. My plan, honestly, was to keep the one that won and return the other four.

Then I set up the test the way you'd set up a kindergarten field day. I weighed each blower on my kitchen scale — because "3.5 lbs" on a spec sheet can mean 5.8 lbs once you snap the battery in and add the tube. I timed how long Gary could hold each one extended at arm's length before his post-surgery shoulder started trembling. I clocked how fast each one cleared a 20-foot strip of our driveway packed with wet oak leaves. And I added one more test I've since started calling the "grandkid test" — could our 9-year-old granddaughter Maddie, who was visiting that weekend, actually lift the blower off the grass, press the trigger, and move a pile of leaves with it?

My sister Linda, over in Asheville, thought I'd finally lost it. "You're scoring leaf blowers like you're running the PTA science fair, Di," she said on the phone. She wasn't wrong. I was. And the results genuinely surprised me.

What Actually Happened When Gary Picked Each One Up

I want to be fair here, because each of these blowers has real strengths — and if your situation is different from ours, one of the others might still be the right pick for you.

The Ryobi 40V Whisper had the best raw power of the group. It cleared the driveway strip in forty-one seconds, faster than anything else I tested. But it weighed in at 8.4 lbs with the battery, and Gary's arm was shaking noticeably by the 90-second mark. Maddie couldn't hold it up off the ground at all.

The Ryobi 18V ONE+ was much lighter — about 5.2 lbs — but it just didn't have the airflow to handle wet Carolina leaves. Gary described it politely as "a hairdryer with commitment issues." Fine for a clean sidewalk; useless for October.

The Worx WG545 looked the lightest on paper, but its handle twisted Gary's wrist into an angle that his healing rotator cuff wasn't ready for. He set it down in under a minute. "Whoever designed this never held one with a bad shoulder," he said quietly.

The Greenworks 24V was the one I genuinely expected to win — solid brand, good reviews, reasonable weight. But the trigger required a harder squeeze than Gary's post-surgical hand was comfortable with, and the battery clicked into place at an awkward angle that forced him to brace with his operated arm. Little design choices like that matter a lot when you're six weeks out of the OR.

And then there was the Seese Pro — the one I almost didn't order, because it was the cheapest and I'd never heard of the brand. Gary picked it up, held it out at full arm's length, and the first thing he said — and I will never forget this, because he was genuinely surprised — was, "Di. This is lighter than my coffee mug."

The Unexpected Winner, By the Numbers

Here's what my stopwatch, my kitchen scale, and three weekends of testing on an actual driveway, by an actual post-op shoulder, told me.

The Seese Pro came in at the lowest real-world pick-up weight of the five blowers, once battery and tube were attached. Gary could hold it extended at arm's length for over four minutes before his shoulder started to fatigue — more than double any other blower in the test. It cleared the 20-foot wet-leaf strip in forty-seven seconds, just six seconds slower than the big Ryobi, but without the arm-shake afterward. And yes — Maddie passed the grandkid test on the first try. She ran around the back yard blasting leaves off the porch like she was conducting a small orchestra.

The things I didn't expect to love, but did: one single button to start it (no priming, no pull cord, no app pairing, no Bluetooth anything), a trigger soft enough that Gary's still-healing hand could squeeze it without tensing his whole arm, and a battery that clicked in with a satisfying snap on the first try — every time. The whole thing is also quiet enough that our neighbor Frank, who has hated our old gas blower with the heat of a thousand suns since 2009, came over on a Saturday morning and asked where we got it.

That night I called Linda in Asheville. I told her, "Lin, the cheapest one won. I'm keeping it and returning the other four in the morning." There was a long pause and she said, "Order me one too, Di. Mike's knees are going."

Gary has now used the Seese Pro every Saturday since the end of October. His shoulder is healed. His pride is intact. He still does the yard himself — which, if you're reading this on behalf of someone you love, you already know was the entire point.
