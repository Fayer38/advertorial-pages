Six Years of Waking Up and Wondering: Is Today Going to Be a Bad Day?

My name is Linda. I'm 64, retired after 31 years of teaching third grade in Charlotte, NC. And for the last six years, degenerative disc disease has been slowly stealing the life I planned for retirement.

You know what the cruelest part is? It doesn't hit you all at once. It takes things away one by one.

First it was the morning walks with my neighbor Pat. Then it was gardening — I couldn't kneel, couldn't bend, couldn't even stand long enough to water my tomatoes. Then it was the grandkids. Try explaining to a five-year-old why Grandma can't get on the floor to play.

The pain wasn't always a sharp scream. Most days it was a deep, grinding ache that sat in my lower back like a fist that never unclenched. Some mornings I'd swing my legs out of bed and just… sit there. Waiting for my spine to decide if today would be manageable or miserable.

Warning: Research shows that the small stabilizing muscles around the spine (the multifidus) can atrophy by up to 80% in patients with chronic lower back pain — and most conventional treatments do nothing to reverse it. This muscle wasting is often the hidden reason pain keeps coming back.

Richard — my husband, the retired surgeon — watched all of this happen. And for the first time in our 38-year marriage, he didn't have an answer.

I Tried Everything My Doctors Recommended. Nothing Lasted.

When you're married to an orthopedic surgeon, you don't lack for referrals. Richard called in every favor he had.

Over six years, here's what I went through:

  • Chiropractic adjustments — three different chiropractors, twice a week for months. I'd feel better for a day or two. Then right back where I started.
  • Pain management clinic — epidural steroid injections. The first one gave me about six weeks of relief. The second one, maybe three. The third barely touched it.
  • Physical therapy — two separate rounds. Helpful while I was going, but the moment I stopped, the pain crept right back.
  • Medications — muscle relaxants that made me foggy, anti-inflammatories that upset my stomach, and one doctor who wanted to put me on gabapentin long-term. Richard talked me out of that one.

I spent over $14,000 out of pocket in those six years. And every single treatment did the same thing: masked the pain temporarily without fixing what was actually wrong.

By last spring, my pain management doctor sat me down and said the words I'd been dreading: "Linda, I think it's time we talk about surgical options."

Richard was quiet on the drive home. I knew that silence. It was the silence of a man who'd seen too many spinal surgeries go sideways — even the ones he'd performed himself.

He'd found a clinical study on NMES therapy — Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation — specifically targeting the multifidus muscles. These are the tiny, deep stabilizing muscles that run along your spine. They're the muscles that hold everything in place.

Here's what Richard explained to me, and it finally made everything click:

When you have chronic back pain, those multifidus muscles start to atrophy. They waste away. Your body essentially stops using them because it's trying to protect you from pain. But without those muscles doing their job, your spine loses its support system. The discs take more pressure. The nerves get compressed. The pain gets worse.

And here's the part that frustrated Richard most as a surgeon: none of the treatments I'd been getting addressed this. Not the injections. Not the chiropractic. Not even the physical therapy — because by the time muscles have atrophied that severely, standard exercises can't recruit them effectively.

"It's like trying to flex a muscle that's fallen asleep," Richard said. "Your brain can't find it anymore. But electrical stimulation can."
