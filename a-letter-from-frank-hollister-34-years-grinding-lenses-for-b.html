A Letter From Frank Hollister: 34 Years Grinding Lenses For Binoculars That Retailed For $1,400. I'm Done Watching My Friends Get Fleeced.

After three decades inside one of America's last optical manufacturers, a retired Rochester lens engineer finally tells the truth about what you're actually paying for when you buy a pair of Austrian or German binoculars — and what he built in his garage to do something about it.

April 19, 2026 · From the desk of Editorial Team

Dear friend,

My name is Frank Hollister. I'm 67 years old, I live in Henrietta, just outside Rochester, and for 34 years I stood at a polishing bench grinding objective lenses for binoculars that retailed at Cabela's for fourteen hundred dollars a pair.

I'm writing this today because my brother-in-law Dennis just handed over eighteen hundred dollars for a pair of Austrian binoculars to watch warblers in his backyard, and I can't stay quiet about it anymore.

Thirty-four years at the bench

I started at the plant in 1988. I was 29, I had two kids under five, and I'd just taken a job grinding objective lenses on the night shift. The plant is still there on the west side of Rochester — I won't name it, because the people who still work there are good people and I don't want to make their lives harder. What I will tell you is this: we made glass for some of the most recognizable optics brands on the American market. The kind you see on the shelf at Bass Pro with a price tag that makes you wince.

I spent my twenties learning prism alignment. My thirties learning coating chemistry — the fully multi-coated layers that take a lens from "okay" to tack-sharp edge-to-edge. My forties and fifties supervising younger techs on the ED glass line. I know what a BAK4 prism stack looks like under a collimator. I know the difference between a lens that passed QC and one that got buffed and shipped anyway because the foreman needed the numbers that month.

And the whole time I was there — for 34 years — I watched the markup happen. We'd ship a finished 12x42 out the loading dock for somewhere around ninety dollars a unit, all in. Glass, housing, rubber armor, nitrogen purge, the whole thing. Six months later I'd walk into a sporting goods store on a Saturday and see that same pair — my work, my bench, my polish — sitting in a display case with a $1,400 sticker on it.

The first time it happened I thought I was reading the tag wrong. The hundredth time, I just walked out of the store.

The day Dennis spent $1,800

I retired in the spring of 2022. Drove home with my last paycheck, a plaque, and a quiet kind of anger I didn't know what to do with yet.

About six months later my brother-in-law Dennis — retired dentist, Finger Lakes house, serious about his warblers — called me up bragging. He'd just dropped $1,800 on a pair of Austrian 10x42s. "Frank, you've got to come over and look through these. The glass is unbelievable."

I drove up the next weekend. He handed them to me on his back deck. I lifted them to my eyes, focused on a cedar waxwing about forty yards out, and my stomach dropped.

Because the glass was unbelievable. It was also, as far as I could tell from the way the image sat in the field, the same prism geometry and the same coating profile I had been grinding for three decades. The housing was nicer. The leather strap was fancy. The logo was stamped in a European font. But the actual optical stack — the part that makes the image — was no better than what we shipped out of Rochester for ninety bucks a unit.

I handed them back and I didn't say anything. You don't tell your brother-in-law he just got taken for $1,700. But I drove home that night and I couldn't sleep. I pulled out an old spiral notebook around 2 a.m. and I started drawing.

What I built in my garage

What I designed that night — and spent the next fourteen months sourcing and spec'ing — is a straightforward 12x42 roof-prism binocular. No gimmicks. No app. No "smart" anything. Just the same prism stack, the same coating package, and the same build tolerances I spent my career enforcing.

Here's what's inside, in plain English:

BAK4 roof prisms, phase-corrected. That's the Barium Crown glass that gives you the round, bright exit pupil instead of the squared-off dim one you get from BK7 junk. Fully multi-coated ED glass on every air-to-glass surface — that's sixteen surfaces on a roof-prism design, and every one of them gets the coating, not just the front two like the cheap pairs pretend. Nitrogen-purged and O-ring sealed, so they don't fog when you walk from a warm cabin into a 28-degree November morning. Magnesium alloy chassis, not plastic. Rubber armor you can actually grip with wet gloves on.

I had them built by a contract optical house I know personally — a shop I'd visited three times over the years on QC trips when I was still at the plant. They're not a "factory in Asia" in the way the marketing guys mean it when they wave their hands. They're a specific shop, with specific techs, and they've been making premium-grade optics for thirty years. The difference is that when I order from them directly, there's no American importer taking their cut, no sporting goods chain marking it up 3x, no rep commission, no glossy catalog mailed to 2 million households.

I sell them out of a small warehouse in Henrietta, about a fifteen-minute drive from my house. My daughter helps me pack orders on Saturdays. My price is $89.

Dennis owns three pairs now. One for the Finger Lakes house, one for the truck, one he lent to his grandson and never got back. He still has the Austrian pair. He says he keeps them in the case on a shelf because they're "fancy." He uses mine.

Why I'm writing this today

I'm not a marketing guy. I'm not trying to build a brand. I don't have a social media team and I don't know what a "funnel" is. I'm a lens guy from Rochester who's tired of watching good people — people like you, probably, if you've read this far — get talked into spending a month's Social Security check on a pair of binoculars that cost ninety bucks to make.

Here's what I want you to understand, because nobody inside the industry will tell you this out loud: the optical stack in a $1,400 pair and the optical stack in a well-spec'd $89 pair is, for 95% of what you'd ever use binoculars for, functionally identical. You're not paying for better glass at $1,400. You're paying for a century of brand equity, a European factory's labor costs, a U.S. importer's margin, a retail chain's 2.5x markup, and a catalog photo shoot in the Alps. That's the whole racket.

A letter came in last month that's been sitting on my desk. I'll share a piece of it because it's why I'm still doing this instead of fishing on Canandaigua Lake like I'm supposed to be.

"Frank — I'm 71, I've been birding since I was a Boy Scout in 1964, and I've always wanted a Swarovski EL. I could never justify it on a retired teacher's pension. Got your 12x42s three weeks ago. Took them to Montezuma last Saturday and picked a male Cerulean out of the canopy at about 180 feet — blue crown, white belly, the works. I cried in the parking lot. Not because of the bird. Because I'd waited 40 years to see one that clearly and it cost me $89."
— Letter from Raymond H., 71, retired schoolteacher, Seneca Falls NY

I get letters like Raymond's almost every week now. I keep them in a shoebox in the warehouse. Some days, when the packing gets tedious and my back hurts and I wonder why a 67-year-old man is running a small business out of a rented bay in Henrietta instead of reading in his recliner, I open the shoebox and I read two or three of them. And I keep going.
