My name is Paul Lucas. I am sixty-seven, and I make wallets in a small workshop in Weatherford, Texas.

The building was bought by my grandfather in 1948. I have worked inside it, in one form or another, since I was fourteen years old.

I am writing this letter myself because I want you to hear it from me, and not from someone hired to sell on my behalf.

There are four hundred wallets left. When they are gone, I will close the door on the workshop, and that will be the end of it.

The workshop, the building, and the man who taught me

The shop is nine hundred square feet. There is one window facing east, a workbench my father built, and a coffee pot that runs all day.

My grandfather paid cash for this building in 1948. He sold harness and tack to ranchers driving cattle through Parker County after the war.

My father took it over in 1962 and turned it toward smaller goods — belts, billfolds, knife sheaths. He worked here until he was seventy.

I came in beside him at fourteen, sweeping floors, then cutting scrap, then stitching. By twenty I was making wallets on my own bench.

Fifty-three years later, I still come in at six in the morning. The light is good, and the building still smells the way it did then.

Why I am finally closing the workshop after fifty-three long years

Three years ago, my hands started locking up in the mornings. The doctor in Fort Worth used a word I had been waiting on.

Rheumatoid arthritis. The same thing that took my father off the bench at seventy. I have watched this story before, from the other side of the room.

The mornings are the hardest. I run hot water over my knuckles for ten minutes before I can hold a needle.

Saddle stitching, which is two needles working against each other, takes a steady grip. My grip is no longer steady all day.

Some afternoons I can finish four wallets. Some afternoons I can finish one and then I sit down.

Cold weather makes it worse. Texas does not get much cold, but when it comes, the bench goes quiet.

My doctor has been honest with me. He said I have perhaps another year of fine work left, maybe a little more if I am careful.

I have no apprentice, and I did not train one. That was a decision I made twenty years ago, and I do not regret it, but it ends the line.

When the last wallet ships, the bench goes quiet, and the building becomes a building again. That is the truth of it.

Last spring, a buyer drove out from Nordstrom in a rental SUV

He was polite. He had done his homework. He knew my father's name, and he knew the year I had taken over the bench from him.

He sat across from me at this same workbench and made an offer that, on paper, was generous for a man in my position.

Sixty-five dollars per wallet. A run of two thousand units, paid up front, delivered over eighteen months.

He showed me the hangtag they had already designed. It said handcrafted in Texas, in a serif font, in brown ink on cream cardstock.

He told me, plainly, that they would retail each wallet at two hundred and twenty-nine dollars on the floor in their flagship stores.

The math is not complicated. He would more than triple the money on each wallet, and my name would be the reason.

I asked him a few questions. Where the displays would sit. Who would write the copy. Whether I would ever meet a single buyer.

Then I told him no, walked him out to his rental, shook his hand, and came back inside and sat down with my wife Linda.

What I told Linda that afternoon at the kitchen table

I said I would rather sell the last four hundred wallets directly, to four hundred men who would actually carry them, than ship two thousand into a department store.

I am not against department stores. I am against my name on a hangtag selling for triple, when the man writing the check has never met me.

  • I want each wallet to go to a person who chose it on purpose. Not someone who picked it off a shelf because it was the closest one.
  • I want a man to carry it for ten years, twenty years, until the leather is as soft as a work glove and shaped to his back pocket.
  • I want the price to be a fair one. Not a markup paying for retail rent in a city I have never visited.
  • I want to write the description myself. I want to answer the email if something is wrong. I want it to be my hand, end to end.
  • Linda listened, drank her coffee, and said, well, Paul, that sounds about right. She has heard worse ideas from me over the years.

Six leather colors and nine thread colors, fifty-four unique combinations total

If you would like your initials embossed on the inside flap, I will do that at the bench, by hand, with brass letter stamps my father bought in 1971.

What separates a hand-stitched wallet from an ordinary factory wallet today

A factory wallet is sewn by a single thread on a sewing machine. When one stitch breaks, the whole row begins to unravel within weeks.

Saddle stitching uses two needles working in opposite directions through the same hole. If one thread fails, the other holds the seam closed.

It takes me about five times longer to stitch a wallet by hand. That is the entire difference, and it is not a small one.

About the leather itself, and where it really comes from today

I use full-grain cowhide from a tannery in Pennsylvania. It is the top layer of the hide, with the natural grain still showing through.

Cheaper wallets use what is called genuine leather, which is the bottom split, sanded smooth and coated in plastic to look like the real thing.

Full-grain leather darkens, softens, and takes on the shape of the hand and the pocket that carries it. That is the patina people talk about.
