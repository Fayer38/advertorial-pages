Nobody starts a power tool company because they want to.

Greg Seese, 60, started one because he watched his father cry.

His dad, 84-year-old Walt, had maintained his half-acre property in Marietta, Georgia, with military precision for 40 years. Every leaf blown. Every walkway spotless. Every Saturday morning like clockwork.

But after a double knee replacement in 2021, Walt couldn't operate his Stihl backpack blower anymore. Twenty-three pounds of machine strapped to a body that had finally said enough.

The afternoon Greg found him sitting on the porch — tears running down his face, leaves blanketing the yard he'd kept spotless since 1983 — something broke open.

"He wasn't crying about the leaves," Greg told me. "He was crying because he felt useless. The yard was the last thing he still controlled. And now even that was gone."

The Problem Nobody Talks About: When Your Tools Turn Against You

Walt's story isn't unique. It's just the version most people never say out loud.

Across America, millions of homeowners over 50 quietly stop doing the things they love — not because they've lost interest, but because their tools have become heavier than their bodies can handle.

The gas blower that used to feel like nothing now sends shooting pain through your lower back after ten minutes. The leaf rake that was "just exercise" now means two days of stiffness. The push broom that cleared the patio in college now leaves your shoulders screaming.

Warning: According to the Consumer Product Safety Commission, over 47,000 Americans are treated in emergency rooms each year for injuries related to powered garden tools — with back and shoulder strain being the leading cause among adults over 55.

And here's the part nobody wants to admit: it's not just physical pain. It's identity loss.

For Walt, that yard wasn't landscaping. It was proof he was still capable. Still sharp. Still the man who raised a family and kept everything running. When he couldn't maintain it anymore, a piece of who he was went with it.

Greg saw that. And it haunted him.

What Most People Try — And Why None of It Works

Before Greg picked up a wrench, he tried every solution already on the market for his father.

Lighter electric blowers? They exist — but they sacrifice so much power that wet leaves laugh at them. Walt tried one. It moved dry dust on a good day. Wet oak leaves on Georgia concrete? Forget it.

Hiring a lawn service? Greg offered to pay for one. Walt refused. "I don't need someone to do my yard. I need to be able to do my own yard." It wasn't about the leaves. It was about independence.

Rakes and brooms? Walt's physical therapist shut that idea down immediately. Repetitive bending and twisting motions after double knee replacement? That's how you end up back in the operating room.

Smaller battery blowers from the big brands? Greg bought three of them. The lightest still weighed over 7 pounds — and produced barely enough airflow to clear a doorstep. As Greg put it: "They made it lighter by making it useless."

Every option on the market forced the same impossible choice: power or comfort. Never both.

That's when Greg's engineering brain kicked in.

"I Compress Air at 40,000 Feet. Why Can't I Do It in a Driveway?"

Here's what most people don't know about Greg Seese: for 28 years, he designed turbine compression systems for aerospace applications. His specialty was moving massive volumes of air through impossibly small spaces — the exact physics problem behind every leaf blower ever made.

The difference? The aerospace industry solved this problem decades ago. The outdoor power tool industry never bothered.

"Gas blowers still use motor technology from the 1970s," Greg explained. "They compensate for inefficient airflow design by adding brute force — bigger motors, more weight, more fuel. It's like building a faster car by strapping on a bigger engine instead of improving the aerodynamics."

Greg took a different approach. He borrowed brushless turbine motor technology from the drone industry — the same motors that keep 50-pound commercial drones stable in 40 MPH crosswinds — and engineered a focused airflow system that produces 200+ MPH jet speed and 1,200 CFM from a unit that weighs just 2.1 pounds.

Eight months. Forty-seven prototypes. Countless failures.

But prototype number forty-eight worked.

He called it the Seese Pro.
