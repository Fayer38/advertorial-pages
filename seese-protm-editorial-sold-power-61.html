I'm going to tell you something most company founders would never admit.

Two years ago, I was bankrupt. Flat broke. I'd lost my shop, sold off every piece of inventory I had, and was staring at a stack of hospital bills I couldn't pay.

A rear-end collision on I-75 had done what 22 years of hauling heavy equipment never quite managed to finish — it wrecked my back for good. L4-L5 herniation. The surgeon said I'd never lift more than 10 pounds comfortably again.

And here's the cruel irony: I'd spent my entire career selling the very tools that were destroying people's bodies.

My name is David, and this is the story of how losing everything led me to build the Seese Pro — a 2.1-pound cordless blower that now does the job of machines five times its weight. But I didn't build it to start a company. I built it because on a Saturday morning last spring, I couldn't even clean my own driveway.

The Problem I Saw for 22 Years (And Ignored)

For over two decades, I ran a power equipment shop in central Florida. I sold every brand you can name — Stihl, Husqvarna, Echo, Ryobi, the works.

And every single fall, I watched the same thing happen.

Guys in their 50s and 60s would walk in looking for a leaf blower. They'd pick up a gas model, feel the weight, wince a little, and buy it anyway. Because what choice did they have?

Then they'd come back. Not to buy more — to complain. "My shoulders are killing me." "I can only use it for 10 minutes before my back locks up." "My wife can't even start the damn thing."

I'd nod, sympathize, and sell them a "lighter" model that still weighed 15 pounds. That was my job.

Warning: Standard gas-powered blowers weigh between 15 and 25 lbs. Repeated use places enormous strain on your lower back, shoulders, and wrists — especially after age 50 when disc hydration and muscle recovery decline sharply.

After my accident, when I could barely walk to my mailbox without pain, I finally understood what my customers had been telling me for years.

The tools we accept as "normal" are slowly wrecking our bodies. We just don't notice until one day, we can't pick them up anymore.

I Tried Every "Solution" on the Market. None of Them Worked.

After the accident, my physical therapist gave me one rule: nothing over 10 pounds in either hand.

So I tried to find a blower I could actually use. Here's what I discovered:

  • Battery-powered blowers from the big brands? Still 8–12 lbs. Better than gas, but my back didn't care about "better" — it cared about light enough.
  • Handheld electric dusters? Lightweight, sure. But they couldn't move a wet leaf off concrete if their life depended on it. Toys, not tools.
  • Hiring a lawn service? $150 a month for someone to do what used to take me 20 minutes. My pride — and my bank account — couldn't handle it.

I was stuck. Too hurt to use real tools. Too stubborn to give up.

And that's when something clicked.

I'd spent 22 years selling blowers. I knew every motor, every impeller design, every airflow spec in the industry. If nobody was building what I needed, maybe I had to build it myself.

Building the Seese Pro From a Hospital Bed (Almost Literally)

I'll spare you the long version. Here's the short one:

I borrowed money from my sister. I partnered with two engineers who specialized in brushless motor technology — the same kind used in commercial drones and electric vehicles. And I gave them one design brief:

"Build me a blower that weighs under a kilogram, hits 200 MPH, and can be used with one hand by someone with a bad back. If my 68-year-old neighbor can't use it for 30 minutes straight, we start over."

It took 14 months and more prototypes than I care to count.

But we did it.

The Seese Pro weighs 950 grams — just 2.1 pounds. That's lighter than a bottle of wine. It pushes air at 200 MPH with 1,200 CFM and 30,000+ RPM — numbers that match or beat gas blowers costing $300 or more.

And the brushless motor? No carbon brush friction means it lasts up to 3x longer than conventional motors. It's the same technology principle behind Tesla's drivetrain — miniaturized and tuned for airflow.

I Didn't Just Build a Blower. I Accidentally Replaced 4 Tools.

Here's something I didn't expect.

I designed the Seese Pro to blow leaves. That was the goal. But once people started using it, they stopped reaching for three other tools too:

  • Car drying towels — The Seese Pro dries a freshly washed car in under 3 minutes. No contact, no micro-scratches, no wringing out chamois cloths. Just point and blow.

  • Ice scrapers — A blast of warm-ish high-speed air clears light frost from a windshield in seconds. No scraping, no cold hands.

One tool. Four jobs. That wasn't the plan — but it's why people keep telling me, "I use this thing every single day."
