Why Most People Just Give Up and Pay

Here's the truth about washing your car at home: it was never actually convenient for most people.

Traditional pressure washers need an outdoor faucet. A nearby power outlet. Thirty feet of clear space. Ten to twenty minutes of setup before you spray a single inch of car. And if something isn't quite right — the hose kinks, the outlet trips the breaker, the water pressure from your tap is too low — the whole thing falls apart before you've started.

If you live in a condo, an apartment, a house where the outdoor spigot is around back while your car is in front — forget it. The friction is enough to make you give up every single time.

And if you've looked at a full-sized pressure washer at a hardware store, you already know the problem: these machines are built for people with workshops and dedicated outdoor spaces. They weigh 30 to 70 pounds. They need a garden hose connection. They're overkill for a car wash and complete overkill to lug out for a 20-minute job.

Meanwhile, the car wash industry quietly collects $432 a year from the average two-car household. Not because people love going. Because the alternative has always been too much hassle.

The problem was never your motivation. It was that no tool existed that actually matched how most people live.
