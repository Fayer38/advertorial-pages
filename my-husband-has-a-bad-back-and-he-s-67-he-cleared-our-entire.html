Last Saturday, I watched my husband, Frank, blow every single leaf off our 60-foot driveway.

He did it in five minutes. With one hand. Without a single wince.

If you knew what the last three years have been like for us, you'd understand why I cried a little watching him do it.

Frank is 67. He's had two cortisone shots in his lower back. His L4-L5 disc has been a problem since 2021. And for the past year and a half, our leaf blower — a 23-pound Craftsman gas monster — has been collecting dust in the garage.

Not because it doesn't work. Because Frank physically can't use it anymore.

When "Just Blow the Leaves" Becomes a Medical Decision

It sounds dramatic, I know. It's just yard work.

But here's what people who've never had back problems don't understand: it's not the task — it's the tool.

Every autumn, the same routine. Frank would strap on the gas blower, hunch forward under its weight, and spend 30 to 40 minutes fighting our yard. By the time he was done, he couldn't sit down for the rest of the evening. Some weekends, he'd be stiff for two days.

Last fall, he threw his back out so badly he missed our granddaughter's soccer game. He was lying on the couch with a heating pad while I drove alone. That was the moment something broke — not in his back, but in his pride.

He stopped going outside on weekends. The driveway stayed dirty. The patio looked neglected. And every time I mentioned it, he'd just say: "I'll get to it."

He never got to it.

Everything We Tried (And Why None of It Worked)

I tried to help. I really did.

First, I bought him a lighter electric blower — a corded one. It was lighter, sure, but the cord was a nightmare. He kept tripping on it, and it barely had enough power to move wet leaves. He used it twice and gave up.

Then I suggested we hire a yard service. $85 every two weeks. Frank hated it — not just the cost, but the feeling of paying someone to do something he'd done his whole life. "I'm not an invalid, Diane," he said.

I bought him a backpack blower thinking it would distribute the weight better. It weighed 19 pounds. His back still hurt. And it was so loud, our neighbor Margaret came over to complain.

We even tried the old-fashioned approach. A rake. A broom. But at 67 with disc problems, bending and sweeping for 30 minutes is just a different kind of torture.

So we were stuck. Too proud to hire help. Too hurt to do it ourselves.

The driveway kept getting dirtier. And Frank kept getting quieter about it.
