THE NUMBNESS DIDN'T START LOUD — IT STARTED AT 2AM

It was 2:14 in the morning when Patricia Whitaker, 69, finally typed the words she had been avoiding for weeks: diabetic neuropathy reversible. Her husband Ray was asleep down the hall. The tingling in her left foot had spread to her right.

For five years her A1C had been creeping upward — from 5.9 to 6.7 — even after she gave up bread, pasta, and her beloved peach cobbler. Nothing had moved the number. Now her toes were going numb when she stood up.

Her uncle Hollis lost two toes to this disease in 2009. He spent his last six years in a wheelchair. Patty hadn't told Ray. She was terrified of saying it out loud — terrified Dr. Reynolds would reach for the prescription pad.

Doctors call the years between an A1C of 5.7 and 7.5 the silent progression window. The number drifts. Energy fades. Afternoon brain fog rolls in around two o'clock. Most women blame age, sleep, stress, or the weather.

What is actually happening is more measurable. Each meal sends a glucose surge through the bloodstream. The pancreas works harder. Insulin sensitivity dulls. Over months and years, small nerve fibers in the feet begin losing their steady fuel supply.

The body whispers before it shouts. A faint tingle at night. A patch of numbness when standing. A pins-and-needles feeling that comes and goes — easy to dismiss, easy to blame on the recliner. By the time it stays, the damage is harder to undo.

Patty understood this the way most women her age understand it — through someone they had loved and watched decline. Uncle Hollis. The friend at church. The sister who lost her eyesight first, then her independence, then everything else.
