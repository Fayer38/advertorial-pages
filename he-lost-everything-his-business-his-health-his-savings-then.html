Two years ago, Ray Callister was lying in a hospital bed with three cracked vertebrae, a pile of medical bills he couldn't pay, and a bankruptcy notice pinned to his refrigerator at home.

He'd just sold the last of his equipment — including four commercial-grade blowers — to cover what the insurance wouldn't.

"I remember staring at the ceiling and thinking: I spent 30 years in the outdoor power tool business, and the very machines I sold are the ones that wrecked my body," Ray told me from his small workshop in Clearwater, Florida. "Every contractor I knew had the same story. Bad backs. Sore shoulders. Numb hands. We just accepted it as part of the job."

But lying in that hospital bed, unable to move, Ray decided he was done accepting it.

What he built next — from a folding table in a rented garage — is now the fastest-growing handheld blower in North America. And it weighs less than a bag of sugar.

The Problem Nobody Talks About — Until Your Back Gives Out

Here's something the outdoor tool industry doesn't advertise: their products are hurting the people who use them most.

The average gas-powered leaf blower weighs between 18 and 25 pounds. Strap it on your back or hold it at arm's length for 30 minutes, and you're putting enormous strain on your spine, shoulders, and wrists.

Now do that every weekend for 20 years.

Warning: According to orthopedic specialists, repetitive use of heavy power tools is one of the leading causes of chronic lower back pain in adults over 50. The cumulative damage often doesn't show up until the injury is already severe.

Ray knew this better than anyone. Before the accident that put him in the hospital, he'd already been living with chronic back pain for over a decade.

"Every Saturday was the same ritual," he said. "I'd stare at the gas blower in the garage and think: do I really want to deal with this today? The weight. The yanking to start it. The vibration shooting up my arms. Half the time, I'd just skip it and let the leaves pile up."

And Ray was a professional. Imagine the millions of homeowners — people in their 50s, 60s, 70s — who are going through the exact same thing every single weekend.

"I Tried Everything. Nothing Worked."

After the accident, Ray couldn't lift anything over five pounds for months. But the leaves kept falling. The driveway kept getting dirty. His car still needed drying after every wash.

So he tried what everyone tries.

A lightweight electric blower. It weighed 9 pounds — better than 23, but still too heavy to hold for more than a few minutes with his injuries. And the power? Barely enough to move dry leaves on a calm day.

A leaf vacuum. Even heavier. And it clogged every ten minutes.

A handheld air duster. Fine for a keyboard. Useless on a patio.

Hiring a service. $150 a month. That's $1,800 a year — money Ray didn't have. Not after the bankruptcy. Not with hospital bills still coming in.

"I was 58 years old, broke, recovering from a car accident, and I couldn't even clean my own front porch," Ray said. "That's the moment that broke me more than the accident ever did. It wasn't the pain. It was the helplessness."

And that's what most people don't understand. It's not just about blowing leaves. It's about independence. It's about being able to take care of your own home without asking for help, without paying someone, without dreading it all week.

A Folding Table, a Rented Garage, and an Idea That Wouldn't Let Go

Recovery is a strange thing. It gives you time you never wanted.

For Ray, it gave him six months of sitting still — and a brain that wouldn't stop engineering.

"I kept thinking about drone motors," he told me. "These tiny brushless motors that spin at insane speeds. They power machines that fly. Why can't they power a blower that actually works — without weighing 20 pounds?"

Ray had no money for a lab. No employees. No factory. Just a folding table in a rented garage, a secondhand 3D printer, and 30 years of experience in how air moves.

He started building prototypes. The first fourteen were failures. Too weak. Too loud. Too fragile.

But prototype number fifteen was different.

It used a brushless turbine motor — the same technology found in high-end drones and electric vehicles — spinning at 30,000 RPM. It pushed air at 200 miles per hour through a precision-engineered nozzle.

And it weighed 950 grams. Less than 2.1 pounds.

"I took it out to my driveway and pointed it at a pile of wet oak leaves that had been sitting there for a week," Ray recalled. "They were gone in seconds. I just stood there, holding this little thing in one hand, and I started laughing. Then I started crying. Because I realized — I'd built the thing I needed, and maybe thousands of other people needed it too."

He called it the Seese Pro.

Why Something This Small Can Outperform Blowers Three Times Its Price

I'll be honest — when I first held the Seese Pro, I didn't believe it. It felt like holding a TV remote. How could this possibly replace my 23-pound gas blower?

Then I turned it on.

The secret is the brushless motor technology. Traditional blowers use brushed motors — old technology from the 1970s. They lose up to 40% of their energy to heat and friction. That's why they need to be big and heavy to generate enough power.

The Seese Pro's brushless motor converts nearly all its energy into airflow. More power from a smaller, lighter package. It's the same principle that lets a small drone outmaneuver a helicopter.
