ONE IN THREE KNEE REPLACEMENTS MAY NOT BE MEDICALLY NECESSARY

In 2014, a study published in Arthritis & Rheumatology reviewed hundreds of total knee replacements performed on American adults. The researchers concluded that roughly one-third of those surgeries were inappropriate by the medical criteria in use at the time.

A separate 2017 analysis in The BMJ flagged the same pattern across industrialized health systems. Patients whose cartilage loss was real but still manageable were being routed toward the operating room before conservative options had been genuinely exhausted.

Nobody is arguing that knee replacement surgery is wrong. It has given millions of people their mobility back. The argument — and it is getting louder — is about timing, and about who benefits when the timing is pushed forward.

Follow The Reimbursement Money

Under current Medicare coding, a total knee arthroplasty reimburses a hospital somewhere in the range of a brand-new compact car. A consultation about conservative management — bracing, topical relief, physical therapy — reimburses roughly what a diner pays its morning waitstaff.

That gap is not the individual surgeon's fault. Most orthopedic surgeons we spoke with are careful, decent clinicians who went into medicine for the right reasons. But the billing structure they operate inside rewards the operating room, not the waiting room.

Layer in the consulting fees that large orthopedic-device manufacturers pay to high-volume surgeons — figures publicly disclosed through the Sunshine Act — and you have a system in which doing something aggressive pays, and doing something patient doesn't.

THE BILLING CODES ARE NOT NEUTRAL. THEY PUSH IN ONE DIRECTION.

The patient sitting across the desk hears the phrase 'bone on bone' and often does not hear what follows. They sign the consent form. They get a surgery date. They rarely get handed a pamphlet explaining what a six-month delay would actually cost them.

What it would actually cost them, according to several musculoskeletal specialists we interviewed, is often nothing. The joint does not usually collapse overnight. The cartilage loss shown on the MRI was there last year too. A measured delay is not reckless.

Before the scheduled surgery date gets circled on the kitchen calendar, it is worth knowing what the twelve weeks after a total knee replacement actually require. Not the brochure version — the version the home health aides see.

Patients cannot drive for four to six weeks. Stairs become a project. The risk of deep vein thrombosis is real enough that blood thinners are standard. Between ten and twenty percent of patients end up back in the ER with a complication within ninety days.

For a patient living alone — widowed, in a quiet cul-de-sac in The Villages or Sun City or Scottsdale — that recovery is not a small thing. It is a logistical crisis that Medicare only partially covers through a home health benefit most families underestimate.

Transdermal botanical patches are not a reimbursable category inside most orthopedic practices. There is no CPT code for recommending one. The twelve minutes a surgeon has for your follow-up are not structured to include a conversation about a product sold directly to the consumer.
