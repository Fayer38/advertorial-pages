Last summer I flew to see my grand-mère Yvette in a village near Aix-en-Provence, mostly for the bread and the silence.

She kissed both my cheeks, held my face in her hands, and said, "Ma chérie, tu ne retiens pas l'eau — elle stagne."

  • She walked me into her garden in her slippers and pointed to the meadowsweet, tall and feathered along the wall.
  • Beside it, the young birch tree she had planted the year I was born, whose leaves she picked every July.
  • Near the well, a patch of horsetail she treated like a weed but used like medicine, as her own mother once had.
  • Every morning for ten days she brewed them together in a chipped enamel kettle that smelled like childhood summers.
  • By day seven my ankle bones were visible again, and the face in her bathroom mirror finally looked like mine.
