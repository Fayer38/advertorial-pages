The 4 Types of Neuropathy You Were Never Told About

Here's what they don't explain in the ten-minute neurologist visit. Not all peripheral neuropathy is the same — and the distinction matters enormously, because the underlying cause decides whether your nerves are truly gone or simply starved.

  • DIABETIC SENSORY NEUROPATHY The most common form, caused by prolonged blood sugar damage starving the tiny capillaries that feed your nerve fibers in the feet and hands.
  • POST-CHEMOTHERAPY INDUCED NEUROPATHY Triggered by certain chemo drugs that bluntly attack healthy nerve cells alongside cancer cells, often leaving survivors with burning hands, feet, and profound cold sensitivity for years.
  • AGE-RELATED IDIOPATHIC NEUROPATHY The quiet one doctors label idiopathic because they can't pin a cause. It shows up after 60 as tingling, numb toes, and that unsteady gait you dread.
  • B-VITAMIN DEFICIENCY NEUROPATHY Often missed entirely by doctors, this form stems from depleted B1, B6, and B12 levels — vitamins your body stops absorbing properly somewhere between your fifties and sixties.

What Your Neurologist Will Offer You First

Walk into any neurology office in America at 68 and you'll leave with one of three things. A gabapentin prescription. A Lyrica prescription. Or a shrug and a suggestion to try duloxetine. Maybe they'll add a lidocaine patch for the burning. Maybe they'll tell you to take a cheap B-complex from the drugstore. Most will send you home with the same flat phrase I heard thirty thousand times in my career — learn to live with it.

Here's the dirty secret I watched play out on my ward for four decades. Gabapentin doesn't heal nerves — it only dulls the signal the damaged nerves are screaming. The grogginess arrives in week one. The lidocaine patch wears off in eight hours. Every single one of these is a muzzle, not a repair.
