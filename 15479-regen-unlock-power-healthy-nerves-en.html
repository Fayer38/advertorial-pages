My father called me in February. The pins-and-needles in his feet had stopped letting him sleep, and his doctor had prescribed gabapentin.

Dad is 82, a retired electrician in Duluth, and not a man who reaches for prescriptions lightly. He wanted another option first.

  • Diabetic peripheral neuropathy — the nerve damage that develops in roughly half of long-term type 2 diabetics over sixty.
  • Chemotherapy-induced neuropathy, which lingers in the hands and feet long after treatment ends for many cancer survivors.
  • Idiopathic neuropathy, the most frustrating category, where the symptoms are real but no underlying cause is ever found.
  • Post-surgical or compression-related nerve pain from knee replacements, spinal procedures, and the ordinary degeneration that arrives after seventy.

Whatever the origin, the result tends to look the same: burning, tingling, numbness, and a creeping loss of balance.

In the worst cases, patients stop driving at night and start measuring their independence in weeks, not years.

I am a health journalist, not a clinician. But I have access to a lab, a rubric, and a stubborn father.

The Methodology

I ordered all five from their official websites to rule out counterfeits. Each bottle arrived sealed, with the lot number photographed.

A sample from each was sent to an independent lab in Chicago to verify the actual ingredient forms and dosages on the label.

My father and I ran staggered eight-week trials, tracking symptoms each morning on a ten-point scale for burning, tingling, numbness, and sleep.
