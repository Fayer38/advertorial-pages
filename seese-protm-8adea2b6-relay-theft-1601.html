Five Cordless Blowers Tested On My Driveway — One Really Surprised Everyone

How one wife's driveway test settled the cordless blower debate.

By Diane Hollister · April 18, 2026

The blower you see advertised as 'lightweight' in a glossy video is rarely the same blower your sore shoulder will forgive.

Most cordless blowers are marketed as effortless, lightweight tools for anyone. But after weighing five popular models on my own kitchen scale, I learned that the gap between marketing copy and actual usable weight can be staggering.


The quiet reality in neighborhoods across America: millions of homeowners over sixty are quietly retiring from yard work, not because they want to, but because the tools have become too heavy to hold. Shoulder injuries, wrist strain, and the slow surrender of outdoor independence are the real cost of buying the wrong blower — and nobody in the aisle warns you.


My husband Gary had his right shoulder replaced in October, and by mid-November he was already trying to rake leaves one-handed in our driveway. I watched him from the kitchen window, quietly wincing every time the rake caught a root, and I decided right there I'd find him a tool that wouldn't fight him back.


So I did what any former elementary principal with a stopwatch and a kitchen scale would do: I turned our driveway into a controlled test lab and ordered five of the most-recommended cordless blowers available.

A Real Driveway Test, Not a Spec Sheet

Every Saturday morning in Raleigh, Gary used to walk out with his coffee and spend an hour tidying the driveway before breakfast.


After the surgery, I started noticing he'd come back inside earlier than usual, set the coffee down untouched, and quietly massage his shoulder while pretending to read the weather forecast on his phone.


I mentioned it twice and both times he waved me off, insisting the old gas blower from the garage wasn't really that heavy.


That Thanksgiving, with our daughter visiting from Charlotte and our granddaughter Maddie running around the yard, everything felt normal. The leaves were mostly cleared, Gary had taken his anti-inflammatory, and we'd settled into the slow, warm rhythm of a holiday weekend.

The Morning the Scale Told the Truth

The following Monday, I watched Gary lift our old corded blower off the garage shelf with both hands, hear a small involuntary sound come out of him, and set it back down on the workbench. He didn't say anything.


He just turned and walked into the kitchen, poured a glass of water, and stood at the sink with his back to me. That was the moment I knew shopping for 'a lighter one' wasn't going to cut it.


I needed the lightest, most capable cordless blower on the market, and I needed proof — not reviews written by people paid to write reviews. That afternoon I pulled out a yellow legal pad, went online, and ordered five different cordless blowers.


I put them all on the same kitchen scale, one by one, the morning the last box arrived. The numbers on the scale told a story the marketing copy had been carefully hiding.

Here's what most blower buyers don't realize: the weight you carry for two minutes in a hardware store aisle feels completely different from the weight you hold at arm's length for fifteen minutes of actual work. Engineers call this 'static hold fatigue,' and it's why a blower that feels manageable for a quick test can become genuinely painful for anyone with a healing joint, arthritis, or simply the normal shoulder wear that shows up sometime after fifty.

The One Blower That Surprised All Three Of Us

For the driveway test, I invited our granddaughter Maddie up from Charlotte — she's nine — to join Gary as my two testers. My logic was simple: if a blower was light enough for a nine-year-old to control with one hand and gentle enough for a man six weeks out of shoulder surgery, it was the right tool.


Otherwise, it was marketing. I set up a stopwatch, a kitchen scale, and drew a chalk rectangle on the driveway covered in a measured pile of dry oak leaves.


Each model got three timed runs, weighed before and after, and scored on four criteria: pick-up weight, one-handed hover time before fatigue, driveway clearing speed, and what I called 'the grandkid test.' Two of the blowers Maddie couldn't even lift off the garage floor. One tipped Gary's shoulder into a wince within twenty seconds.


But one of them — one — surprised both of them.

That one was the Seese Pro, and it changed everything.

When Gary picked up the Seese Pro for the first time, he actually looked down at it and asked if the battery was even installed. It was.


At just 2.1 pounds with the battery in place, it weighed less than half of what the Worx WG545 and Greenworks 24V had registered on our scale that morning. But weight alone wasn't what surprised us.


What surprised us was the airflow. Somehow, despite being the lightest blower in the test by a significant margin, it cleared the chalk rectangle faster than two of the heavier, more 'powerful-on-paper' competitors.


Maddie lifted it with one hand. Gary held it at full arm's length for over a minute without setting it down.


The Ryobi models were solid, honest tools — I'd still recommend them to a neighbor in their thirties. The Worx had a clever turbine design but felt cumbersome after the first minute.


The Greenworks ran out of battery before finishing the second test run. The Seese Pro finished the entire driveway on a single charge with power to spare, and Gary smiled for the first time all November.
