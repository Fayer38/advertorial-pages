For eleven years I made wallets that landed on specialty boutique shelves under hangtags I would never pay for myself.

Last quarter I finally ran the numbers. The boutiques were keeping the lion's share while I cleared the smallest cut.

  • You walked out of a boutique with a leather wallet feeling slightly sick about the receipt.
  • The synthetic billfold in your back pocket is cracking, but premium tags feel like a logo tax.
  • You can spot full-grain hide from twenty feet, yet you've never owned one yourself.
  • Every wallet on a department store wall costs three times what the maker actually earned.

I am the maker. I cut, skive, edge, and saddle-stitch every piece by hand in a small Southwest workshop.

The worst part wasn't the boutique markup. It was watching customers walk past my work because they assumed it was beyond reach.

So I sent one email and severed every wholesale account in the country. Then I posted the wholesale invoices online.

The Real Math

Here is what nobody at the register tells you. The hangtag is built backwards from the boutique's rent.

A specialty store needs the wallet to retail at a number that covers a SoHo lease, payroll, and visual merchandising.

Walking that math backwards from the shelf, the artisan who actually built the thing ends up with the smallest slice.

For years I accepted that arrangement because it felt like the only way to get my work in front of buyers.

Then I realized the hangtag was teaching customers exactly the wrong thing about what real leather should cost.

Buyers were learning that full-grain American leather demanded a boutique-tier price tag. That number had nothing to do with the hide or my hours.

It had everything to do with somebody else's storefront, and I was the cheap part of an expensive product.

So I made a decision that most artisans never quite manage to make for themselves.

I drafted one email to every wholesale account I had built over a decade and severed the relationship.

Then I drove to the warehouse and brought every unit that was three weeks from those shelves back home.

The challenge was figuring out how to put these wallets in the hands of people who would actually appreciate them.

No catalog. No rep. No SoHo storefront subsidizing my craft with someone else's down payment.

Just the workshop, my email list, and the same wallets that were headed for boutique hangtags.

Luckily, the math gets very simple when you remove every middleman from the chain.
