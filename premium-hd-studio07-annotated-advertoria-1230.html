Raymond Holcomb has been grinding optical glass in a converted apple-packing shed above the Columbia River since 1978. At seventy-one, he still works the bench six days a week, machining prism housings for the binoculars that carry his initials into the hands of American birders, hunters, and pilots.

Next month, the shed closes. His cardiologist gave him the news in August. His wife Eleanor, who packed every order for thirty-four years, died the winter before. 'I'm finishing what's on the racks,' he says, 'and that's the end of it.'

There are 412 binoculars left. Raymond refused the regional wholesaler who wanted to truck the lot to a chain retailer and triple the sticker. Instead, he is selling them directly to readers, at a price that would make any honest European dealer blush.

This is the story of how a flagship Austrian binocular ends up costing a used-truck's worth at your Cabela's counter — and why a widower in Oregon is about to undercut the entire category, one handcrafted pair at a time.

Raymond Didn't Choose Glass — Glass Chose Him

🖼️

He didn't set out to become an optics maker. His father, Harold Holcomb, a Navy optical technician who spent WWII aligning periscope prisms on Pacific submarines, set up a hobby bench in the garage in 1961. Raymond was twelve.

By nineteen he was hand-polishing BaK-4 prisms for his father's mail-order customers. By twenty-eight he had taken over the shop, added his mother's maiden name to the branding, and rented the apple shed above Hood River that still houses the operation.

'My father used to say a binocular should outlast the man who buys it,' Raymond tells me, thumbing a rear eyepiece cap. 'If it doesn't, you've built furniture, not an instrument.'

His customers bear that out. Park rangers at Mount Rainier still carry pairs from the early nineties. Retired commercial pilots order replacements for children and grandchildren. An Audubon chapter in Texas rotates a set of his 8x42s through four generations of officers.

I bought a pair off Ray in 1994 for a trip to Denali. They went through rain, they went through the divorce, they went through my son learning to spot elk. Thirty-one years later the focus wheel is still tight. Tell me one piece of European glass that does that.

Then, three Februarys ago, Eleanor got sick.

The Winter Eleanor Died, the Racks Started Filling Up

🖼️

"Empty wooden office chair in a small workshop office with a pair of reading glas"

Eleanor Holcomb was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer in February 2023. The oncologist at OHSU gave her six months. She lasted four. For thirty-four years she had run the office — answering calls, writing invoices by hand, driving boxes to the USPS station on Oak Street every Thursday afternoon. Without her, the business had no hands but his.

'I stopped answering the phone in July,' Raymond says. 'I couldn't hear a voice that wasn't hers without getting angry. I think I was angry at everyone who called because none of them were her.'

His daughter Margaret drove up from Salem most weekends. She offered to move him into the guest room, hire someone for the shed, handle the website. He wouldn't hear of it. His son-in-law, a pharmacist, quietly canceled his own vacation to come help with estate paperwork. Raymond signed nothing.

Then, on a Tuesday morning in late September, Margaret drove up unannounced and found the apple shed light on at 5:40 a.m. He was sitting at the bench, coffee untouched, collimating a barrel he'd left in the jig the night Eleanor entered hospice.

'The glass doesn't ask how you're doing,' he says. 'It just needs you to pay attention. I could pay attention. That was the only thing I could still do.' He worked through fall and into the first snow. Grinding, aligning, sealing.

For eleven months the phone stayed off. He didn't post to the website. He didn't answer the dealer inquiries. He built two pair a day, sometimes three. No invoices went out. The Thursday USPS run stopped.

By August 2024 the racks in the back room held 487 completed binoculars. Each one was collimated, purged with nitrogen, wrapped in the same ivory-colored linen Eleanor had always used. He'd signed the underside of each barrel with her initials beside his.

The Hand-Figured Prism That No Factory Machine Can Copy

🖼️

To understand why Raymond's 12x42 costs what it costs — and why a European flagship costs what it costs — you have to understand one step: the figuring of the prism. It is the step that every mass-producer automates, because hand-figuring adds four hours of skilled labor to every pair.

The BaK-4 prism inside the binocular is the element that bends light twice and delivers the image to your eye. Factory prisms are ground on a diamond wheel to within a hair's precision — good, but not great. A hand-figured prism is polished wet, under a felt pad, against a cast-iron lap, until the surface is flatter than the curve of still water. You feel the difference immediately at dusk, when most optics start to blur.

'I'm not building these for people who want them to look expensive,' Raymond says. 'I'm building them for the guy at 5 a.m. in November trying to make out a scaup on open water.'

Here is how a single pair gets built.

The magnesium alloy housing is machined in-house on a 1972 Bridgeport mill. Objective lenses — 42mm, fully multi-coated — are seated with a torque wrench at 4.5 Newton-meters, sealed with a rubber O-ring, then purged with dry nitrogen at 20 psi to prevent internal fog. The prisms are figured by hand over two evenings in the workshop, temperature held at 68°F. The focus wheel, which most brands subcontract to China for next to nothing, Raymond turns on a lathe in his own shop.

Total labor per pair: 11 to 13 hours, across four days. Raymond signs each barrel by hand with a stylus — his initials, then Eleanor's, then the serial number.

When a finished pair comes off the bench and I hold it up to the window, I see the exact same Columbia River my father saw through his first prototype in 1959. That's the part no factory can copy. That's the part I can't sell.

'Either You Stop, Raymond, or the Bench Stops You'

On August 14, 2025, Dr. Patel at Providence Hood River Memorial ordered an echocardiogram. The results came back on August 21: moderate aortic stenosis, left ventricular hypertrophy, and an irregular rhythm that spiked whenever Raymond held his arms overhead — exactly the posture required to torque a housing shut.

'Either you stop, Raymond, or the bench stops you,' Dr. Patel told him. 'I'm telling you as your doctor and as someone whose father used your binoculars. If you finish one more run, you're not going to see spring migration.'

Raymond had already known. The shortness of breath when he climbed the shed stairs. The way his left hand locked around the alignment key some mornings. A bottle of nitroglycerin he'd started carrying in his shirt pocket since May, not telling Margaret.

Margaret caught him three times that summer: asleep over the lathe at 2 p.m., hand trembling too hard to thread a retaining ring, and — the one that finally broke her — weeping quietly in the office chair with Eleanor's old reading glasses on the desk in front of him.

'Dad,' she said, 'Mom didn't die so you could die at the bench making glass for strangers.'

It hurt because she was right. Eleanor had packed every one of those orders believing they were going to someone who would use them, not to a widower grinding them into his own chest.

That night, at the kitchen table in the farmhouse above the shed, Raymond made the decision: close the workshop by year's end, and move every pair on the racks out the door — directly to the people his father had always said they were for.

Three weeks after the cardiologist's verdict, a wholesaler from Portland drove up and offered to buy the entire remaining inventory at about a third of what it retailed for at specialty dealers. His plan: box-store the lot through a regional sporting-goods chain, sticker each pair at roughly triple the Portland wholesale number, and clear it by Christmas.

'He wanted me to let my father's name sit on a plastic rack under a fluorescent light next to a stadium seat cushion,' Raymond said. 'These are instruments. They go on necks. They go to the blinds at four in the morning. They don't go on racks.'

Instead, he chose direct. No distributor, no regional rep, no dealer markup. Each pair sold to the birder, the hunter, the retired range officer — at a price that reflects what it actually costs to build, plus Raymond's labor, and nothing else. The wholesaler's entire margin, gone.

When the 412 pairs are gone, they are gone. There is no second production run, no successor model, no licensed brand carrying on. The apple shed lease terminates December 31. The Bridgeport mill is already promised to a community college in Bend.

'This isn't a clearance sale,' Raymond told me, sliding a pair across the bench. 'This is me finishing the job my father started and getting these into the right hands before I stop. If that happens to cost less than what the European luxury houses charge, that's not a discount. That's just what honest glass should have always cost.'
