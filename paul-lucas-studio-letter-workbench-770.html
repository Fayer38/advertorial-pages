Where it starts

A wallet begins with the hide. I open a fresh side around six, before the phone starts.

This morning it's English Bridle. Firm, waxed on both sides, the kind that holds a crease cleanly.

The other hide I reach for often is Glazed Harness, softer in the hand, with a richer depth of color.

Both come from American cowhide, tanned in the States. I know the tanneries. I've met the people.

Most wallets are cut from splits and corrected grain, glued shut, finished to look like leather.

That's why they crack in a year. The top layer peels. The thread unravels. You've seen it.

I don't use splits, corrected grain, or bonded leather. Only full-grain. It's the difference that lasts decades.

People ask me about the six leathers and nine threads like it's a marketing gimmick.

It isn't. It's fifty-four combinations because every wallet someone orders is answering a question.

Jack's question was how to honor his father's seventieth. Chestnut and green was his answer.

Another man last month ordered black Bridle with ivory thread for his son's graduation.

A woman in Vermont picked burgundy with navy for her husband. They'd met sailing, forty years back.

I read every order. I know who it's for. That's not a sales line. It's just how a small shop works.

The customization isn't a feature list. It's a conversation between you and the wallet you'll carry.

Why I can offer a lifetime guarantee without any fine print

The stitching is done by hand with waxed linen thread. Saddle stitch, two needles, locked at every end.

If one thread breaks, the rest hold. You don't get that from a machine chain-stitch at the factory.

I guarantee my stitching and craftsmanship for life because I stand behind what I do at this bench.

If something I made ever fails you, send it back. I'll repair it. I'll do right by you.

That's not a marketing position. It's the same promise my grandfather made to his customers.

What you actually get when you order a Finnigan from my shop

I want to be plain about this because I don't like it when brochures get fancy.

You get a wallet cut, stitched, burnished, and finished by my hands at this bench in the South.

You get leather I picked from hides I opened myself, with thread I chose for strength and color.

You get a lifetime guarantee on the stitching and craftsmanship. No hedging. No asterisk.

I should be honest about what my bench can do in a week, because it matters.

This isn't a factory. Orders are cut and stitched one at a time, in the order they arrive.

I work six days. A finished Finnigan takes the better part of an afternoon to do right.

When the queue gets long, lead times stretch. I'd rather tell you that upfront than rush a wallet.

Right now the queue is manageable, but orders have been coming in steady since the fall.

I don't cut corners to catch up. I'd rather you wait two extra weeks than carry a sloppy wallet.

The leather moves differently by season. Winter hides are firmer. I let them relax before cutting.

You'll get an email when your wallet is cut, another when it's stitched, another when it ships.

I don't farm any of that out. It's me, at this bench, with the hide you chose in my hands.

Some leather colors run out before the next shipment comes in from the tannery.

When Glazed Harness in chestnut goes out of stock, it's usually four to six weeks until the next run.

If your combination is available today, that's the honest window I can promise you right now.

You won't find the Finnigan on Amazon, in department stores, or on a big-box site.

That's a choice, not an oversight. I sell directly from my shop because I want to know who bought it.

When there's a middleman, margins get squeezed and corners get cut. The leather gets thinner.

I'd rather sell fewer wallets at the quality I can stand behind than move volume I can't vouch for.

So if you want one, it comes from here, from my bench, to your door. Nothing in between.
