A Nordstrom Buyer Offered Sixty-Five Dollars Each. I Said No.

After forty-one years at the bench, my hands are giving out — the last four hundred wallets go direct from me to you.

By Paul Lucas Published April 2026 · April 14, 2026
Excellent ★★★★★ 2,847 reviews
I'd rather sell them direct than see my name become a markup.

My name is Paul Lucas. I'm sixty-seven years old, and I make wallets by hand in a 900-square-foot workshop in Weatherford, Texas. My grandfather bought this building in 1948 and ran a saddle-repair shop out of it until he passed it to my father. I've been at the bench here since 1984.

I'm writing this myself because three years ago my hands started locking up in the mornings. The doctor called it rheumatoid arthritis. It's the same thing that took my father off the bench at seventy. I have maybe two good years of stitching left in me, if I'm honest.

So I've decided to make four hundred more wallets. When those are gone, the shop goes quiet. This letter is about why I'm selling them the way I am — direct to the man who'll actually carry one — instead of the way a buyer from Nordstrom wanted me to.

The Buyer In The Rental SUV

Last spring a woman drove out from Dallas in a rented Escalade. She was pleasant. She'd done her homework. She sat in my workshop for forty minutes and told me she loved the story — the 1948 building, the full-grain American cowhide, the hand-stitching.

Then she made her offer. Sixty-five dollars a wallet, two thousand units, delivered to their distribution center in Ontario, California. They were going to retail them at two hundred twenty-nine. A little hangtag would read handcrafted in Texas.

I did the math in my head while she was still talking. The markup would be two hundred fifty percent. My name on the leather, my stitches in the thread, and a stranger in Ontario collecting the difference for doing nothing but moving a box.

When the count hits zero, it's zero. There isn't a next batch.

I walked her to her rental. I was polite. My wife Linda was in the kitchen when I came back inside, and I told her I wasn't doing it. She said I know you aren't. She'd already put the kettle on.

I don't begrudge anyone a living. But I'm sixty-seven and my hands are running out. If these are the last four hundred wallets from this shop, I want each one going to a man who'll carry it until it's soft as a work glove, not to a display case.

What My Father Taught Me

My father, Finnigan, started this shop in 1952 after he came home from Korea. He cut his first wallet from a scrap of saddle leather he'd bought off a retired cowboy in Mineral Wells. That wallet lasted thirty-one years before the owner had it rebuilt, not replaced.

Dad used to say a wallet is the one thing a man touches every day of his adult life. He thought that meant it deserved real materials and real stitching, not glue and a foil stamp. I was eleven when he handed me my first awl. I've never made a living any other way.

When his hands gave out in 1989, he sat me down at this same bench and told me two things. Don't ever let the leather tell a lie. And don't let anybody else put a price on what your hands do. I've tried to keep both promises for thirty-seven years.

The Wallet I Make Myself

It's called the Finnigan. Three card slots on each side, a long bill pocket, no coin pouch because American men don't carry coins. The leather is full-grain American cowhide from a tannery in Johnstown, Pennsylvania, the same one my father used after 1967.

I hand-cut every piece on a granite slab, bevel the edges with a tool I bought in 1991, and saddle-stitch the whole thing with waxed thread. Two needles, one seam, no machine. A machine stitch can unravel from one broken thread. A saddle stitch can't.

You choose the leather from six colors and the thread from nine — fifty-four possible combinations. I stamp your initials on the inside if you want them. I sign the inside of every one, because my father signed his and I promised him I would too.

Why I'm Going Direct

The Nordstrom buyer wasn't the first. Over the years I've had calls from department stores, online marketplaces, two subscription boxes, and a men's magazine that wanted a private-label run. I've said no to all of them for the same reason.

Every middleman in that chain needs a cut, and every cut comes out of either the leather, the stitching, or the price the man in the back pocket pays. I won't thin the leather so a warehouse in Ontario can keep its margin.

Selling direct means the man who buys this wallet is paying for the hide, the thread, and my forty-one years at the bench. Nothing else. No hangtag tax. No retail rent. No buyer in a rental SUV taking her share on the drive back to Dallas.

The Men Who Still Write Me

I have a wooden box in the corner of the shop with customer letters going back to 1986. One is from a dentist in Bozeman, Montana named Greg Thornton. He bought a wallet from my father in 1978 and one from me in 2004. Both still work.

Another letter came in 2019 from William Whitmore, a retired teacher in Tulsa. He passed away last year; his widow Clarice sent me a note telling me his Paul Lucas wallet had outlived two cars and one marriage before him. He included a photo. The leather looked like a catcher's mitt in the best possible way.

I don't keep those letters to brag. I keep them because on the bad mornings — when my knuckles don't bend right and I can't thread a needle before ten — those letters are the reason I'm still making the next one.

The Final Four Hundred

I'm not running a sale. There is no coupon code, no holiday markdown, no limited-time offer. The price I'm asking is what the wallet costs to build plus a fair day's wage for the time my hands have left. That's the whole formula.

Right now, as I'm writing this in the middle of April 2026, there are three hundred forty-seven wallets of the four hundred left unclaimed. When the count reaches zero, this letter comes down and the workshop door gets a different sign.

  • At Retail A wallet like this, with my name on a hangtag, would sit under a glass counter with a two-hundred-fifty-percent markup paid to the store.
  • Through A Wholesaler Sixty-five dollars to me, two hundred twenty-nine to the customer, and a long chain of middlemen collecting the spread for moving boxes.
  • Direct From The Bench You pay the leather, the thread, and the hands. I pay the rent on the shop. Nobody else takes a cut on the way.

I'm not trying to be dramatic about it. I'm just being plain. I'm sixty-seven, my hands are what they are, and I'd rather spend the time I have left at this bench building for men who'll carry the thing than negotiating with buyers who want my name on their hangtag.

Linda thinks I should have retired two years ago and taken her to see our daughter in Santa Fe more often. She's probably right. But I promised my father I'd finish on the bench, not in a rocking chair, and four hundred more feels like the right number to finish on.

Questions Men Have Asked Me

Can you really still make a wallet with arthritic hands, and will the quality suffer on the later ones?

The mornings are slow, but the stitches are the same. When my hands can't hold the standard, I'll stop early. I won't ship a wallet I wouldn't carry.

What happens if the stitching fails years from now, after the workshop is closed for good?

My lifetime guarantee on the stitching stands. Linda and my nephew Tomás will handle repairs from the back room for as long as they're able.

Why not just train an apprentice and keep the workshop going after you retire from the bench?

I tried twice. Neither stayed past a year. This work pays less than a union welder's wage and asks more patience than most men have today.

One Last Thing

I wrote this letter over three mornings because some days my hands won't let me type more than a page. My daughter Emily fixed the commas. Linda read it twice and told me it sounded like me, which is the only editor's note I trust.

If you build a Finnigan, it will show up in a plain cardboard box with a handwritten card. No branded tissue, no magnetic closure, no unboxing moment. Just the wallet, the card, and a repair note in case you ever need Linda's address.

If you decide it's not for you, I understand completely. A wallet is a small thing in a man's life. But the ones I'm building now are the last ones I'll build, and I'd rather you know that up front than find out later.
