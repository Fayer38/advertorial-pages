I Thought the Box Was Empty — Until I Started My Yard Again

After my back fusion, I could not lift a gas blower anymore.

By Robert Mallon, retired HVAC installer, Loveland

The surgeon looked me straight in the eye at Christ Hospital and laid down the rules. No more gas blower. No twisting. Nothing over eight pounds. I nodded like a man who still had his pride, and then I walked out to our driveway in Loveland.

By October the oak leaves had covered the drive. Patty was calling lawn services that charged more each visit, and I stood at the garage door every Saturday staring at my old gas blower like it sat on the other side of a canyon I could not cross.

I'm Robert Mallon. I turned sixty-seven last March. I spent forty-one years crawling through attics and basements installing HVAC systems, and I always took care of my own property. Hiring someone to blow my own leaves felt like handing a stranger my keys and my dignity.

Then the Saturday before Thanksgiving, Patty handed me a small box from an online order. I lifted the box, frowned, and told her it had to be empty. She laughed and told me to open it. Inside sat the tool that would give me my fall back.

What the Surgeon Said and What the Fall Took

The fusion was at L4-L5. I came out of surgery in February twenty twenty-four, and for weeks I shuffled down our hallway in Loveland holding onto the walls. The doctor said I would get stronger, but he would not lie about the limits.

No lifting over eight pounds. No twisting motion. No yanking on a pull cord. My old gas blower alone weighed eleven pounds dry, and after I filled it with gas it sat closer to fourteen. That machine was finished for me, and so was half the work I used to do.

Patty never complained, but I saw her looking out the kitchen window at the leaves collecting along the fence. She had started writing lists of lawn companies, and the numbers she was quoting made me wince. The work used to be free when I did it. Now it had a bill attached.

I had spent my working life climbing ladders and carrying copper line sets. I had shown up at five in the morning for forty-one winters. And now I could not pick up a two-gallon can of driveway salt without my back sending a warning through my hip down to my knee.

By the end of October I was avoiding the garage altogether. Patty would ask if I wanted to go look at the yard, and I would invent some project inside the house. A man with a fused spine does not want to stand next to the equipment he can no longer use.

The Box I Thought Was Empty

Patty had been doing her own research. She watches those home reviews on her tablet at night, and she had ordered a blower without telling me because she knew I would have said no. The Saturday before Thanksgiving the package showed up on the porch.

She set the box on the kitchen counter and pushed it toward me. It was maybe the size of a cereal box. I picked it up with one hand, felt nothing, and told her somebody had shipped her an empty carton. Patty grinned and told me to cut the tape.

Inside was a slim handheld machine with a battery on the side, a nozzle on the front, and a trigger on the grip. I lifted it with two fingers. Two point one pounds. It felt like a hair dryer that belonged in her bathroom drawer, not a yard tool.

It was called Seese Pro. Patty had read reviews from men my age with back surgeries and rotator cuffs and hip replacements. She had not told me because she did not want me to talk myself out of it before I tried it. She had a point.

I carried it out to the driveway with my coffee still in the other hand. I pushed the battery into the slot, squeezed the trigger once, and the thing jumped to life. No pull cord. No choke. No cloud of exhaust. Just a quiet, steady rush of air.

I aimed it at a pile of oak leaves near the front steps and they moved like a swarm. I did not believe it for a second. I turned the nozzle toward a patch of acorns, and they rolled across the concrete like marbles. One hand. No back strain. Nothing.

How a Two-Pound Tool Changes a Bad Day

Here is what I did not expect. The power was real. This is not a toy that moves a few leaves around. The air comes out fast and focused, and when I aimed it at wet leaves packed against the curb, they broke loose and rolled where I pointed them.

I worked the front walk in ten minutes. Then the driveway. Then the porch. I kept stopping because I expected my lower back to speak up, and it was quiet. The machine was doing the work the way my arms used to. I did not have to brace or twist.

The battery lasted longer than I did. I ran the blower for close to forty minutes before I stopped for a water break, and the indicator was still showing plenty of charge. By the time I came back out, I had forgotten I was supposed to be careful.

Patty brought me a sandwich at lunch and stood on the porch watching me move acorns around the dogwood tree. She did not say anything for a minute. Then she said I looked like myself again. That was the first time she had used that word since the surgery.

I want to be clear. This is not a miracle. My back is still fused. I still get stiff if I sit too long. But the difference between standing at the garage door feeling useless and finishing the driveway before coffee gets cold — that difference is everything.

Why the Old Gas Blower Nearly Put Me in a Chair

Before the fusion, I fought that gas blower every fall. The pull cord alone was a three-jerk operation on a cold morning. You plant your feet, twist your core, and yank. For a healthy thirty-year-old, that is nothing. For a man with lumbar damage, that is how backs break.

Then there is the weight. A full gas blower sits around thirteen or fourteen pounds with fuel. Carry that on your right side for thirty minutes while leaning forward, and every disc in your lower spine pays the bill. My surgeon did not forbid yard work. He forbade that machine.

The engine noise wore me down too. Those two-stroke engines shake your arms and ring in your ears for an hour after you shut them off. My neighbor in Loveland uses one, and his wife says he cannot hear the television anymore on Saturday nights. That is not retirement.

And the fumes. You are breathing hot exhaust the entire time you work. After forty years in crawl spaces breathing things I should not have been breathing, my lungs have paid enough. I do not need a fall chore that leaves me coughing all the way through dinner.

The cord blowers were not an option either. Our lot is long and wraps behind the garage. You drag a hundred-foot extension cord, it gets caught on a root, you pull it, and your shoulder goes. I tore a rotator cuff in ninety-eight. I have no interest in finding the other one.

So what was left? Nothing I could afford to buy and then hate. This is why the Seese Pro mattered. It solves all four problems at once — no pull cord, no fumes, no heavy weight, no cord to snag. It was made for somebody in my exact shape.

Six Weeks of Doing My Own Yard Again

By the second weekend I had my rhythm back. I would take the blower out right after breakfast while the air was still cold, work for thirty or forty minutes, come in for coffee, and go back out. I was getting more done than I did before the surgery.

Patty's flowerbeds run along the back of the house. She had been worried about the dead pachysandra and the mulch that the squirrels had kicked all over the patio. I cleared the whole bed in fifteen minutes holding the blower in my right hand, resting my left on my hip.

The snow came the second week of December. I was a little nervous, but I took the blower out on the back porch and pushed the light dusting right off into the yard. It moved the dry snow as easily as it moved dry leaves. My porch was clear in three minutes.

Come spring I used it on the driveway to move the fine dust and gravel that the salt trucks had left. Later I used it on grass clippings after Patty ran the push mower. It earned its keep in every season, not just the fall. That surprised me the most.

Six weeks in, I sat on the back porch with Patty and realized I had not called a lawn service all fall. The yard looked better than it had in years. And my back felt fine. I had not needed ibuprofen after a single session. That had never been true before.
