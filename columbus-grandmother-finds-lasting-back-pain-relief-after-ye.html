HE RAN TOWARD ME AND I COULDN'T EVEN PICK HIM UP

When Nicky turned four, he did what any little boy does when he sees his grandmother. He sprinted across the yard with his arms wide open, ready for that big swooping hug Linda Moretti had given him since the day he was born.

But Linda couldn't bend down. She couldn't lift him. She stood there, frozen, and quietly asked her daughter to pick him up instead. That moment at her grandson's birthday party broke something inside her that three years of back pain hadn't managed to break.

Linda is sixty-three. She taught fourth grade in Columbus, Ohio for thirty-one years. She raised two kids. She hosted every Sunday dinner. And degenerative disc disease was stealing all of it, one small joy at a time.

It started with the garden. Linda had built raised beds in her backyard fifteen years ago, growing tomatoes and zinnias every summer. By the second year of worsening pain, the beds were full of weeds. She couldn't kneel. She couldn't bend over a pot. She couldn't even stand long enough to water.

Then the Sunday dinners stopped. For decades, Linda's kitchen was the gathering place. Pot roast, her mother's lasagna recipe, the good dishes. But standing at the stove for an hour had become unbearable. She told her family she was just taking a break. She didn't tell them she cried about it afterward.

The stairs in her own home became an obstacle course. She started sleeping in the guest room downstairs because climbing to her bedroom left her in agony. Her morning routine now included twenty minutes of gentle stretching just to stand up straight.

I SPENT THOUSANDS ON TREATMENTS THAT ONLY HELPED FOR A DAY OR TWO

Friends stopped inviting her to walk the neighborhood. She declined so many times they quit asking. Her world had become the couch, the heating pad, and the television. At sixty-three, she felt like she was living the life of someone decades older.

The worst part wasn't the pain itself. It was watching her own independence disappear. Every week, another thing she used to do without thinking became something she needed help with. That terrified her more than any diagnosis.

Linda tried everything her insurance would cover and plenty it wouldn't. Chiropractor visits twice a week for over a year. Physical therapy sessions that helped for a day, sometimes two, before the pain settled right back in. She kept a notebook tracking her appointments and how she felt afterward. The pattern was heartbreaking.

She bought two different TENS units after seeing them advertised. They buzzed on the surface of her skin and maybe distracted her from the pain for an hour. But the moment she turned them off, everything came back. They were masking pain, not addressing anything underneath it.

Over two years, the bills stacked up. Copays, out-of-network specialists, devices that gathered dust in a drawer. Linda started researching surgical options, not because she wanted surgery, but because she had run out of ideas. Her second orthopedist mentioned the possibility of a procedure. She went home and cried.
